Introduction

This thesis examines organic memristive devices built from ion-containing polymer composites and explores how their internal dynamics can serve as a computational resource. The work lies at the intersection of three fields: solid-state electronics, polymer physical chemistry, and computational neuroscience. This chapter connects those fields in the order in which they become necessary. It begins with the limits of contemporary computing, then turns to the biological synapse as an engineering model, introduces the memristor as the relevant device concept, and finally develops the polymer-composite strategy used in the experimental chapters.

The Crisis in Modern Computing

Modern digital computers still depend on an architecture that has proven highly successful, but its limitations are now increasingly apparent. Simply making transistors smaller and increasing clock speeds no longer fully addresses the issues. This section first examines the stored-program architecture and the three primary constraints that now define its practical limits: the memory wall, the energy wall, and the slowing of classical transistor scaling. It then discusses two ways to overcome these constraints: integrating computation and storage within the same physical substrate, and adopting event-driven dynamics instead of traditional clock-based synchronous arithmetic.

Von Neumann Architecture and Its Limitations

The architectural model of modern digital computers was proposed by von Neumann in 1945's “First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC”. Its main idea is the stored-program concept, in which instructions and data are stored in the same memory and fetched one word at a time by a central processing unit. This combination of code and data led to the creation of general-purpose computers, enabling single machines to execute diverse tasks by loading different binary instruction sequences. This architecture, which includes a CPU, a main memory containing code and data, and a connecting bus, became the starting point for digital systems. Various engineering optimisations have refined this model (from registers and caches to branch prediction and memory controllers), but its overall topology has remained basically unchanged.

The strength of this model also creates its bottleneck, given that the processor and memory are physically distinct subsystems. They communicate through a channel whose bandwidth sets a practical limit on performance. Every instruction fetch, operand load, and result write-back requires data to move across that channel. In a modern processor, the channel is not a single wire but a hierarchy of registers, caches, controllers, and DRAM, built to bridge units that operate on very different timescales. Still, the main issue remains: the transistors that perform the arithmetic and the cells that hold the state are separated. No arithmetic can proceed before the operands arrive, and no result can be preserved until it is moved back. This is the processor–memory dichotomy.

In the single-port architecture, only one word can be read or written per clock cycle, a limitation known as the von Neumann bottleneck. Many architectural decisions have helped in reducing its effects, for instance, by implementing solutions such as pipelined fetch, multi-level caches, speculative execution, prefetching, out-of-order issue, banked memory systems, and coprocessors like vector units and GPUs. However, all these methods maintain the separation between computation and storage. As a result, the bottleneck is shifted but not removed, and it reappears at the slowest interface in each generation.

Wulf and McKee gave this imbalance a quantitative form in 1995 and named it the memory wall. At that time, processor performance was improving at about 60% per year, while DRAM latency and bandwidth improved at a tenth of that pace. If both trends are extrapolated at this point, the resulting machine will show an average cycle dominated by memory access. The following decades confirmed this prediction, and nowadays microprocessors devote between 30% and 60% of their silicon area and an even larger fraction of their power budget to caches, prefetch logic, memory controllers, and interconnect. Much of the chip is spent keeping the arithmetic units supplied with data. This imbalance is particularly problematic for workloads with irregular access patterns or working sets larger than the on-chip cache. The bandwidth and energy aspects of this imbalance are summarised in the figure.

In 2014, Horowitz provided a comparison of costs for elementary operations in a representative CMOS process where an addition of 32-bit integers costs on the order of 0.1 pJ, but an SRAM access can cost tens of picojoules, while a DRAM access could cost several hundred picojoules to nanojoules. In other words, when workloads are dominated by data movement, the energy budget will be spent more on operand transport and less on arithmetic. This is the energy wall, which comes from the parasitic capacitance of interconnects and from the fact that the total wire length of a memory subsystem is much larger than the wire length inside an arithmetic unit. Given this, if computation and storage remain spatially separated, the architecture will be limited by the physics of moving charge through wires.

For several decades, these architectural inefficiencies were rendered practically invisible by silicon scaling. Moore had already observed that transistor counts double at regular intervals, but this was paired with Dennard scaling: if every linear MOSFET dimension and the supply voltage are scaled by 1/k, the transistor switches k times faster, consumes 1/k^2 of the energy per switch, and occupies 1/k^2 of the area. This meant that if both trends continued, each technology generation would deliver more transistors that were also faster and cooler. The memory and energy walls were present, but they did not yet hinder progress.

Dennard scaling stopped occurring at around 2005, when gate-oxide thinning produced leakage currents that no longer allowed proportional voltage scaling. Transistor counts continued to increase, but the performance per transistor and the energy gains have slowed, while power density has become the main constraint. This is the post-Dennard or dark-silicon regime. Under these conditions, a new process node can no longer hide the inefficiencies of the von Neumann model. They have to be addressed at the architectural and materials level. Meanwhile, workloads have been shifting increasingly toward machine-learning kernels, which are dominated by repeated matrix operations on parameters stored in memory. This shift worsens the data-movement bottleneck right when silicon scaling stops compensating for it. Hennessy and Patterson describe this moment as an inflection point, and argue that the next stage of computer architecture will rely heavily on domain-specific hardware in which memory and computation are brought much closer together.

Two responses to this separation have emerged: The first one doesn't dramatically deviate from traditional computing and aims to perform operations directly within (or very close) the memory array. This route is described as in-memory, near-memory, or processing-in-memory computing. The second response goes further, using temporally structured event streams and treating the intrinsic dynamics of the physical device as part of the computation itself. This route is known as event-driven or temporal computing. The two responses, set against the von Neumann baseline they address, are summarised in the figure.

In-memory computing bypasses the classical fetch–compute–write-back cycle by executing operations directly inside the memory array. This is demonstrated by implementing analogue matrix–vector multiplication using a resistive crossbar architecture. In this setup, each memory cell contains a conductance value that reflects a matrix entry. Then, voltages are applied across the rows, representing the input vector. Finally, the resulting column currents represent the sum of products, in accordance with Kirchhoff's laws. This changes the array from a passive storage device into an active computing tool. Demonstrations using metal-oxide resistive memories, phase-change materials, and organic ionic devices have achieved matrix–vector operations with energy efficiencies that surpass those of traditional digital accelerators, and similar principles can be extended to convolutional and attention-like processes (the matrix-multiplication kernels at the core of transformer architectures).

At the device level, however, this version of in-memory computing still requires memory properties. Each cell should hold a quasi-static conductance that encodes a trained weight, and which should be set once, read many times, and remain stable enough to meet the application's numerical precision requirements. Non-volatility over seconds to years becomes a design goal.

Event-driven computing is motivated by the fact that many signals of interest carry information in time. Natural sounds, visual transients, motor commands, biomedical waveforms, and streaming sensor outputs are all non-static examples. For such signals, the computational model should be a system that receives events in continuous time and evolves in response, because information is not only present in the amplitude values but also in the waveform's evolution and timing. At the algorithmic level, this is the domain of spiking neural networks. At the circuit level, it corresponds to event-driven asynchronous logic. At the device level, which is the level of this thesis, it requires materials whose intrinsic relaxation dynamics are matched to the signals they process.

This framework mirrors biological nervous systems, which efficiently handle complex sensorimotor and cognitive tasks while consuming much less power than artificial systems. This efficiency partly results from architectural choices that diverge significantly from the von Neumann model. Neurons share information through sparse events, and synapses pass signals that change depending on recent activity. Information is stored and altered through slow, gradual electrochemical processes, in which the ionic medium actively participates in computation. Although directly translating the brain into silicon isn't practical or useful, several principles do carry over: sparse-event coding, the use of slow device physics as a fundamental computing element, and viewing device heterogeneity as a feature for functional diversity rather than a flaw. These principles form the basis of neuromorphic engineering.

Boyd and Chua showed that any time-invariant nonlinear operator with fading memory, in an appropriate function space, can be approximated by a finite Volterra series (a power-series expansion in time-shifted inputs). This means that a system whose current state retains a decaying record of past inputs can perform various temporal computations when paired with a trainable linear readout. This concept was later formalised through algorithms such as Jaeger's echo state network and Maass, Natschläger, and Markram's liquid state machine, which were combined under the umbrella of reservoir computing. In this framework, an untrained dynamical system—the reservoir—is driven by the input stream, with only the readout being trained. When the reservoir is realised in a physical substrate, the approach is known as physical reservoir computing, with implementations in optical, mechanical, spintronic, volatile-memristive, and organic electrochemical systems.

This redefines how a working device should be evaluated. If the goal is to maintain a trained weight permanently, a device that can't retain memory for long periods will be considered unsuitable. However, in a reservoir architecture, this finite memory retention is what defines the period during which previous inputs influence the current output, a concept referred to as the fading-memory timescale. Variability between devices can also play a different role: while it's a disadvantage for weight storage, a controlled variation in intrinsic timescales can enable a common readout to interpret temporal structures over a wider range than any individual device could achieve.

The Biological Synapse as the Engineering Template

In neural circuits, a single physical element both transmits a signal and stores part of the history that shapes future transmissions: the synapse. The same molecular event that relays information from one neuron to the next also alters its future strength, so signalling and memory share a substrate. What follows is therefore restricted to synaptic signalling and plasticity, the processes that define the target functions for artificial synaptic devices.

Neural Signal Transmission: A Brief Overview

A neuron is a polarised cell specialised to receive, integrate, and transmit electrical signals. Dendrites (the branching extensions of a neuron) collect inputs from other cells. The soma (the neuron cell body) then integrates those inputs and, once the membrane potential crosses a certain threshold, the axon hillock (the cone-shaped region of the soma) fires an outgoing signal. The axon then propagates that signal over distances that range from millimetres to metres, and its terminals release a chemical messenger onto downstream cells. A mature animal neuron typically receives signals from 10^3–10^4 other neurons through their dendritic contacts. Each such contact is a synapse. The way a neuron computes information is then influenced heavily by the number and strength of its synaptic contacts, and by how they change over time, more than by the soma itself.

The axon's signal is the action potential: a stereotyped depolarisation lasting a few milliseconds caused by the opening of voltage-gated Na+ channels, followed by K+-mediated repolarisation. Action potentials are all-or-nothing events that carry little information about stimulus strength in their waveform. Instead, information is encoded in the timing, rate, and coincidence of spike trains. In this sense, neural computation is event-driven. Between spikes, a neuron only consumes leakage current. During a spike, it expends a small, relatively constant amount of energy. Computation then arises from the patterns of signals arriving at many synapses. The neuron's anatomy and the action potential waveform are summarised in the figure.

Synaptic transmission is mostly a chemical process. When an action potential reaches an axon terminal, it causes depolarisation, opening voltage-gated Ca^2+ channels. The incoming Ca^2+ then triggers vesicles filled with neurotransmitters like glutamate in excitatory cortical synapses to fuse with the presynaptic membrane. The neurotransmitter then diffuses across the 20 nm synaptic cleft and binds to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. These receptors can open ion channels directly or through second-messenger cascades, leading to an ionic current, either an excitatory or inhibitory postsynaptic current (EPSC or IPSC), that briefly modifies the postsynaptic membrane potential, and whose amplitude and kinetics define the strength of the synapse at that moment. These same molecular mechanisms that carry the signal can also influence the strength of future transmissions.

The synapse is a transmission element, a modulator, and a memory element at the same time: it transmits a signal, adjusts the amplitude and time course of that signal, and stores everlasting changes produced by previous activity. This co-localisation of transmission, modulation, and memory makes the synapse, more than the neuron as a whole, the most useful biological template for post-von-Neumann electronic devices. Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning

One important concept in neuroscience is that experience leaves a physical trace in the nervous system, and that this trace is stored mainly at synapses. Hebb spearheaded this idea in 1949, suggesting that if a presynaptic cell repeatedly helps a postsynaptic cell fire, the connection between them should be strengthened, resulting in the well-known summary, “cells that fire together, wire together”. This shows two key aspects of the rule: It is local, as it relies on the two cells linked by the synapse, and it is sensitive to correlation, since joint activity has greater significance than independent activity. Hebb's postulate was not tested directly for more than two decades, but it now organises much of the language of synaptic learning. In this sense, the synaptic plasticity mechanisms to be described next can be interpreted as Hebbian, anti-Hebbian, or Hebbian-like rules functioning on different timescales.

Bliss and Lømo were the first to experimentally demonstrate that brief, high-frequency stimulation in a rabbit's hippocampus increases the postsynaptic response, with these effects lasting for hours. Subsequent research revealed that this enhancement persisted for days to weeks. This led to the term long-term potentiation (LTP), which is now the main example of persistent, activity-dependent synaptic change. In the hippocampus, it involves two key postsynaptic glutamate receptors: AMPA receptors, which mediate the fast EPSC after glutamate release; and NMDA receptors, which open only when the postsynaptic membrane is already depolarised due to a voltage-dependent Mg^2+ block, so they act as Ca^2+-permeable coincidence detectors for simultaneous pre- and postsynaptic activity.

The Ca^2+ influx through NMDA receptors triggers signalling pathways that insert additional AMPA receptors into the postsynaptic membrane. Later presynaptic spikes then produce a larger EPSC. The complementary process, long-term depression (LTD), produces a persistent decrease in synaptic strength under low-frequency or anti-correlated activity, principally through the reverse trafficking of AMPA receptors. Together, LTP and LTD provide the bidirectional, long-lasting modification of synaptic amplitude that is considered a primary physiological basis for learning.

These long-lived changes coexist with faster and reversible modulations known as short-term plasticity. After a presynaptic action potential, the immediate strength of the synapse depends on its recent activity. Short-term facilitation is a transient increase in EPSC amplitude that occurs when there is a second spike that follows the first after a short delay, which is usually attributed to residual presynaptic Ca^2+. Short-term depression is a transient decrease caused by vesicle depletion or receptor desensitisation after a recent release. The time constants range from tens of milliseconds to several seconds, and different synapses display different combinations of facilitation and depression.

This way, short-term plasticity acts as a temporal filter at a single synapse, causing identical presynaptic spike sequences to generate varying postsynaptic responses based on the timing between spikes. Essentially, the synapse operates as a nonlinear, history-dependent filter that relies on transient state changes, which reset after some time.

The temporal character of synaptic learning is further exemplified through spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), a biological process described in cortical pyramidal cells by Markram and co-workers and characterised quantitatively in hippocampal cultures by Bi and Poo. In this rule, the sign and magnitude of the change depend on the delay between pre- and postsynaptic spikes, usually within a window of a few tens of milliseconds. If the presynaptic spike precedes the postsynaptic spike, the synapse is typically potentiated. If, on the contrary, the order is reversed, then the synapse is depressed. Finally, if the delay is too long in either direction, no lasting change is produced. As such, the synapse acts as a detector that retains a short memory of recent presynaptic activity, compares it with postsynaptic firing, and converts this temporal relationship into a longer-lasting change in amplitude.

The synapse described by these results is not just a passive storage of “weight”. Instead, it operates across multiple timescales at once. At the millisecond level, it conveys individual spikes. Over tens to thousands of milliseconds, short-term facilitation and depression adjust its response based on recent activity. Over minutes, hours, and longer periods, LTP and LTD alter the baseline amplitude. These layers exist together at chemical synapses and are driven by partially overlapping yet distinct molecular mechanisms. Abbott and Regehr argued that, from this perspective, a synapse is better understood as a computational unit rather than just a memory store. For hardware, both long-lasting and decaying state changes are important.

Why Hardware Emulation of the Synapse?

The motivation for hardware synapses is directly derived from the The Crisis in Modern Computing section. In traditional computing, a software neural network performs calculations on data retrieved from a memory hierarchy physically separate from the arithmetic units. Each forward pass incurs data movement and energy costs as described earlier, and these costs increase with the number of simulated synapses. A typical human brain contains approximately 10^14–10^15 synapses and consumes about 20 W. A standard von Neumann architecture that consumes orders of magnitude more energy cannot easily bridge this gap through improved design alone, since the cost of fetching a weight before multiplying it by an activation is limited by interconnect physics. Thus, in nature, the synapse exemplifies the most compact, low-power form of co-localised analogue memory and computation, making it an optimal physical template. The order-of-magnitude envelopes of these implementation regimes are compared in the figure.

The research programme built around this idea is neuromorphic engineering. Its programmatic origin is usually attributed to Mead. The goal is not only to accelerate neural algorithms but also to create circuits in which device physics, topology, and dynamics inherently represent computational characteristics akin to those found in the nervous system. In this regard, the synapse has emerged as the main device focus. Over the last twenty years, research has connected device physics, materials science, and circuit design in the quest for a physical “artificial synapse”.

A hardware synapse should provide an analogue, multi-state conductance that can be set reversibly by electrical stimuli and read non-destructively with a small probe current. It should also be compact enough for large arrays, so that many thousands of elements can be addressed with a manageable number of input and output lines. Its response should be nonlinear in a meaningful way, for example by displaying a pulse-number or pulse-amplitude threshold below which little change is induced. Finally, its dynamics should match the signals it is meant to process, whose relevant timescales span from milliseconds to seconds and beyond.

Among these requirements, the distinction between binary and analogue conductance is especially important. A binary synapse stores one bit per element. Representing graded synaptic weights with binary elements would then require many devices per connection. On the other hand, an analogue synapse can encode several distinguishable states in a single physical element through its conductance range. For the ionic devices considered in this thesis, the write energy can also be low because the state is changed by moving ions over short distances, instead of having to charge and discharge large digital circuits.

Reversibility matters just as much. A device whose conductance can be potentiated but not reliably depressed cannot follow the bidirectional rules outlined in the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section. While it may function as a programmable weight in a feed-forward classifier, it cannot fully replicate synaptic learning behaviour. The core specification involves analogue, reversible, multi-state, low-energy changes in conductance controlled by electrical pulses. This is the specification that motivates the devices studied in the rest of the thesis.

The biology of the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section reveals that biological synapses incorporate quasi-stable memory via LTP and LTD, while providing temporal filtering through short-term plasticity and STDP, allowing both functions to coexist within the same physical structure. A hardware system can then be useful in two complementary ways: as a persistent or quasi-persistent programmable weight (as seen in crossbar arrays for in-memory computing), and as a dynamic fading-memory element. The second role is the primary focus of this thesis, following the reservoir-computing approach to event-driven temporal computing introduced in the section.

This leads to a materials question. In biology, the timescales of short-term plasticity are determined by different processes such as Ca^2+ buffering, vesicle recycling, receptor desensitisation, and other local mechanisms that vary across synapses. A hardware alternative for this role should offer an analogous way to adjust its electrical timescale. Classical transistor-based memories do not provide that kind of analogue, history-dependent element. The next section asks whether memristive devices do.

The Memristor: Theory and History

A memristor or memristive device shows a current–voltage (i–v) response that depends on an internal state, which itself is influenced by the device's previous electrical activity. The ideal form of this is the memristor, with the broader experimental category being the wider family of memristive devices. This section starts by explaining the theory, then examines the first real-world physical implementation of a memristive device, explores the main mechanistic types, and finally considers the typical device parameters from two perspectives: trained-weight storage and volatile temporal computing. It is important for the rest of the thesis that the formal definition includes both quasi-stable and relaxing internal states.

Theoretical Foundation

Classical linear circuit theory describes a two-terminal passive element by the relation it imposes between two of four fundamental variables: voltage v, current i, charge q = i dt, and flux linkage = v dt. The familiar elements occupy three of the non-trivial pairings: the resistor R links v and i through v = R i, the capacitor C connects q and v through q = C v, and the inductor L ties and i through = L i. Two of the remaining pairings are simply the definitions of charge and flux linkage as time integrals. Yet, the last non-trivial relation, the one between charge q and flux linkage, had no classical element associated with it for decades.

Chua filled this gap in 1971, stating that the passive-element taxonomy was incomplete without a fourth two-terminal element that related charge to flux linkage. He named it the memristor, a combination of “memory resistor”. The element is commonly defined by the equation v = M(q) i. The memristance M(q) = d/dq carries the units of resistance, but, in contrast to a classical resistor, it depends on the charge that has previously flowed through the element.

If M is constant, the device is just a regular resistor. However, if M depends on q, the device retains a memory of its past activity in its current electrical response. This is typically indicated by a pinched hysteresis loop in the i–v plane under periodic excitation. The loop passes through the origin, since zero voltage yields zero current instantaneously, but its overall shape depends on the internal state left behind by previous stimulation. This pinched hysteresis is, then, the diagnostic used to identify memristive behaviour in the device literature. For more than three decades after Chua's proposal, the memristor remained mostly a theoretical construct. Thin-film metal-oxide resistive switches with hysteretic current–voltage characteristics had already been reported since the late 1960s, and many of them can now be recognised as memristive systems. At that time, however, they were not interpreted within Chua's formalism. The field still lacked a standard physical device that connected the theoretical idea to a tangible thin-film structure.

In 2008, that connection was made possible by Strukov, Snider, Stewart and Williams at HP Laboratories. Their device consisted of a thin titanium dioxide (TiO2) layer sandwiched between two platinum electrodes. Within the film, a reduced TiO_2-x region acted as an oxygen-deficient conductive layer, while the neighbouring TiO2 region served as a resistive barrier. When a voltage was applied, oxygen vacancies moved, shifting the boundary between these two regions. Given that the total resistance depends on the relative thickness of the conductive and resistive parts, the device resistance could then be modulated continuously by moving this internal boundary back and forth. Referring back to the equation, the boundary position is the internal state x, oxygen-vacancy drift supplies the state equation f, and the resulting i–v response reproduces the pinched hysteresis predicted by the theory.

This result transformed the memristor from a formal circuit element into a concrete experimental object, thus giving life to a large body of work on resistive switching and memristive devices. A two-terminal resistance that can be programmed by electrical pulses, tuned over several conductance values, and read at low voltage is close to the analogue weight element required by neuromorphic hardware. Crossbar demonstrations of in-memory matrix–vector multiplication followed soon. It is worth pointing out that the 2008 TiO2 device is only one mechanism, based on oxygen-vacancy drift in a transition-metal oxide, while the memristive formalism is much broader.

Classification of Memristive Mechanisms

In the field of device physics, the term memristor is usually considered a phenomenological concept, not a description of a particular microscopic mechanism. A device is considered "memristive" if its current–voltage response exhibits the pinched hysteresis that Chua described and if its internal state can be both set and read electrically. This can be achieved through many different physical processes. Literature reviews tend to group memristive devices into several broad mechanistic families, as described next.

It is worth noting upfront that these taxonomies combine two different classification axes. The first is the spatial pattern of the state change, whether filamentary or interface/distributed, depending on whether the conductance change is localised to a narrow conductive path or extended across the active region. The second is the physical nature of the state variable itself: ionic distribution, structural phase, magnetic configuration, or redox population. The two axes are orthogonal: an ionic device can also be non-filamentary, and the five families surveyed below should therefore be read as locating a device along both axes.

Historically, the most visible family is that of filamentary resistive switching. In this case, when a voltage is applied, a narrow conductive path forms through a previously insulating thin film, and said path can later be ruptured or reformed. In conductive-bridge random-access memories (CBRAM), the filament is built from metal atoms displaced electrochemically from an active electrode (like silver or copper) through a solid electrolyte. In valence-change memories, which are generally based on binary transition-metal oxides, the filament is instead associated with oxygen vacancies or partially reduced metal-cation states inside the oxide lattice.

These devices have the advantage of showing high ON/OFF ratios, quick switching times, and non-volatile memory retention, while their main limitation is stochasticity, because filament formation, positioning, and breaking vary across devices and cycles. With all this said, filament-based devices can work well as binary or coarse multi-level memories, but they aren’t as ideal for detailed analogue representations. Panel (a) of the figure contrasts the CBRAM and VCM variants of these devices, while panels (b)–(e) display the other four families described below.

The second family operates through interface or non-filamentary switching. In these devices, conductance changes over a distributed region rather than through a single localised filament. Some implementations modulate the height or width of a Schottky barrier by accumulating or depleting mobile ions near a metal–semiconductor interface. Other implementations, especially in mixed ionic–electronic systems, drive a distributed electrochemical doping of the semiconductor. Given that the state change is spatially extended, these devices can support a quasi-continuous range of conductance values. The ON/OFF ratio is often smaller than that of filamentary devices, but variability is usually lower and the current tends to scale more predictably with device area. This one is the closest spatial-pattern class for several organic electrochemical devices, including the polymer-composite devices studied here, whose state variable is itself described under the ionic family below.

The third family consists of phase-change memories. The active material is a chalcogenide alloy, usually Ge2Sb2Te5 or a related composition, whose resistance depends on whether the material is in a crystalline or amorphous configuration. Joule-heating pulses are then used either to melt and quench the material into the amorphous state, or to anneal it back toward the crystalline state. Phase-change memories offer excellent retention, large ON/OFF ratios, and high endurance. The cost lies in relatively large write energies and, unless the device is carefully engineered, a tendency toward binary or few-state operation. Still, they are mature storage-class memories and are nowadays among the most important platforms for analogue in-memory computing demonstrations.

The fourth family is that of spintronic or magnetic resistive devices. Their resistance depends on the magnetic configuration of a tunnel junction, typically determined by the relative orientation of two ferromagnetic layers. Spintronic devices can switch very quickly and at a low write energy, and they form the basis of spin-transfer-torque magnetic random-access memory. Their limitations for analogue synaptic use boil down to a relatively small ON/OFF ratio (often of order two) and a limited number of stable intermediate states. Even so, their intrinsic stochastic dynamics have been put to use as a computational resource within appropriately designed architectures.

The fifth and final family is that of ionic memristors, and it is the one most relevant to this thesis. Their internal state is the distribution of one or more mobile ionic species inside a solid matrix. The host can be an inorganic oxide, a chalcogenide, a mixed ionic–electronic conductor, or a polymer electrolyte. Likewise, the mobile species can be a small cation such as Li+, Na+, or Ag+, a proton, or a small anion. What sets this family apart is the fact that the state variable is itself a chemical distribution of mobile species, and not only a structural, morphological, or magnetic configuration of the host. Its kinetics are then governed by ionic transport, coordination, and relaxation within the matrix.

Ionic systems are intrinsically history-dependent: the distribution of mobile species at any moment reflects the previous electric fields applied to the device. Also, they often relax once the field is removed: the intermolecular forces and concentration gradients that oppose the biased ion distribution end up driving the system back toward equilibrium. Thus, volatility is a part of their physical operation. Furthermore, the associated timescales are set by ion transport and binding, both of which can be tuned chemically by selecting the host matrix and mobile species. That link between chemistry and relaxation is why this thesis focuses on the organic polymer-electrolyte subfamily later.

Key Memristive Parameters

Since memristive devices exhibit diverse mechanisms, they are typically evaluated using a shared set of practical parameters. The accessible conductance range is defined by the high-resistance state (HRS) and low-resistance state (LRS), with their ratio indicating the ON/OFF ratio. The set and reset voltages determine the electrical stimuli required to switch between states, while the write energy reflects the cost of each programming event. Switching speed indicates how quickly the device responds, and retention measures how long a state is maintained without reprogramming. Endurance shows the number of cycles before degradation. For analogue use, it is also important to consider additional factors such as the linearity and symmetry of conductance changes under positive and negative pulses, along with device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability.

These parameters are most naturally understood in the context of devices designed to store trained weights in analogue in-memory-computing arrays. In this scenario, the goal is a stable conductance matrix representing a trained neural network. Retention must be long enough to preserve weights between programming events, and endurance must cover re-training and write–verify cycles. Linearity and symmetry, in turn, determine the numerical accuracy of in-place gradient-based training, while variability reduces inference accuracy. Much of the memristor research is based on this perspective. As a result, organic and polymer-composite devices are often seen as less desirable due to their shorter retention times, limited durability, and greater variability between devices.

However, not every parameter is improved by making it as large, as small, or as stable as possible. For a trained-weight memory, the longer the retention, the better, but for a fading-memory device, a more useful retention time is one matched to the signal timescale. A similar interpretation can be made about switching speed, write energy, update linearity, and symmetry. Their optimum values differ between trained-weight storage and temporal computing, and, in general, a single device cannot optimise both regimes at once. Variability is another concept that requires clarification. Intra-device instability (that is, when a device cannot reproduce its own response under identical conditions) remains a defect. Inter-device heterogeneity, on the other hand, is a different matter altogether. It is harmful in a trained-weight array, yet useful in a reservoir, where a set of devices with different time constants and nonlinear transfer functions provides a much richer projection of the input than a set of identical devices would.

Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors

Over the past decade, a range of computational paradigms has developed around volatile, heterogeneous, and history-dependent devices. In these approaches, a memristive device that decays its state over a controlled timescale functions as a physical leaky integrator, serving as one of the basic components of temporal computation. The three examples discussed below are later revisited in the application chapter, where the polymer-electrolyte devices are evaluated using the metrics implied by each paradigm.

The most prominent example is physical reservoir computing, already introduced in the section. In a volatile-memristor reservoir, the input is encoded as a stream of voltage pulses applied to an array of devices. Their internal states evolve in response to the stimuli and then relax between them. At every moment, the overall state of the array offers a nonlinear mapping of recent input history into a high-dimensional feature space. A linear readout trained on that space can then tackle temporal classification, regression, or prediction tasks. Demonstrations have been reported in diffusive metal-filament devices, dynamic oxide memristors, and organic electrochemical systems.

A second paradigm uses volatile memristors as coincidence detectors and temporal-correlation extractors. If pulses arrive closer together than the relaxation time of the device, the internal state accumulates. If, on the contrary, they arrive after the state has already decayed, little accumulation takes place. The device response then carries information about the temporal proximity of events, which can be exploited to distinguish coincident or near-coincident inputs from dispersed ones. Architectures based on this idea have been proposed and partially demonstrated with STDP-like rules, where the sign and magnitude of the response depend on the relative timing of pre- and postsynaptic events. In this sense, the device directly implements the timing sensitivity already introduced in the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section.

The third approach uses banks of volatile memristors with different relaxation time constants as multi-timescale transient filter banks. In this case, a single input is processed simultaneously by devices with different intrinsic timescales: fast devices respond quickly to sharp changes and then return to baseline, while slow devices integrate information over longer durations. A downstream classifier can then detect onsets, bursts, rhythms, or slow envelopes that cannot be captured by any single time constant alone. Ionic memristors are especially attractive here, since their relaxation timescale can, in principle, be shifted by changing the host composition or the mobile ions.

In these modes, the relevant metrics differ from those based on trained weights listed in the Key Memristive Parameters section. In reservoir computing, the key metric is the device setup's memory capacity, which indicates how well the present reservoir state reconstructs past inputs at various delays. This is then complemented by class separability on a benchmark task and the robustness of computation against intrinsic device variability.

In the case of coincidence detection, relevant metrics include the width and symmetry of the timing window, the discrimination margin between coincident and dispersed inputs, and the energy cost per detected event. Finally, for multi-timescale filtering, metrics focus on the accessible range of time constants, channel isolation, and system-level power under realistic input conditions. HRS, LRS, ON/OFF ratio, and retention alone do not capture these aspects. With all this being said, these metrics are task-matched, meaning that they favour devices with physical timescales that align with the signal's temporal structure.

In summary, the memristor is more than just a memory cell; it represents a circuit concept, a variety of physical mechanisms, and serves as a link between biological synapses and dynamic computing. Ionic memristors are particularly interesting because their state relies on the chemical distribution of mobile species, with relaxation playing a key role in their function. The following section assesses the main inorganic memristor families used in current neuromorphic hardware, showing their advantages and the reasons for exploring alternative organic solutions.

Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing

The taxonomy of memristors shown in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section can now be narrowed down to the inorganic families that dominate the neuromorphic hardware of today, including metal-oxide resistive memories, phase-change memories, and spin-transfer-torque magnetic tunnel junctions. Each of these families is technologically important and, together, they have made memristive hardware a credible post-von-Neumann substrate. Other inorganic candidates (ferroelectric tunnel junctions and FeFETs, Mott-insulator devices, and pure threshold-switching selectors) have also been explored as neuromorphic options, but their capacity in analogue synaptic operation is narrower than that of the three families surveyed below, and they are therefore left outside the scope of this comparison.

Metal-Oxide Memristors

The broadest and most technologically developed inorganic family is the one based on resistive switching in transition-metal-oxide thin films, in both their VCM and CBRAM variants already described in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section. After the TiO2 device developed by Strukov and colleagues, the research field rapidly grew to include other oxide materials, such as hafnium dioxide (HfO2), tantalum oxide (TaOx), nickel oxide (NiO), tungsten oxide (WOx), and titanium dioxide itself. Many of these materials were already familiar to silicon processing as gate-dielectric or interconnect-related layers, which made back-end-of-line integration feasible from the start. CMOS compatibility is, in fact, the main practical reason for the prominence of metal-oxide memristors in neuromorphic demonstrations, because large arrays can be fabricated on top of existing addressing electronics. As a result, demonstrations such as the metal-oxide crossbar of Prezioso et al. followed.

Both VCM and CBRAM devices typically need an initial forming process, during which a localised soft breakdown creates the filament geometry that can then be modulated by set and reset pulses. Once formed, the device can behave as a bistable memory, or even as a graded multi-state element (depending on the compliance current and the pulse protocol). The non-filamentary interface-type alternative mentioned in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section adds another option, which makes finer analogue gradation possible by taking advantage of distributed oxygen-vacancy redistribution at a metal–oxide Schottky interface.

The strengths of metal-oxide memristors are essentially those of a mature binary or few-level non-volatile memory. Retention can reach years at room temperature, switching can occur on nanosecond timescales, and endurance can exceed 10^9 cycles. Furthermore, densities compatible with sub-10 nm crossbars are, in principle, possible. These properties have made metal-oxide resistive memories the reference platform for commercial and pre-commercial in-memory-computing demonstrations. Interestingly, the same broad family can also be made volatile through diffusive metal-filament devices, in which Ag or Cu clusters spontaneously dissolve after programming. In other words, metal-oxide memristors are not intrinsically non-volatile, given that their behaviour depends on materials, stack geometry, and protocol.

The picture changes, however, once these devices are asked to behave as an analogue synapse rather than as a non-volatile weight cell. The stochasticity already noted in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section translates directly into synaptic noise: set voltage, reset voltage, and the achieved conductance retain an appreciable device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle spread, while non-ideal linearity and asymmetry under positive and negative pulses limit the accuracy of in-place gradient-based training on metal-oxide crossbars. What is more, multi-level operation is possible, but usually only for a limited number of reliably distinguishable states, so many architectures end up using multi-cell unit codes or hybrid digital–analogue representations. Thus, analogue linearity, symmetric bidirectional update, and fine-grained timescale engineering are harder targets to achieve on this kind of devices.

Phase-Change Memory

Phase-change memory devices differ from filamentary metal-oxide memories in that the resistance is not set by a single localised path, but by the proportion of crystalline and amorphous material in the programmed volume, as mentioned in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section. PCM is also an advanced technology among memristive applications. It has been developed as a storage-class memory since the 1970s, it has gone through several commercial product generations, and it has become one of the strongest inorganic platforms for analogue in-memory computing. Le Gallo and co-workers, for instance, demonstrated mixed-precision in-memory arithmetic on PCM arrays with enough accuracy to solve practical linear systems when paired with a digital co-processor. Ambrogio et al. later showed neural-network training using PCM elements as the long-term weight store, paired with a volatile capacitor for fine-grained updates. All in all, these results make PCM the most serious benchmark for trained-weight analogue applications.

Read as a synapse, however, PCM reveals a different cost profile. The reset operation requires local melting, so the write currents and thermal loads are large and neighbouring cells must be thermally isolated. The quenched amorphous phase is, in addition, not perfectly stable. Its resistance drifts over time, usually with a power-law dependence, and this drift has to be compensated by periodic reading, calibration, or algorithmic correction.

For this reason, tight thermal control and pulse protocols must be carefully designed in order to achieve a precise analogue programming of intermediate states. The underlying physics here is thermal and structural, which limits the degree of chemical control over the relevant timescales. Crystallisation and amorphisation kinetics can certainly be adjusted through alloy composition and device geometry, but not with the degrees of freedom available in soft-matter systems. With all this being said, PCM is an interesting analogue in-memory-computing platform, but its natural strength lies in high-retention, high-endurance trained weights rather than in the chemically tunable fading-memory regime pursued here.

Spin-Transfer-Torque Magnetic Tunnel Junctions

As introduced in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section, the implementation of these devices is a magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) with two ferromagnetic layers separated by a thin insulating barrier (typically MgO). In a spin-transfer-torque (STT) device, a spin-polarised current switches the free layer between parallel and antiparallel configurations; read and write operations share the same electrical port, while the internal state variable is the magnetic configuration of the free layer (not a chemical or morphological state of the host).

Beyond the fast switching and low write energy already noted, STT–MTJs are attractive for neuromorphic computing because they reach endurance above 10^12 cycles, femtojoule-scale write energies, and back-end-of-line CMOS integration. The stochastic computing demonstrations of Torrejon et al. and Mizrahi et al. already cited in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section, exploit precisely these properties.

Approached from the synapse side rather than the weight cell side, however, STT–MTJs carry their own set of trade-offs. Increasing the small parallel/antiparallel resistance ratio noted in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section, or creating many programmable intermediate states, requires more elaborate stacks such as synthetic antiferromagnetic references, perpendicular-anisotropy layers, or multilayer configurations. These additions increase process complexity.

There is also a direct trade-off between thermal stability, which sets retention, and switching current, which sets write energy. The materials stack is also quite complex. Because it requires ferromagnetic multilayers, synthetic antiferromagnets, and crystalline MgO, it isn't compatible with simple solution processing or fabrication on arbitrary substrates. Overall, STT–MTJs are best viewed as fast, non-volatile, few-state stochastic elements, strong in probabilistic computing and embedded non-volatile memory, but less natural as a platform for analogue, multi-timescale synaptic emulation.

General Limitations of Inorganic Memristors

The three inorganic families reviewed above differ strongly in both mechanism and maturity. Still, they share several limitations with respect to the specific neuromorphic-materials problem addressed in this thesis.

The first limitation is found in their chemical tunability. These devices rely on a limited selection of materials, including transition-metal oxides, tellurium-based chalcogenide alloys, or engineered ferromagnetic stacks. The available design pathways or "knobs" are mostly geometric, stoichiometric (within narrow windows), or process-related. It is therefore difficult to tune the energetic or kinetic parameters, for instance, substituting one mobile ion for another with different binding chemistry, or by changing the host matrix in a way that directly changes segmental motion.

The second limitation has to do with dynamical timescale engineering. Each inorganic family can certainly display volatile behaviour in specific implementations: diffusive metal-filament devices provide fading-memory dynamics, PCM shows resistance drift in the amorphous phase, and superparamagnetic MTJs introduce stochastic fluctuations on engineered timescales. What is harder, however, is to sweep a relaxation time constant systematically over several orders of magnitude within one materials platform by simply tuning a chemical variable. Vacancy drift, crystallisation, and magnetic relaxation are each determined by the intrinsic energy landscape of the host. In other words, for heterogeneous reservoirs and multi-timescale filter banks, where deliberately distinct time constants are needed, this limitation becomes more important.

Then there is fabrication and integration. The inorganic families normally rely on vacuum deposition, high-temperature annealing, and top-down lithography. These workflows are excellent for dense crossbars integrated on CMOS, but they restrict substrate choice and processing temperature. Large-area, flexible, biocompatible, or printable platforms are not their natural environment. Solution-processable active layers, on the other hand, offer a different route: they can, in principle, be deposited on a much wider range of substrates at mild temperatures, and their active chemistry can be modified by simply altering the precursor solution.

The fourth limitation is that of stochasticity. In reservoir computing, stochasticity can be useful when it appears as controlled inter-device diversity, but it may become harmful when it appears as intra-device instability. In filamentary and phase-change systems, these two effects can be difficult to separate. A device family in which heterogeneity can be introduced deliberately through composition, while each device remains relatively stable under repeated operation, would therefore provide a much cleaner route to controlled variability.

These limitations define the gap addressed here. Analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on soft, solution-processable substrates is not, in essence, the natural strength of the inorganic families. Organic Materials for Memristive Applications

The inorganic platforms surveyed above each solve part of the problem, but none of them can be tuned by chemistry alone: reshaping the switching physics requires redesigning a rigid multilayer stack rather than changing a molecule, a host, a salt, or a mixing ratio. Organic and polymeric materials lift that constraint, and the rest of this section develops the case in three steps: the Advantages of Organic Memristors section identifies the features that make them attractive for this problem, the Mechanisms in Organic Memristors section surveys the main physical mechanisms behind organic memristive behaviour (with particular weight on the ion-mediated and mixed ionic–electronic operation adopted in the rest of the thesis), and the Historical Development of Organic Memristors section closes with a historical survey that frames the question motivating the composite strategy: which organic architecture is best suited for controllable, reversible, ion-mediated neuromorphic dynamics?

Advantages of Organic Memristors

The main advantage of organic materials in memristive and neuromorphic devices is, by far, the vast number of independent variables available in the active layer. A semiconducting polymer can be modified through backbone design, side-chain functionalisation, and molecular weight. An ion-transport host can be tuned through chelating groups, topology, and glass-transition temperature. A redox-active unit can be selected from a molecular library, while a mobile ion can be chosen independently of the host according to charge, radius, and Lewis acidity. In the inorganic platforms discussed earlier, the same level of chemical control is far harder to reach.

Solution processability is the second main feature. Many organic active layers relevant to memristive operation, such as conjugated polymers, hyperbranched polyethers, polyelectrolytes, redox-active polymers, and their blends, can be deposited from solution by spin coating, blade coating, inkjet printing, spray coating, or roll-to-roll methods. For the composite devices of this thesis, for instance, host-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor ratios can be modified by simply changing volumes or concentrations of the individual components when preparing the solution.

Furthermore, most organic memristive layers can be processed below 150 degrees Celsius, and often well below 100. Top contacts can be deposited through shadow masks without a high-temperature annealing process. Because of this, the fabrication can be compatible with flexible plastics, paper, textiles, elastomers, and even biological substrates. The mechanical flexibility of the final device arises from the processing route and soft active layer as opposed to coming from a single molecule. Flexible and stretchable organic synaptic devices show that this compliance is well within reach of standard organic-electronic fabrication and that it matters for wearable, implantable, and conformal neuromorphic systems under the nervetronic heading.

Taken all together, independent chemistry, solution processability, and mild processing define a materials space that is genuinely different from the inorganic one. Low cost, reproducibility, biocompatibility, and long lifetime still have to be demonstrated case by case for each system. What this materials class does provide, however, is control over internal-state physics without having to rebuild the entire device stack.

Two consequences matter most here. First, organic systems can support finely tunable analogue responses, because their switching mechanisms (that is, charge trapping on molecular sites, redox activity in a macromolecular host, or mobile-ion displacement in a polymer matrix) are typically distributed over many sites. This makes intermediate states much more accessible than in mechanisms based on a single localised filament.

Secondly, relaxation timescales can be engineered chemically. Ion transport in a polymer electrolyte depends on how strongly the mobile ion binds to the polymer, on how mobile the host segments are, and on how many mobile species are actually present. In an organic composite, each of these quantities becomes a design variable. Timescales from milliseconds to many seconds, relevant to biological short-term plasticity and slow temporal signals, can therefore be explored within a single materials family through compositional changes.

Each demonstration in the literature occupies a different point in this materials space, each with its own pros and cons. The history of the field portrayed in shows that combining reversibility, endurance, multi-state analogue control, and interpretable ionic physics in a single two-terminal organic device remained a difficult challenge well into the last decade.

Mechanisms in Organic Memristors

Unlike phase-change memory or valence-change memory devices, organic memristors do not specify a single mechanism. Organic devices can display memristive behaviour through several physical processes, each with its own state variable, reversibility, timescale, and degree of analogue control. It is then more useful to compare the main families by asking what each of them offers, what it costs, and where the ion-mediated family sits among them.

The first family is that of charge trapping and detrapping in an insulating or weakly conducting matrix. The active layer contains deep or shallow traps associated with molecular defects, embedded nanoparticles, or interfaces between incompatible polymer phases. Under bias, carriers occupy or empty those traps. The device state is then defined by the trap occupation, and the resistance changes because the local space-charge environment modifies electronic transport through the matrix.

This route is promising, since trapping is common in disordered organic semiconductors and can occur at modest voltages. Its weakness, however, comes from the limited analogue control. The state space is often just binary, the conductance can depend non-monotonically on trap occupation, and the microscopic identity of the traps is, in many cases, difficult to establish. Retention is then set by trap depth relative to thermal energy, and that depth is not easy to tune systematically through composition alone. The second family concerns redox switching. Here, the active layer (or one component of it) contains a species whose oxidation state can be changed under bias. This species can be a discrete molecule in a polymer host, a redox-active polymer, or a redox unit attached to a conjugated backbone. The device state is defined by the distribution of oxidised and reduced species, given that this distribution shifts the local electronic structure and the conductance. In this case, the state variable is a chemical oxidation state, the relevant potentials are experimentally accessible, and the process can be reversible over many cycles. Important examples include redox-active conjugated polymer systems and early electrochemical devices based on coordination complexes.

However, not every redox process is cleanly reversible due to side reactions, dimerisation, and slow degradation, which can all limit endurance. In addition, purely redox-based devices often behave as bistable or few-state systems, unless the response is distributed over many sites. Such a distribution can certainly recover analogue behaviour, but the interpretation then shifts from the chemistry of a single well-defined switch to the statistics of an ensemble.

The third family is that of conformational or structural switching. In this case, the active layer contains a molecular species whose geometry, isomerisation state, or aggregation state can be modulated by an electric field, local heating, or coupled electrochemical processes. Examples include photochromic and electrochromic switches, as well as bistable molecular architectures whose conductance depends on molecular conformation. The state of these systems is related to a well-defined structural change, so they offer a high level of molecular design precision. However, the synthesis can be challenging, the switching timescale is fixed by a particular molecular rearrangement, and scaling from molecular behaviour to device-level averages adds more complexity.

The fourth family, and the one most relevant to this thesis, is that of ionic redistribution in mixed ionic–electronic systems. Here, the active layer includes an electronic conductor, usually a conjugated polymer, along with mobile ions contained within an ion-conductive host. When bias is applied, ions move and gather near interfaces or within specific regions of the active layer. This redistribution influences electronic transport through mechanisms such as interfacial doping, electrostatic band bending, electrochemical doping, or a combination of these. The change in conductance can be gradual, involve multiple states, and is directly linked to the quantity and spatial arrangement of the displaced ionic charge.

This process is closely linked to light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs), where ionic redistribution under bias creates a doped junction, and it is also linked to organic electrochemical transistors (OECTs), where gate-driven ion exchange modulates a conducting-polymer channel. The two-terminal memristive mode, however, uses the same kind of ionic redistribution in a different way, such that the driving bias itself takes on the role of the gate, while the residual ionic distribution left after the bias is removed is what carries the memory of the stimulus.

For these devices, the state variable is therefore the position and distribution of mobile ions in a polymer host. Once the bias is removed, relaxation is what determines the fading-memory process, which is governed by ion transport, polymer binding environment, salt concentration, and film morphology. Nevertheless, in working with this family of devices, potential drawbacks are that ionic motion is slower than electronic switching and that side reactions with electrodes must be avoided through chemistry and contact selection.

The last family included here is that of organic filamentary conduction. Metallic species (typically Ag+ or Cu+ from an active electrode) migrate through the organic matrix and form conductive bridges similar to those found in inorganic CBRAM. These devices inherit the strengths of filamentary switching, such as large ON/OFF ratios and low switching voltages, but also its weaknesses, like stochastic filament formation and intra-device instability. The opportunity in these devices is that of mechanisms where chemical specificity controls the internal-state physics itself.

Historical Development of Organic Memristors

The organic memristor literature of the last three decades does not really follow a single storyline, instead having parallel developments, each based on different mechanisms and architectures. In this sense, the useful question is not which device came first, but which problems each stream solved, and which combinations of features were still missing when the work reported in this thesis began.

The earliest two-terminal organic memristive devices are usually taken to be the polyaniline (PANI)-based electrochemical junctions developed by Erokhin, Berzina, and co-workers from the mid-2000s onwards. In those devices, a polyaniline channel is interfaced with a poly(ethylene oxide)-based polymer electrolyte, and the local doping state of the polyaniline is set electrochemically by ionic redistribution under bias. Two features of this work matter here. First, the active layer already combined a conjugated polymer with an ion-conducting polyether host, which is essentially the materials logic later extended throughout this thesis. Second, the device was framed from the start as an adaptive, analogue-tunable element rather than a binary switch, and was shown to support history-dependent conductance changes useful for stimulus-response learning.

A parallel development repurposed the slow, history-dependent ionic redistribution of light-emitting electrochemical cells as a memristive function. Zakhidov and co-workers reported a light-emitting memristor in which an ionic [Ru(bpy)3]^2+ complex supplied both the emissive species and the mobile ionic component. For the most part, its importance was that it showed that ionic redistribution in a polymer host could produce memristive behaviour, even if the device itself was not yet a practical memory element. In parallel, resistive switching was reported in simple polymer films such as poly(vinyl alcohol). These devices made it clear that memristive phenomena were accessible in inexpensive polymer matrices, but they were typically binary, mechanistically opaque, and not analogue.

A second development used the organic electrochemical transistor as the platform for synaptic emulation. Gkoupidenis and colleagues showed that PEDOT:PSS OECTs could reproduce short-term plasticity, paired-pulse facilitation, and long-term potentiation and depression under certain pulse protocols, and later extended this approach to devices with global electrolyte connectivity. At the same time, Xu and colleagues reported core–sheath nanowire synaptic transistors, while Van de Burgt, Fuller, and co-workers developed the non-volatile organic electrochemical neuromorphic device (ENODe), in which a dedicated reservoir supplies a linearly updateable synaptic weight. Taken together, these works showed that organic electrochemistry can support synaptic learning rules at low voltage and with good analogue control, and they also established the importance of ion exchange between an electrolyte and a conducting polymer channel. OECT devices with halide-perovskite gates pushed the accessible timescales even further.

The three-terminal route is useful, but architecturally rather distinct from the device pursued here. An OECT synapse contains a gated channel, a separate electrolyte, and, in the non-volatile type, a dedicated programming reservoir. Read and write can be separated, but each synaptic element requires at least a third terminal and a contacted electrolyte volume. A simpler two-terminal device (with the ionic physics contained inside the active layer itself) is an attractive complement at this point, especially for the reservoir and coincidence-detection paradigms introduced in the Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors section.

The two-terminal line, in turn, developed in parallel. Liu and co-workers reported a biomimicking redox organic memristor in which an organic redox system mixed in a polymer matrix produced a short-term-to-long-term memory transition under repeated stimulation. This was significant because it demonstrated coexisting memory regimes in a passive two-terminal device. At the same time, it also exposed one of the main difficulties of the redox route: these processes are not always fully reversible, and the long-term regime can sometimes involve partially irreversible chemical transformations rather than a controlled state evolution—a limitation already documented for redox-based organic memory more broadly. At that point, analogue, reversible, multi-state behaviour in a two-terminal organic device without permanent redox changes still remained an open problem.

Two further lines matter here. Stretchable and mechanically compliant organic synaptic devices, including the bioinspired artificial afferent nerve of Kim and co-workers and the nervetronic framing reviewed by Park and co-workers, demonstrated that the mechanical advantages of organic processing can be realised at the device level. Molecular memristors, in turn, especially the metal-coordinated azo-aromatic layers reported by Goswami and co-workers, showed what precise molecular design can achieve in terms of contrast, endurance, and multi-state operation. Their cost lies in a demanding synthesis, which places them in a rather different practical regime from the composite devices considered here.

Hybrid systems sit alongside these families. Perovskite–organic hybrids combine the ionic conduction of halide perovskites with organic charge-transport layers, while conjugated polymer systems with non-volatile switching have also been explored. More recently, organic electrochemical reservoir computing has shown that mixed ionic–electronic organic devices can operate as physical-reservoir elements while remaining volatile.

By the early 2020s, organic neuromorphic electronics had grown into a more mature subfield with many active developments, set within a broader neuromorphic-device landscape that had itself begun to consolidate around shared physical principles. Ion-mediated mechanisms had been shown in both three-terminal and two-terminal architectures. Analogue and symmetric programming had been achieved in electrochemically gated devices. Short-term-to-long-term transitions had been demonstrated in two-terminal redox systems. Mechanical compliance had been shown in stretchable devices, while molecular-level control had been shown in engineered molecular memristors.

What had not yet been consolidated, however, was the full set of features in a single simple two-terminal platform. The three-terminal electrochemical route achieved reversibility and analogue control, but at the cost of architectural complexity. The two-terminal redox route achieved simplicity, but not necessarily full reversibility. The molecular route achieved precision, but at the cost of a demanding synthesis. The stretchable route, in turn, demonstrated mechanical compatibility, but did not by itself demonstrate a specific memory mechanism. In other words, there is a lack of a two-terminal, reversible, multi-state, ion-mediated device with interpretable chemistry and standard organic-electronic components. The route pursued here is a composite of a conjugated semiconducting polymer, an ion-conducting polymer host, and a dissociated alkali-metal salt, as will be seen in the subsequent chapters of this thesis. Polymer Electrolyte Composites: The Enabling Strategy

The Organic Materials for Memristive Applications section narrowed the field to devices in which ionic redistribution in a polymer host is coupled to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor, and it motivated the two-terminal route as a simpler complement to electrochemical-transistor architectures. The remaining question is what should actually be placed inside the active layer. The strategy adopted here is a composite: a semiconducting conjugated polymer, an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host, and a dissociated alkali-metal salt, all processed together from a common solution into a standard two-terminal stack.

Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles

An ion-conducting polymer is a macromolecular host in which dissolved or dispersed ions can move under an applied electric field without the help of a liquid solvent phase. The field started in 1973, when alkali-metal salts were first shown to form solid complexes with poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO), and was put on a quantitative footing two years later, when Wright reported the first measurements of ionic conductivity in those complexes. The picture was then consolidated during the late 1970s and early 1980s, when the same complexes were established as practical solid ion conductors. Solid polymer electrolytes were originally pursued mainly for lithium batteries, but they also exhibit characteristics of particular interest in this work: cation coordination, segmental motion, host crystallinity, and composition. These are the same principles that make polymer electrolytes useful for tunable memristive applications.

The polyether host has a high density of ether oxygens along the backbone or side chains. In PEO, those oxygens appear regularly along the -\!CH_2\!-\!CH_2\!-\!O\!- repeat unit, and the chain conformation in the amorphous phase allows each cation to coordinate several ether oxygens from one or more polymer chains. Hybrane-type hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts arrange the oxygen density differently, through ether and ester groups attached as side chains on a branched polymer core, but the coordination chemistry is of the same kind.

In both hosts, the oxygen atoms play the same chemical role. They act as hard Lewis bases that solvate alkali cations, stabilise salt dissociation, and define the local coordination environment of the cation. The anion, in contrast, is usually held more weakly (mainly through electrostatic compensation) and tends to be more mobile than the cation within the same host. This is especially the case for soft, charge-delocalised anions such as triflate (CF3SO3-) or bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TFSI, N(SO2CF3)2-).

Ion transport in these hosts is usually described through three related microscopic phenomena. From the vehicular side, a cation and its solvation shell move together through the polymer matrix, so long-range transport is coupled to polymer motion. From the hopping or site-to-site side, the cation instead transfers between coordination sites through a local rearrangement of oxygens, without macroscopic motion of the host. This last view is conceptually close to Grotthuss-type transport in proton conductors, even though the chemistry of the transfer itself is different. Finally, from the segmental-motion-assisted side, short polymer segments reorganise around the cation, creating and destroying coordination sites that allow effective diffusion. These three phenomena are not mutually exclusive, although the weight of each contribution during transport depends on host chemistry, cation identity, salt concentration, and temperature.

Examining device dynamics more closely, the macroscopic ionic mobility of a polymer electrolyte generally correlates with the segmental mobility of the host material. This segmental mobility is determined by the glass transition temperature T_g and by whether the host remains amorphous during operation. Below T_g, segmental motion is essentially frozen and ionic conduction drops. Above T_g, it is activated, and ionic transport becomes measurable, with the temperature dependence in this regime typically following the Vogel-Fulcher-Tammann form characteristic of fragile glass-formers.

Even above T_g, however, not all of the host contributes equally. Crystalline regions contribute little to conduction and can even trap ions in ordered environments; the textbook picture is that conduction proceeds essentially through the amorphous fraction, although it has since been shown that specific crystalline stoichiometric phases can themselves support measurable ionic motion. Low-crystallinity or fully amorphous hosts are therefore preferred for ion-transport applications. A low T_g and a low crystalline fraction can be pursued through copolymerisation, branching, or plasticisation. Hyperbranched architectures such as the Hybrane family are desirable in this manner because branching itself suppresses crystallinity. For room-temperature memristive operation, the host should remain amorphous and mobile under ambient conditions, and ideally, when no plasticisers need to be added.

The strength of cation binding depends directly on the identity of the cation. For this matter, the concept of hard–soft acid–base (HSAB) reasoning becomes important. Ether and ester oxygens in polyether and polyester-amide hosts are hard Lewis bases, meaning that their donor pairs are localised, their polarisability is low, and their coordination to alkali cations is primarily electrostatic. Among the alkali cations, Li+ is the hardest Lewis acid (small, with a high charge density), and should therefore interact most strongly with hard oxygen donors. Na+ and K+ are progressively softer, with larger radii, lower charge densities, and weaker electrostatic coupling to the same oxygen environment.

This means that, in a common polyether host and at fixed composition, the residence time of the cation at a coordination site should follow the coordination-strength order Li+ > Na+ > K+. This is expected to be reflected in the devices' fading-memory timescale: harder cations are expected to relax more slowly, while softer cations should relax much more quickly. Whether the measured dynamics follow this ranking is a question for the experimental chapters. A second set of design parameters is provided through composition. A polymer electrolyte is defined not only by host and ion identity, but also by the salt-to-host ratio; in a composite device, the polymer-to-semiconductor ratio plays a role as well. The salt-to-host ratio sets the number and spatial density of occupied coordination sites, while the host-to-semiconductor ratio sets the volume available for ionic transport and the connectivity of the electronic phase. The two are therefore treated as independent composition axes in the later chapters.

These considerations motivate the host choices made in this thesis. Hybrane DEO750 8500 is a hyperbranched polyester-amide with a high density of ether and ester oxygens. It provides an amorphous, low-crystallinity ion-transport host and, just as importantly, the same material family has already been validated in light-emitting electrochemical cells. It is used in the chapter as the host of the proof-of-concept Super Yellow (SY)/Hybrane/lithium triflate (LiOTf, LiCF3SO3) composite, where it supports the full synaptic functionality demonstrated in a single device.

PEO, in turn, acts as the central host for the broader comparative work. Its chemical role is similar to that of Hybrane: ether oxygens coordinate alkali-metal cations, segmental motion enables transport, and both composition and cation identity can be varied systematically. The reason for choosing it as the anchor of the comparative corpus, however, is not just chemical analogy. PEO is the most thoroughly characterised solid polymer electrolyte in the literature, with its phase diagrams, coordination geometries, conduction mechanisms, and salt-dependent dynamics having been mapped across decades of work. Anchoring the cation and composition series in PEO therefore places the measured dynamics on a host whose structural and transport behaviour is already independently constrained, so that observed differences across compositions and cations can be attributed to the variable of interest rather than to uncharacterised host effects.

Light-Emitting Electrochemical Cells as Precedent

The argument so far is that of ionic redistribution within an organic polymer host, which can be coupled to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor in a way that is both stable and reversible. Polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs) serve as a good example for this concept. In an LEC, this coupling is utilised to produce electroluminescence rather than memory, with extensive studies of the underlying physics.

Typically, an LEC is a simple two-terminal sandwich device. It contains a conjugated, light-emitting polymer mixed from a shared solution with an ion-conducting polymer and a dissociated salt. When voltage is applied, ions move within the host: cations migrate toward the cathode, anions toward the anode, forming doped regions near the contacts. These doped areas inject electrons and holes into the undoped bulk, resulting in a p-i-n-like profile that develops dynamically during operation. Electroluminescence then occurs in the intrinsic region between the two doped edges.

At the microscopic level, two defining models have been used to describe LEC operation. In the electrochemical-doping model, the dominant effect is doping of the conjugated polymer. In the electrodynamic model, by contrast, the dominant effect is the lowering of charge-injection barriers by ion accumulation at the interfaces. Subsequent experiments clarified the conditions under which each model applies, and a unifying framework eventually showed that the two descriptions are not contradictory mechanisms, as they instead correspond to different injection regimes. Both modes share the same internal ion redistribution, but they differ in which specific consequence of that redistribution determines the electrical response. The two models, their reconciliation, and the bias-off configuration that the memristive composite reuses are compared on a common cathode–anode position axis in the figure. Studies of electrochemical side reactions have also identified degradation pathways and the strategies needed to suppress them.

LEC operation shows that ionic redistribution in a polymer host can coexist with electronic transport in a conjugated polymer over useful operating windows, with lifetimes reaching hundreds or even thousands of hours. The ions do not necessarily undergo destructive side reactions within a few cycles, and the conjugated polymer does not necessarily degrade irreversibly under normal operation. Contacts can be engineered, through metal choice and operating protocol, to support repeated charge injection and withdrawal. Thus, this same redistribution can plausibly serve as the internal state variable of a two-terminal memory device for memristive applications.

Furthermore, LECs help clarify the role of the solid electrolyte. In both LECs and memristive composites, the electrolyte is not the main electronic conductor; its function is to provide mobile ions whose redistribution changes the electronic response of the conjugated polymer. The difference lies in how that redistribution is subsequently used. In an LEC, the bias is kept on so that the doped p-i-n profile sustains emission. In a memristive device, the bias writes an ionic distribution, and the residual distribution left behind after the bias is removed is what carries the memory. The electrolyte chemistry and its coupling to the conjugated host are governed by the same principles in both scenarios.

The most direct precedent is the Hybrane-based composite that anchors the proof-of-concept device of the chapter. Mardegan and co-workers demonstrated stable LECs based on Hybrane hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts blended with a conjugated light-emitting polymer, reporting long operational lifetimes under continuous bias. Their work validates the semiconducting-polymer / ion-conducting-host / salt architecture, the solvent system, and the processing protocol that the chapter then redeploys under pulsed bias to operate the same composite as a memristive device.

Rationale for the Composite Approach

The composite architecture starts from a practical constraint: the active layer should not rely on one material to behave simultaneously as a good semiconductor, a good ion conductor, and a well-controlled ion–electron coupling medium. Those roles are easier to understand, and easier to tune, when they are assigned to different parts of the blend. The conjugated polymer carries the electronic current. The oxygen-rich host solvates the ions and gives them a medium in which to move. The salt supplies the mobile charged species. This separation does not remove the coupling between the components, since the device works precisely because they interact, but it makes the coupling experimentally tractable. One can change the salt, the cation, or the blend ratio while leaving the rest of the stack largely unchanged.

The salt is the most useful example of this separation. It contributes only a small fraction of the total mass, yet it fixes the identity and density of the mobile ions that write the internal state. From the HSAB argument in the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section, the harder alkali cations should bind more strongly to ether oxygens and should therefore be slower to displace from their coordination environment. In this view, the cation is not just a charge-balancing spectator. It is one of the main chemical parameters that sets the slow part of the device response.

The anion is expected to behave differently. Charge-delocalised anions such as triflate interact only weakly and nonspecifically with the host oxygens; their position is constrained mainly by electrostatic compensation with the cationic charge. They should therefore redistribute more readily than the corresponding alkali cation. The important point is that this mobility contrast is already present in an ordinary dissociated salt. It does not have to be imposed later through a more elaborate multilayer structure.

This built-in asymmetry gives the composite its central memory motif. Under bias, both ionic species are displaced, but they need not relax on the same timescale. A fast, volatile component can arise from the more mobile anions, especially near the contacts, while a slower component can remain associated with cations that are more strongly coordinated inside the host. The analogy with short- and longer-term plasticity, introduced in the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section, is therefore functional rather than biological: the same physical element can retain traces of previous stimulation over more than one time window.

Composition adds a second level of control. The salt fraction changes the number of mobile ions available to participate in the response, and therefore affects the possible size of the conductance change. The amount of ion-conducting host changes the volume and connectivity of the segmentally mobile phase in which those ions can rearrange. In principle, these two ratios should allow the amplitude of the response and the rate at which it builds up or decays to be varied separately. In practice, the variables are unlikely to be perfectly independent, which is why the comparative chapter treats this separation as an experimental question rather than as an assumption.

The same architecture is also experimentally convenient. All three components can be processed from a common solvent, so a new formulation can be prepared by changing stock-solution ratios and depositing the resulting blend on the same patterned electrode geometry. The device stack does not need to be redesigned for each composition. That mundane fabrication point matters here: it makes a composition and cation series realistic, rather than turning every data point into a separate materials-development project.

For the computational part of the thesis, the useful object is not a single optimised memory element but a controlled family of related ones. Devices made with the same transport polymer and host, but with different blend ratios or cations, should cover different ionic relaxation times. When read through a common circuit, that family can be treated as a set of temporal kernels with different decay constants. Heterogeneity, which would be undesirable if each device were meant to store a precisely trained weight, becomes useful when the goal is to span a range of fading-memory timescales. The application chapter develops this idea quantitatively from the measured device behaviour.

The experimental chapters test this rationale in two stages: first with the Hybrane/LiOTf proof-of-concept device of the chapter, and then with the broader PEO-based corpus in which composition and cation identity are varied systematically. Scope and Objectives

The preceding sections moved gradually from the limits of conventional computing to biological synapses, memristive devices, inorganic and organic implementations, and finally polymer-electrolyte composites. This closing section now turns that background into the scientific questions, objectives, and chapter structure of the thesis.

Open Scientific Questions

The preceding sections lead to three groups of questions, which are stated here in the form addressed by the chapters that follow.

The first question is whether a fully reversible two-terminal organic memristive device can be realised from a solution-processed polymer-electrolyte composite using standard organic-electronic fabrication. The historical survey of the Historical Development of Organic Memristors section pinpointed exactly this combination—two-terminal architecture, reversibility, multi-state analogue control, and chemically interpretable ionic physics—as a gap in the field. The second question follows naturally from the first: can such a device support a broad set of synaptic functions in a single platform, including analogue switching, distinguishable short- and long-term memory regimes, EPSC-like response, STDP, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism? The third question, in turn, concerns the origin of the memory regimes themselves. Can the coexistence of fast and slow memory components be explained by the cation–anion mobility asymmetry anticipated in the Rationale for the Composite Approach section? And does cation–host coordination account for the slow component through the HSAB argument developed above?

The next set of questions broadens the work from one exemplar to a comparative corpus. Within a common PEO host and a common alkali-metal triflate family, how does composition affect the device dynamics when the polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions are varied around a reference formulation? With composition fixed, how does changing the cation along the Li+, Na+, K+ series shift the measured fading-memory timescale? These questions are tackled through three measurements available across the entire corpus: I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation. The richer synaptic suite (including EPSC, STDP, separated memory retention, and impedance) remains, however, a Chapter 2 result rather than a uniform comparative dataset.

The final set of questions concerns use itself. Can the volatile response of these organic memristors be exploited as a temporal-computing primitive? Can composition and cation identity provide application-level timescale matching, such that a deliberately heterogeneous device family spans a controlled range of fading-memory constants? Can measured variability actually contribute to temporal computation, rather than just degrading it? These questions are answered in simulation in the application chapter, using behavioural models extracted from the comparative corpus; the proof-of-concept device is used here only as a source of priors and consistency checks.

Thesis Objectives

The scientific questions of the Open Scientific Questions section translate naturally into the ordered objectives listed below. These objectives, in turn, structure the remaining chapters and delimit the evidence claimed by the thesis.

Design, fabricate, and characterise a proof-of-concept two-terminal organic memristive device based on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite, processed from a single solution onto a standard ITO/composite/Ag stack, and demonstrate the basic memristive functionality in a single platform.

On that single exemplar device, demonstrate the full set of synaptic functionalities relevant to the thesis: analogue multi-state switching, distinguishable short-term and long-term memory regimes with separately measured retention curves, excitatory-postsynaptic-current behaviour under pulsed stimulation, spike-timing-dependent plasticity with a biologically plausible time constant, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism.

Provide a mechanistic account of the two memory regimes of that exemplar in terms of the displacement of mobile triflate anions and lithium cations within the oxygen-rich ion-conducting host, supported by hard-soft acid-base reasoning on the cation–oxygen interaction.

Build a comparative experimental corpus on a PEO-based polymer-electrolyte platform loaded with alkali-metal triflate salts, fabricated under a common protocol, and characterise it through three common dynamical measurements — I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation — that are abundant across the corpus.

Within that corpus, quantify (a) the composition dependence of the dynamics through a PEO/lithium triflate (PEO/LiOTf) concentration series in which the polymer and salt mass fractions are varied around a reference composition, and (b) the cation dependence through a PEO/LiOTf, PEO/sodium triflate (PEO/NaOTf, NaCF3SO3), PEO/potassium triflate (PEO/KOTf, KCF3SO3) set at a fixed reference composition, on the same three common measurements.

Treat TMPE-based composites and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiN(SO2CF3)2, NaN(SO2CF3)2, KN(SO2CF3)2) systems as exploratory side evidence with a clearly delimited dataset, used to motivate or cross-check specific trends, and not as parallel main platforms.

Construct compact, experimentally grounded behavioural models per composition and per cation from the three common dynamical measurements of the comparative corpus, with the proof-of-concept device used only as a source of priors and consistency checks where its richer synaptic data are directly relevant; the models capture a fading-memory time constant, a nonlinear pulse-update rule, a device-to-device variability envelope, and a read transfer function.

Demonstrate, in simulation and from those models alone, three application-level temporal-computing schemes that use the composition- and cation-driven timescale spread of the comparative corpus: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector built on the measured variable-delay depotentiation kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants.

The objectives mirror the evidence base in a fairly direct way. Objectives 1–3 are exemplar-level and rely on the full synaptic measurement suite of the proof-of-concept device. Objectives 4–6 are comparative and rely on the three common dynamical measurements, without assuming that the richer synaptic metrics are also available across the whole corpus. Objectives 7–8, in turn, are computational and use measurements already acquired in the experimental chapters, so that no further fabrication is required for them.

Thesis Outline

The thesis is organised in five chapters, which follow this objective list.

Chapter 1 — Introduction. The present chapter provides the conceptual and materials background, ending with the polymer-electrolyte composite as the strategy that will be used throughout the rest of the thesis.

Chapter 2 — Proof of Concept: Polymer-Based Composite Organic Memristive Devices. This chapter reports the SY/Hybrane/LiOTf proof-of-concept device. It is the only device in the thesis for which the complete synaptic suite is treated as primary evidence: I–V hysteresis, potentiation/depression, EPSC, separated short- and long-term retention, STDP, and impedance. It is also where the two-memory-regime mechanism is developed in terms of triflate-anion and lithium-cation displacement, under the HSAB framing of the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section. The chapter is built on Prado-Socorro et al. and contains the most complete synaptic dataset of the entire thesis.

Chapter 3 — PEO–Triflate Polymer-Electrolyte Memristive Devices: Composition and Ion-Identity Dependence. This comparative chapter reformulates the proof-of-concept architecture on a PEO host loaded with alkali-metal triflates. It studies composition through a PEO/LiOTf concentration series, and cation identity through a fixed-composition PEO/LiOTf, PEO/NaOTf, PEO/KOTf set. Both studies rely on the same three common measurements: I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation. TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems are reported separately as exploratory side evidence. Variability, in turn, is quantified from replicates and treated as a property of the device family.

Chapter 4 — Data-Driven Temporal Computing with Volatile Polymer-Electrolyte Memristors. This chapter turns the experimental measurements into temporal-computing functionality, without requiring any further fabrication. Compact behavioural models are extracted per composition and per cation from the three common measurements. Three schemes are then demonstrated in simulation: a heterogeneous physical reservoir, a spike coincidence detector grounded in the measured variable-delay kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built from devices with distinct fitted time constants. Circuit-level constraints such as 1T1M addressing, current compliance, read/write protocols, and variability envelopes are also treated explicitly.

Chapter 5 — Conclusions and Outlook. The closing chapter brings together the device-chemistry axis (the chapter and the comparative chapter) and the application axis (the temporal-computing chapter). It benchmarks the work against temporal, reservoir, and event-driven neuromorphic hardware, rather than against digital memory technologies. It also states the limitations of the evidence base, especially the restriction of the richer synaptic metrics to the proof-of-concept device and the exploratory status of the TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems.

All in all, the thesis links polymer-electrolyte composition and cation chemistry to measured ion-mediated dynamics, and then uses those dynamics as temporal-computing primitives. The next chapter takes the first step in that chain: the proof-of-concept SY/Hybrane/LiOTf device, its memristive and synaptic functionality, and the mechanistic interpretation that anchors the comparative and computational work that follows.
