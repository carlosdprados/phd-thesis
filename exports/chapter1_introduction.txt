Introduction

This thesis is concerned with organic memristive devices made from ion-containing polymer composites, and with the use of their dynamics as a computational resource. Three fields feed into the work: solid-state electronics, the physical chemistry of polymers, and computational neuroscience. The chapter is laid out roughly in that order. It opens with the limits of contemporary computing, looks next at the biological analogy that motivated neuromorphic engineering, then at the physics of memristive devices, and ends with the organic polymer-composite devices that the rest of the thesis is built around.

The Crisis in Modern Computing

Contemporary digital hardware is running into limits that cannot be resolved simply by making transistors smaller or clocks faster. The discussion begins with the stored-program architecture and the three constraints that now bound it in practice: the memory wall, the energy wall, and the slowdown of classical transistor scaling. It then turns to two responses to those constraints: placing computation and storage in the same physical substrate, and replacing clocked synchronous arithmetic with event-driven, temporally structured operation.

Von Neumann Architecture and Its Limitations

The architectural model that underlies modern digital computers was articulated by von Neumann in the “First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC”, circulated in 1945. At its centre is the stored-program concept: instructions and data live in the same memory and are drawn from it, one word at a time, by a central processing unit under the control of an instruction pointer. This unification of code and data was the abstraction that made general-purpose computing possible. A single physical machine could implement any computable function by loading a different sequence of binary words into memory. The resulting architecture (a CPU, a main memory holding code and data, and a bus connecting them) has become the normal starting point for digital systems, to the extent that its constituent parts—cores, caches, DRAM, register file—are treated as unquestioned primitives. More than seventy years of engineering effort have been spent optimising every stage of the pipeline while its basic topology has been left, essentially, alone.

A direct consequence of the stored-program model is that processor and memory are physically distinct subsystems separated by a communication channel, and the bandwidth of that channel sets a primary limit on system performance. Every computational step (instruction fetch, operand load, result write-back) requires one or more traversals of it. In a modern processor, the channel is a hierarchy of caches bridging register files clocked at several gigahertz and DRAM that operates one or two orders of magnitude slower. Data has to be moved along interconnect metal between the transistors that do arithmetic and the cells that hold state. No arithmetic can proceed without first transporting operands, and no result can be preserved without transporting it back. This pattern is the processor–memory dichotomy.

The single-port form of the architecture allows only one word to be read or written per clock cycle, which produces the von Neumann bottleneck. Decades of architectural innovation have progressively mitigated this bottleneck without replacing the underlying topology: pipelined fetch, multi-level caches, speculative execution, prefetching, out-of-order issue, banked memory subsystems, coprocessors ranging from vector units to GPUs. Each innovation preserves the compute–storage distinction and amortises data movement over larger blocks or deeper queues. None of them removes the asymmetry. The bottleneck has been displaced rather than dissolved, and tends to reappear at whichever interface is slowest in a given architectural generation.

Wulf and McKee diagnosed the quantitative severity of this imbalance in 1995 and named it the memory wall. Processor performance was then improving at roughly 60% per year, whereas the latency and bandwidth of commodity DRAM improved nearly an order of magnitude more slowly. Extrapolating the two curves forward leads, within a decade or two, to a regime in which the average cycle is dominated almost entirely by memory access. The decades that followed bore the projection out in detail. Modern microprocessors allocate between 30% and 60% of their silicon area, and an even larger fraction of their power budget, to caches, prefetch logic, memory controllers, and interconnect—all of it spent simply to keep the arithmetic units fed. Even heavily cache-optimised codes spend a non-negligible fraction of cycles stalled on memory, and matters are worse for workloads with irregular access patterns or with working sets larger than the on-chip cache. The memory wall is a performance argument. A second and more fundamental argument has to do with energy. Horowitz's 2014 survey of elementary-operation costs in a representative CMOS process put numbers on this in a way that has been widely cited since: a 32-bit integer addition costs on the order of 0.1 pJ, and a register-file read a few picojoules, while an on-chip SRAM fetch costs tens of picojoules and a DRAM fetch reaches several hundred picojoules to nanojoules—three to four orders of magnitude more than the arithmetic operation it enables. In any workload dominated by data movement, the total energy budget is then fixed by the cost of shuffling operands, not by the cost of computing results. That asymmetry is the energy wall. It reflects the fact that moving charge across a wire dissipates energy in the wire's parasitic capacitance, and that the total wire length in a memory subsystem far exceeds the wire length inside an arithmetic unit. Any architecture that spatially separates computation from storage is therefore bounded in speed and in energy by interconnect physics, and that bound does not scale away with transistor miniaturisation.

For several decades these architectural inefficiencies were absorbed, rather than eliminated, by the exponential improvement of the underlying silicon. Moore's observation that transistor counts double at regular intervals was paired with Dennard scaling: scaling every linear MOSFET dimension and the supply voltage by 1/k makes the transistor switch k times faster, consume 1/k^2 of the energy per switch, and occupy 1/k^2 of the area, leaving power density at constant activity invariant. While the two laws held together, each generation delivered more transistors that were also faster and cooler, and the memory and energy walls felt distant rather than binding.

Dennard scaling broke down around 2005, when gate-oxide thinning drove leakage currents into a regime that ruled out further proportional voltage scaling. Transistor count has continued to grow since then, but per-transistor performance and energy gains have slowed, and power density has begun to climb with each generation. This is the post-Dennard or dark-silicon regime. Under these conditions the inefficiencies of the von Neumann architecture become primary constraints rather than secondary ones: the cost of data movement can no longer be hidden by a new process node, and must instead be addressed architecturally. Workloads have moved in the same direction. They have shifted from cache-friendly scientific and business codes to machine-learning kernels dominated by repeated matrix multiplications over parameter tensors resident in memory, which sharpens the data-movement bottleneck just as silicon scaling stops compensating for it. The Turing-Award lecture of Hennessy and Patterson treats this moment as an inflection point comparable to the transition from vacuum tubes to transistors, and argues that the next decade of computer architecture will be defined by domain-specific hardware in which memory and computation sit alongside one another.

In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms

Two broad responses to the situation described in the Von Neumann Architecture and Its Limitations section have emerged over the past decade. Both abandon the strict separation of computation and storage, but they do so along different axes. The first keeps the clocked, arithmetic character of digital computing and performs operations directly inside the memory array; it goes under the labels in-memory, near-memory, or processing-in-memory computing. The second drops the synchronous arithmetic model altogether. It runs on temporally structured event streams instead, and treats the intrinsic dynamical behaviour of the physical devices as part of the computation. This second route is known as event-driven or temporal computing.

In-memory computing replaces the classical fetch–compute–write-back loop with operations implemented physically inside a memory array. The canonical example is the analogue matrix–vector multiplication performed on a resistive crossbar. Memory cells hold conductances that encode the matrix entries, the input vector is applied as row voltages, and column currents (by Kirchhoff's laws) yield the sums of products in a single clock period. The energy cost is dominated by steady-state dissipation, not by per-operation data transfer. Demonstrations in metal-oxide resistive memories, in phase-change materials, and in organic ionic devices have shown matrix–vector products at energy efficiencies well beyond digital accelerators, and the same mechanism generalises to convolutional and attention primitives. The memory array, in this view, is an active computational substrate.

At the device level, in-memory computing still demands the properties of a memory. Each cell should hold a quasi-static conductance that encodes a trained weight; the conductance should be set once and then read reliably without drift; device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability should fall below the numerical precision the application requires. Non-volatility over seconds to years is a design goal. By this yardstick polymer-composite ionic devices look poor: their device-to-device variability is appreciable, their conductance drifts during repeated reads, and their retention is on the order of seconds to hours. Read in this way, they are flawed competitors to metal-oxide or phase-change memories.

The second paradigm starts from a different observation. Many tasks of interest—processing natural auditory and visual signals, controlling continuous motor systems, classifying biomedical waveforms, extracting features from streaming sensor data—are defined on inputs that carry their information in temporal structure. For such tasks the natural computational model represents inputs as streams of events in continuous time, and lets the system state evolve continuously in response, instead of stepping through clock cycles. At the algorithmic level this is the regime of spiking neural networks; at the circuit level it is event-driven asynchronous logic; at the device level it calls for substrates whose intrinsic relaxation dynamics match the temporal structure of the inputs they process. The present thesis sits at the device level.

The inspiration for this second paradigm is biological. Mammalian nervous systems carry out demanding sensorimotor and cognitive tasks on power budgets orders of magnitude below their artificial counterparts, and a meaningful part of that efficiency comes from architectural choices that depart systematically from the von Neumann model. Neurons emit discrete events sparsely. Synapses carry conductances that are themselves dynamical variables. State is maintained and modified through slow, graded electrochemical processes, and the ionic medium itself is part of the computation. A direct transcription into silicon is neither feasible nor desirable, but several lessons are transferable: sparse-event representations, slow device physics as a computational primitive, and device heterogeneity treated as functional diversity rather than as defect. Together these form the conceptual basis of neuromorphic engineering.

A clean theoretical articulation of the computational value of such devices is due to Boyd and Chua. They showed that any time-invariant nonlinear operator with fading memory on a suitable function space can be approximated uniformly by a finite Volterra series. The implication is that a physical system whose present state carries a smoothly decaying trace of its past inputs can, when combined with a trainable linear readout, implement a broad class of time-varying computations. The insight was operationalised by Jaeger's echo state network and by the liquid state machine of Maass, Natschläger and Markram, and the two were later unified under the heading reservoir computing. An untrained dynamical system (the reservoir) is driven by the input stream, and only a linear readout is trained. When the reservoir is realised in hardware the paradigm becomes physical reservoir computing, and the literature includes demonstrations on optical, mechanical, and spintronic systems, on volatile memristive devices, and on organic electrochemical substrates.

This reframes how a memristive device should be judged. Finite retention, a defect under the weight-storage criterion, becomes useful in a reservoir because it sets the window over which past inputs influence the present output: it is the fading-memory timescale. Device-to-device variability is similar. Penalised under weight storage, a spread of intrinsic timescales read by a common linear layer resolves temporal structure over a wider range than any single device could on its own.

The devices studied here are accordingly not treated as candidate non-volatile weights. The experimental programme has three layers. First, a single device is characterised deeply enough to establish its physical mechanism. Second, a comparative corpus across composition and cation identity quantifies how chemistry tunes fading-memory behaviour. Third, a data-driven simulation study uses those dynamics as temporal-computing primitives.

The Biological Synapse as the Engineering Template

If the separation of storage and computation is the structural obstacle in digital computing, the next question is what a replacement primitive should actually do. Biology gives a useful answer. In a biological circuit the same physical element that propagates a signal also stores the history that gates its propagation, and that element is itself modified by the signals passing through it. That element is the synapse. The biological detail needed here is therefore the detail of synaptic signalling and plasticity, since these are what set the functional requirements for a hardware synapse.

Neural Signal Transmission: A Brief Overview

The neuron is a morphologically polarised cell specialised to receive, integrate, and transmit electrical signals. Its dendrites collect signals from other cells. The soma integrates those signals and, when the integrated membrane potential crosses a threshold, fires an outgoing signal at the axon hillock. The axon propagates that signal over distances ranging from millimetres to metres, and its terminals release a chemical messenger onto downstream cells. A mature mammalian neuron typically receives inputs from 10^3–10^4 others through contacts on its dendrites, and each such contact is a synapse. The computational role of the cell is set almost entirely by the pattern, strength, and dynamics of its synaptic contacts, not by anything intrinsic to the soma considered on its own.

The signal that travels along the axon is the action potential: a stereotyped millisecond-long depolarisation produced by voltage-gated Na+ channels opening, followed by K+-mediated repolarisation, and regenerated at each internodal patch. Action potentials are all-or-nothing. Their waveform is essentially independent of the input strength that elicited them, and information is carried instead in the temporal properties of spike trains: rate, precise timing, and coincidence across neurons. The physical substrate of neural computation is, in this sense, event-driven. Between spikes a neuron consumes only a leakage current, and during a spike it dissipates a small and nearly fixed amount of energy. Useful computation is performed by the patterned arrival of events at the many synapses, not by a clocked update of a shared register.

Transmission at the synapse is mostly chemical. When an action potential reaches an axon terminal, the depolarisation opens voltage-gated Ca^2+ channels, and the Ca^2+ influx triggers vesicles loaded with a neurotransmitter (typically glutamate, the dominant excitatory transmitter in the cortex) to fuse with the pre-synaptic membrane. The transmitter diffuses across the 20 nm synaptic cleft and binds to receptor proteins on the post-synaptic membrane, where it opens ion channels directly or through second-messenger cascades. The ionic current produced—the excitatory or inhibitory post-synaptic current (EPSC or IPSC)—briefly shifts the post-synaptic membrane potential, and its amplitude and kinetics define the strength of the synapse at that moment. The process is probabilistic and quantal. Crucially, the same machinery that propagates the signal can, by modulation of its own components, change the strength of subsequent transmissions.

The synapse is thus, at once, the place where signals are transmitted, where they are modulated (through the regulated amplitude and time course of the post-synaptic current), and where memory is stored (through long-lived changes in that amplitude driven by the cell's own activity). It is this co-localisation of transmission, modulation, and memory in a single physical element that makes the synapse, rather than the neuron, the more informative biological template for post-von-Neumann electronic devices. Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning

That experience leaves a physical trace in the nervous system, and that the trace lives preferentially at synapses, is one of the oldest and best-supported hypotheses in modern neuroscience. Its conceptual formulation is due to Hebb, whose 1949 monograph argued that if a pre-synaptic cell repeatedly participates in firing a post-synaptic cell, the connection between the two ought to be strengthened. The modern précis (“cells that fire together, wire together”) captures the structural claim: the learning rule is local, in that it depends only on the activity of the two cells directly coupled by the synapse, and correlation-sensitive, in that it is driven by joint rather than independent activity. As a physiological hypothesis, Hebb's postulate went without a direct experimental test for over two decades. It has since come to organise essentially every subsequent account of synaptic learning, and the various forms of synaptic plasticity described below can be read as mechanistic instantiations of Hebbian, anti-Hebbian, or Hebbian-like rules operating at different timescales.

The canonical demonstration that synaptic strength can be persistently altered by patterned activity was provided by Bliss and Lømo. Brief, high-frequency tetanic stimulation of the perforant path in the anaesthetised rabbit hippocampus produced an increase in the amplitude of the population post-synaptic potential that lasted for hours, and in later work for days to weeks. The phenomenon, long-term potentiation (LTP), is now the textbook example of persistent, experience-dependent synaptic change. Its mechanism in the hippocampus involves two classes of ionotropic glutamate receptor on the post-synaptic membrane: AMPA receptors (-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolepropionic acid), which mediate the fast EPSC in response to glutamate release, and NMDA receptors (N-methyl-D-aspartate), which open only when the post-synaptic membrane is already depolarised. NMDA receptors thus act as Ca^2+-permeable coincidence detectors for simultaneous pre- and post-synaptic activity.

NMDA-gated Ca^2+ influx triggers a signalling cascade that leads to the regulated insertion of further AMPA receptors into the post-synaptic membrane, which raises the EPSC elicited by subsequent pre-synaptic spikes. A complementary phenomenon, long-term depression (LTD), produces a persistent decrease in synaptic strength under low-frequency or anti-correlated activity, and proceeds through the reverse trafficking of AMPA receptors. LTP and LTD between them provide the bidirectional, long-lasting modification of synaptic amplitude that is widely taken to be the physiological substrate of declarative and procedural learning.

Superimposed on these long-lasting changes is a family of rapid, reversible modulations known collectively as short-term plasticity. After a pre-synaptic action potential, the immediate strength of the synapse depends on the recent history of its own activity. Short-term facilitation is a transient increase in EPSC amplitude when a second spike follows the first closely; it is attributed to residual pre-synaptic Ca^2+ that has not yet been cleared from the terminal. Short-term depression is a transient decrease attributed to vesicle depletion or receptor desensitisation after recent heavy release. The characteristic time constants run from tens of milliseconds to several seconds, and different synapses display different mixes of the two.

Short-term plasticity is, in effect, temporal filtering performed at the level of the individual synapse: the same sequence of pre-synaptic spikes produces different post-synaptic responses depending on the inter-spike intervals within that sequence. The synapse becomes a nonlinear, history-dependent temporal filter, and it does so through transient, self-resetting state changes that never consolidate into a permanent modification of the connection.

A third and unifying form of plasticity is spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP), described in cortical pyramidal cells by Markram and co-workers and characterised quantitatively in hippocampal cultures by Bi and Poo. STDP sharpens Hebb's postulate into a timing-sensitive form. Whether a given synapse is potentiated or depressed by a pairing of pre- and post-synaptic spikes depends on the sign and the magnitude of the interval between them, typically over a window of a few tens of milliseconds. If the pre-synaptic spike reliably precedes the post-synaptic spike the synapse is potentiated. If the order is reversed it is depressed. If the interval is long in either direction, no lasting change is induced. The STDP window lets a single synapse work as a directional correlation detector whose output is a long-lived change in efficacy. The rule is inherently temporal: the synapse must keep a short-lived trace of its own recent pre-synaptic activity for long enough to compare it to the post-synaptic response, and then convert that comparison into a persistent change of amplitude.

The synapse that emerges from these lines of evidence is not a passive “weight” at all, but a stateful element with several timescales running in parallel. On the millisecond scale it transmits individual spikes through quantal release. On the scale of tens to thousands of milliseconds, short-term facilitation and depression modulate that transmission according to recent activity. On the scale of minutes, hours, and longer, LTP and LTD produce persistent changes in baseline amplitude. These layers coexist at chemical synapses in the mammalian CNS, and are supported by partially overlapping but distinct molecular machinery. Abbott and Regehr argued on this basis that the synapse is better understood as a computational element than as a memory element. Short-term plasticity lets a single synapse act as a high-pass, low-pass, or band-pass temporal filter depending on its mix of facilitation and depression, and the trained circuit relies on many such filters running in parallel. A hardware synapse should therefore support both durable and decaying state changes inside the same device class.

Why Hardware Emulation of the Synapse?

The need for a hardware embodiment of synaptic function follows directly from the The Crisis in Modern Computing section. A software neural network is run as arithmetic on values drawn from a memory hierarchy that is physically distinct from the arithmetic units, and every forward pass pays the memory and energy wall costs catalogued above. Those costs scale with the number of synapses simulated. The cortex of a small mammal contains on the order of 10^12–10^14 synapses, and its nervous system runs on something like 10 W. No near-term von Neumann substrate is likely to close the gap, because the cost of fetching a weight before multiplying it by an activation is bounded by interconnect physics rather than by microarchitectural tuning. The synapse is the most compact low-power example of co-localised analogue memory and computation that we know, and that is what makes it a useful physical template.

The research programme that pursues this observation in circuit form is neuromorphic engineering. Its programmatic origin is usually attributed to Mead, and its defining ambition is to build electronic circuits in which the devices, the topology, and the dynamics together physically instantiate the computational primitives of the nervous system, rather than emulating them in software. Within that programme the synapse has become the key device-level target, and the search for a physical “artificial synapse” has driven a body of work spanning device physics, materials science, and circuit design over the past two decades.

A hardware synapse should support an analogue, multi-state conductance that can be set reversibly by applied electrical stimuli, read non-destructively through a small probe current, and laid out at high density so that many thousands of such elements can be addressed from a manageable number of input and output lines. Its response to those stimuli should be nonlinear in a biologically meaningful sense (for example a pulse-number or pulse-amplitude threshold below which no change is induced), and its dynamics should match the timescales of the signals it is meant to process, which span several orders of magnitude from milliseconds to seconds and beyond.

Among these requirements, the demand for analogue, rather than binary, multi-state conductance modulation deserves emphasis. A binary synapse encodes one bit per element, and by the standard rate-coding argument it would take prohibitively many such elements to represent the graded synaptic weights of biological circuits. An analogue synapse, by contrast, can in principle encode several bits per element through the available range of conductance values. For the ionic-conductance devices considered here, the write energy can also be several orders of magnitude below the cost of the equivalent digital multi-bit write.

Reversibility matters just as much. A synapse whose conductance can be potentiated but not reliably depressed cannot support the bidirectional Hebbian rules of the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section, and ends up useful only as a programmable weight in a feed-forward classifier, not as a substrate for on-chip learning. The basic specification is therefore analogue, reversible, multi-state, low-energy conductance modulation under electrical pulses, and it is this specification that motivates the devices considered in the rest of the thesis.

The biology of the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section sharpens the specification beyond retention-centric accounts of artificial synapses. Biological synapses are at the same time quasi-stable stores (LTP/LTD) and transient, decaying filters (short-term plasticity, STDP). The two roles coexist inside the same physical element and contribute jointly to circuit computation. A useful hardware template therefore has two device roles to fill: persistent or quasi-persistent programmable weights for the long-term component, and dynamic fading-memory elements for the short-term component. The fading-memory role is the one pursued here, and it mirrors the reservoir-computing picture developed in the In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms section: a bank of devices with a spread of time constants forms the heterogeneous reservoir that physical reservoir computing requires.

One requirement is implicit in this list and deserves to be made explicit: chemical tunability. The timescales of biological short-term plasticity are set by Ca^2+ buffering, vesicle recycling, and receptor desensitisation, and these differ across synapse types. A useful hardware family should expose an analogous way to tune electronic timescales. No classical transistor-based memory meets the full list. The next section asks whether any physical device class does.

The Memristor: Theory and History

Circuit theory turns out to provide a useful answer. A memristive device has an instantaneous current–voltage relation that depends on an internal state imposed by past activity. The concept itself is the memristor, and the broader experimental family is the family of memristive devices. This section develops the theory, reviews the first canonical experimental realisation, classifies the main mechanistic families, and then reinterprets the usual device parameters in terms both of trained-weight storage and of volatile temporal computing. The formal definition accommodates quasi-stable and relaxing internal states alike; later chapters use the relaxing-state half of that definition.

Theoretical Foundation

Classical linear circuit theory describes a two-terminal passive element by the relation it imposes between two of the four fundamental lumped variables: the voltage v, the current i, the electric charge q = i dt, and the flux linkage = v dt. Three of the six possible pairings among these four variables are occupied by the familiar elements of elementary electromagnetism. The resistor R is defined by the direct v–i relation v = R i, the capacitor C by the q–v relation q = C v, and the inductor L by the –i relation = L i. Two further pairings (between q and i, and between and v) are trivial consequences of the definitions of charge and flux linkage as time integrals. The remaining non-trivial pairing, that between charge q and flux linkage, had no classical element associated with it.

Chua filled the gap in 1971. He observed that completeness of the passive-element taxonomy required a fourth fundamental two-terminal element relating charge to flux linkage, and he named it the memristor (a contraction of “memory resistor”). The element is defined by the constitutive relation = (q) or, equivalently and in the form used more widely in the device literature that followed, by v = M(q) i, where the memristance M(q) = d/dq has the units of a resistance but depends functionally on the charge that has flowed through the device.

The operational content is straightforward: a memristor is a resistance whose present value is fixed by the history of the current that has flowed through it. If M is constant the device reduces to an ordinary resistor. If M depends non-trivially on q, the device retains a memory of past activity in its present electrical response. The universal fingerprint of such behaviour in the i–v plane is a pinched hysteresis loop that passes through the origin under periodic excitation. The pinch reflects the fact that zero current requires zero voltage instantaneously, while the device still carries a history that biases its response to subsequent stimuli. This hysteretic fingerprint is the elementary experimental criterion by which memristive behaviour is diagnosed in the device literature.

The ideal memristor defined by = (q) is, however, a restrictive mathematical object, and very few physical devices satisfy it exactly. Chua and Kang therefore generalised the concept in 1976 to that of a memristive system, specified by the pair of equations

in which u and y are the electrical input and output, x is an internal state vector, and f and g are generally nonlinear. The state equation x = f(x, u, t) may carry relaxation terms that drive the state toward a rest value once the input is removed. A device whose state relaxes is, on this definition, a memristive system, even though it is not an ideal memristor in the strict sense. Volatile devices are members of the same family, and it is this broader framework that is used for the devices reported here.

For more than three decades after Chua's original proposal, the memristor lived principally as a theoretical construct. Thin-film metal-oxide resistive switches with hysteretic current–voltage characteristics had been described in the device-physics literature since the late 1960s, and in retrospect many of those systems can be reclassified as memristive. None of them, however, had been interpreted explicitly within Chua's formalism, and the field lacked a canonical experimental realisation that tied the formal concept to a concrete thin-film device.

The identification was made in 2008 by Strukov, Snider, Stewart and Williams, working at HP Laboratories. Their device was a thin film of titanium dioxide (TiO2) sandwiched between two platinum electrodes. Within that film a narrow, heavily reduced TiO_2-x sublayer acted as an oxygen-deficient, electronically conductive region, and the adjacent stoichiometric TiO2 layer acted as a resistive barrier. Under an applied voltage the oxygen vacancies populating the sublayer drift along the electric field, and the boundary between the conductive and the resistive region is displaced through the film. The overall resistance of the stack depends on the geometric proportion of the two regions, so the device resistance is modulated continuously by the displacement of that internal boundary. In the language of the equation, the position of the boundary is the internal state x, the drift of oxygen vacancies supplies the state equation f, and the pinched hysteresis predicted by Chua's theory is recovered quantitatively from the resulting i–v response.

The impact of this result was disproportionate to the incremental physics it reported. By tying an explicit thin-film device to a previously formal theoretical construct, Strukov and co-workers turned the memristor from a mathematical curiosity into a concrete experimental object, and they catalysed a large body of subsequent work on resistive-switching and memristive devices. A two-terminal resistance that can be set to a quasi-continuous range of values by electrical pulses, and read non-destructively at low voltage, was quickly recognised as a physical realisation of the analogue weight element neuromorphic hardware needs. Crossbar arrays operating as in-memory matrix–vector multipliers followed within a few years. The 2008 TiO2 device is, of course, one specific resistive-switching mechanism (oxygen-vacancy drift in a transition-metal oxide), and the memristive formalism accommodates several mechanistically distinct families. Classification of Memristive Mechanisms

In current device-physics usage, memristor designates a phenomenological category rather than a microscopic mechanism. A device is called memristive if its current–voltage characteristic shows the pinched hysteresis loop predicted by Chua's theory and if its internal state can be set and read electrically. It is not required to rely on any specific physical process to do so. Review articles have accordingly identified several broad mechanistic families, each with its own operational strengths and weaknesses. A brief taxonomy will help clarify the physical diversity that the umbrella term hides, and locate the ionic-polymer devices studied here within the broader experimental landscape.

Historically the most visible family is filamentary resistive switching. Under an applied voltage a narrow conductive path—a filament—forms across an otherwise insulating thin film, and is then ruptured or reformed by further electrical stimuli. In conductive-bridge random-access memories (CBRAM) the filament is built from metal atoms electrochemically displaced from an active electrode (typically silver or copper) through a solid electrolyte. In valence-change memories, which are usually based on binary transition-metal oxides, the filament is built from ordered oxygen vacancies or partially reduced metal-cation states inside the oxide lattice. Filamentary devices commonly show high ON/OFF ratios, short switching times, and non-volatile retention. Their drawbacks are two. The first is the stochastic nature of filament nucleation and dissolution, which translates into appreciable device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability. The second is the limited number of reliably distinguishable intermediate conductance states that this stochasticity allows. Filamentary devices are well suited to binary storage and to coarse multi-level weights, then, but they are a less natural platform for dense analogue representations.

A second family operates through interface or non-filamentary switching. Here the active area of the device modulates its conductance uniformly rather than through a localised filament. Typical implementations vary the height or width of a Schottky barrier at a metal–semiconductor junction by accumulating or depleting mobile ionic species near the interface; in certain mixed ionic–electronic systems they instead drive a distributed electrochemical doping of the bulk semiconductor. Because the modulation is spatially distributed and not localised, interface-type devices usually support a quasi-continuous range of intermediate conductance values, and therefore finer analogue gradation than their filamentary counterparts. ON/OFF ratios tend to be smaller, but variability is typically lower and the current scales predictably with junction area, which simplifies array integration. Several of the organic electrochemical devices surveyed later in the chapter fall into this class, and so, in large part, do the ionic polymer-composite devices studied experimentally here.

A third family consists of phase-change memories. The active element is a chalcogenide alloy—usually Ge2Sb2Te5 or a close compositional relative—whose electrical resistance depends on whether it sits in a crystalline or an amorphous phase. Programming is carried out by Joule-heating pulses that either melt and rapidly quench the material, or anneal it partially. Phase-change memories deliver excellent retention, large ON/OFF ratios, and high endurance. The price is appreciable per-write energy, and a tendency towards binary or few-state behaviour unless the device is specifically engineered to hold analogue intermediate states. They have matured into a workable technology for storage-class memory, and they are increasingly used in in-memory analogue-computing demonstrations.

A fourth family, the spintronic or magnetic resistive device, implements memristive behaviour through the magnetic configuration of a tunnel junction. Its resistance depends on the relative orientation of two ferromagnetic layers. Spintronic devices operate at very high speed and low per-write energy, and they form the basis of commercial spin-transfer-torque magnetic random-access memory. Their main limitations for neuromorphic applications are a comparatively small ON/OFF ratio (often of order two) and a limited range of stable intermediate states. Recent work has nevertheless shown that the intrinsic stochastic dynamics of these devices can itself be turned into a computational resource within appropriately designed architectures.

A fifth family, and the one most relevant here, is that of ionic memristors. Their internal state is the distribution of one or more mobile ionic species inside a solid matrix. Ionic memristors can be subdivided further according to the nature of the host (inorganic oxide, chalcogenide, mixed ionic–electronic conductor, polymer electrolyte) and according to the identity of the mobile species, which ranges from small cations such as Li+, Na+, or Ag+ to protons and a variety of small anions. What sets ionic memristors apart as a class is that the internal state is a chemical configuration of mobile species, not a morphological or magnetic configuration of the host, and that the kinetics of that state are governed by ionic transport and binding within the matrix rather than by electronic processes alone.

Two consequences follow. First, ionic systems are intrinsically history-dependent, because the distribution of mobile species at any given instant reflects the cumulative effect of past electric fields on their migration. Second, ionic systems usually relax spontaneously in the absence of a driving field. The same intermolecular forces and entropic gradients that oppose the non-equilibrium distribution imposed under bias drive the system back towards equilibrium once the bias is gone. Volatility is therefore not a pathology of ionic memristors. It is an intrinsic feature of the underlying physics, and the timescales are set by ionic transport kinetics that can in turn be tuned chemically through the host matrix. This fifth family, and in particular its organic polymer-electrolyte subfamily, is the platform used in the experimental work that follows.

Key Memristive Parameters

The mechanistic heterogeneity surveyed in the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section is reflected in the specialised vocabulary that has grown up around device evaluation. A typical specification report quotes several parameters. The high-resistance state (HRS) and low-resistance state (LRS) define the extremes of the accessible conductance range, and their ratio is the ON/OFF ratio—a figure of merit for the dynamic range of the device. The set and reset voltages specify the magnitude of electrical stimuli needed to program the device between its extreme states, and the associated write energy fixes the per-operation energetic cost. The switching speed describes how rapidly the device responds to a programming pulse. Retention quantifies the time for which a given state is preserved without re-programming. Endurance measures the number of programming cycles the device tolerates before its behaviour degrades. Parameters relevant specifically to analogue operation include the linearity and symmetry of the conductance update under identical positive and negative pulses, and the device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability of all of the above.

These parameters have their natural setting in the evaluation of devices intended for trained-weight storage in analogue in-memory-computing arrays. In that setting the target application is a fixed conductance matrix implementing a trained neural network. Retention has to be long enough to preserve trained weights between re-programming events, typically anywhere from seconds to years depending on the deployment scenario. Endurance has to cover occasional re-training and periodic write–verify cycles. Linearity and symmetry sit at the centre of the numerical accuracy of gradient-based training performed in place on the array, and variability translates directly into a loss of inference accuracy at deployment. A large fraction of the memristor-device literature is written from this standpoint, and it is one of the main contexts in which organic and polymer-composite devices have been judged. They have often come off badly, because their retention times are short, their endurance is limited, and their device-to-device variability is high.

Not every entry in this parameter list is unambiguously good when maximised. The retention case is the one already developed in the In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms section, and applied parameter-by-parameter it extends naturally to switching speed, write energy, linearity, and symmetry: the optima of those quantities differ between the trained-weight and fading-memory regimes, and they cannot in general be satisfied simultaneously by a single device. The design target for a fading-memory device is a retention matched to the signal timescale, not “as long as possible”. The table sets the two optima alongside one another for each parameter.

Variability needs a slightly finer distinction. Intra-device instability (a given device failing to reproduce its own response under the same protocol) remains a defect under either regime. Inter-device heterogeneity, on the other hand, is penalised under weight storage but useful under reservoir operation. A reservoir of elements with a spread of time constants and nonlinear transfer characteristics is more expressive than a set of identical elements, because the spread provides independent nonlinear projections of the input onto different readout axes. The distinction between intra-device instability and inter-device heterogeneity recurs in the evaluation of polymer-composite devices in the later chapters.

Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors

A different family of computational paradigms has grown up over the past decade specifically around volatile, heterogeneous, history-dependent devices. In them a memristor whose state decays on a well-defined timescale is the physical instantiation of a leaky integrator—the elementary dynamical building block of temporal computation. The three paradigms surveyed below are revisited in a later chapter, where the polymer-electrolyte devices are evaluated against the metrics they imply.

The most prominent of the three paradigms is physical reservoir computing, introduced at the conceptual level in the In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms section. In a volatile-memristor reservoir the input signal is encoded as a stream of voltage pulses applied to an array of memristive devices whose internal states evolve under the stimuli and relax between them. At any instant the collective state of the array supplies a nonlinear projection of the recent history of the input onto a high-dimensional feature space, and a linear readout trained by ordinary least squares on that space is enough to solve a broad class of time-varying classification, regression, and prediction tasks. Experimental demonstrations have been reported in inorganic volatile memristors using diffusive metal-filament devices and dynamic oxide memristors, and analogous demonstrations in organic electrochemical systems have begun to appear.

A second paradigm uses volatile memristors as coincidence detectors and temporal-correlation extractors. Because the internal state of such a device relaxes in the absence of a driving pulse, a pulse train whose inter-pulse intervals are short compared with the relaxation timescale produces a progressive accumulation of state. A train whose intervals are long produces essentially no accumulation at all. The integrated response of the device therefore carries direct information about the temporal proximity of successive events, and it can be used to discriminate coincident or near-coincident inputs from temporally dispersed ones. Architectures based on this principle have been proposed, and partially demonstrated, in combination with STDP-like plasticity rules in which the sign and magnitude of the long-term response depend on the relative timing of pre- and post-synaptic events. The approach is in effect a direct hardware transcription of the spike-timing-sensitive plasticity introduced in the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section, and it depends essentially on the relaxation dynamics that the volatile memristor supplies.

A third paradigm uses banks of volatile memristors, engineered to display distinct relaxation time constants, as multi-timescale transient filter banks for streaming signals. In such an architecture a single input is processed in parallel by devices of different intrinsic timescales. Elements with short time constants respond sharply to fast transients and return quickly to baseline. Elements with long time constants respond more gradually and integrate over longer windows. The ensemble of outputs is then read by a downstream classifier to extract onsets, bursts, rhythmic patterns, or slow envelopes that no single time constant could pick out on its own. The chemical tunability of ionic memristors, and especially their ability to span several orders of magnitude in relaxation timescale through modest changes in host composition or mobile-species identity, makes them especially well matched to this architecture.

The performance metrics relevant to these three paradigms differ systematically from the trained-weight metrics catalogued in the Key Memristive Parameters section. For reservoir computing the main figure of merit is the memory capacity of the device ensemble, a quantitative measure of how faithfully the reservoir state reconstructs past inputs at various lag times. To this are added the class separability the reservoir provides on a representative benchmark task and its robustness to the intrinsic variability of the constituent devices.

For coincidence detection and temporal-correlation extraction the relevant metrics are the width and symmetry of the effective timing window, the discrimination margin between coincident and dispersed inputs, and the energy cost per detected event. For multi-timescale filtering the central quantities are the span of accessible time constants across the ensemble, the linearity and isolation of each channel, and the system-level power consumption under realistic input statistics. None of these is meaningfully captured by HRS, LRS, ON/OFF ratio, or retention in isolation. They are task-matched: they reward a device whose physical timescales line up with the temporal structure of the signals it is required to process.

The memristor is at once a concept in circuit theory, a heterogeneous family of physical devices, and a bridge between the synaptic template of the The Biological Synapse as the Engineering Template section and the dynamical computational paradigms set out above. Among the mechanistic families surveyed here, ionic memristors stand out: their internal state is a chemical configuration of mobile species, and relaxation and history dependence are intrinsic to their operation. The next section benchmarks the inorganic memristor families that dominate present-day neuromorphic hardware. It identifies the device requirements they meet well, and the ones that motivate a complementary organic route. Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing

The abstract taxonomy of the Classification of Memristive Mechanisms section can now be narrowed to the three inorganic families that account for most of present-day neuromorphic-hardware activity: metal-oxide resistive memories, phase-change memories, and spin-transfer-torque magnetic tunnel junctions. Each occupies a distinct and technologically powerful region, and together they have firmly established the credibility of memristive hardware as a post-von-Neumann substrate. The specific combination pursued in this thesis (analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on solution-processable soft substrates) lies outside the regions in which these families are strongest, and that mismatch is what motivates a complementary investigation in organic and polymeric materials.

Metal-Oxide Memristors

The broadest and most technologically developed inorganic memristor family operates through resistive switching in transition-metal-oxide thin films. After the TiO2 device of Strukov and co-workers was identified with Chua's memristor concept, research activity shifted quickly onto a wider palette of oxide systems. The best-studied of these are hafnium dioxide (HfO2), tantalum oxide (TaOx), nickel oxide (NiO), tungsten oxide (WOx), and titanium dioxide itself. Many of those materials had been in use for years as gate-dielectric or interconnect-related layers in conventional silicon technology, and integrating them into back-end-of-line processes was therefore immediately feasible. Compatibility with standard CMOS flows is the main practical reason for the prominence of metal-oxide memristors in neuromorphic-hardware demonstrations: arrays of thousands to millions of devices can be fabricated on top of existing addressing electronics without disturbing the underlying silicon, and large-scale integrated demonstrations (including the metal-oxide crossbar of Prezioso et al.) have followed accordingly.

At the device level, metal-oxide memristors are usually classified mechanistically by which mobile species carries the switching behaviour: valence-change memories (VCM) and electrochemical metallisation / conductive-bridge memories (CBRAM). In VCM devices the mobile species is an oxygen vacancy, whose field-driven migration produces local reduction and oxidation of the host cation and, with that, a change in the electronic conductance of a confined filamentary region. In CBRAM devices the mobile species is instead a metal cation originating from an electrochemically active electrode (typically Ag+ or Cu+). The cation migrates across the oxide layer under the applied field and plates out into a metallic filament that shorts the stack.

Both mechanisms commonly require an initial forming step in which a localised dielectric soft breakdown establishes the filament geometry that subsequent set and reset pulses can modulate. Once formed, the device shows either a bistable or, depending on the compliance current and the pulse protocol, a graded multi-state resistance. A third, intermediate class of metal-oxide devices operates instead through non-filamentary interface-type modulation: the conductance of the entire device area is adjusted by a distributed oxygen-vacancy redistribution at a metal–oxide Schottky interface. This third class tends to support finer analogue gradation, at the cost of lower ON/OFF ratios.

The strengths of metal-oxide memristors are essentially those of a mature binary- or few-level non-volatile memory. Retention times of years at room temperature are routinely reported. Switching times can reach the nanosecond range. Endurance can exceed 10^9 cycles in the best-engineered stacks, and densities compatible with sub-10 nm crossbars are in principle achievable. These properties have made metal-oxide resistive memory the canonical platform for commercial and pre-commercial in-memory computing demonstrations, and it is the platform against which most other families are implicitly benchmarked. Volatile subspecies of the same family also exist. Diffusive metal-filament devices, in which the filament spontaneously disrupts by Ag or Cu nanocluster diffusion after the programming bias is removed, have been used explicitly as fading-memory reservoir elements. Metal-oxide memristors are therefore not uniformly non-volatile: the literature supports either persistent or relaxing behaviour through controlled choice of materials, stack geometry, and programming protocol.

The weaknesses of the family for high-precision analogue synaptic operation are equally well documented. Conductance modulation in filamentary devices is intrinsically stochastic. The nucleation of the filament, its lateral position within the active area, and the exact cross-section through which it conducts are all set by thermally and electrically driven fluctuations at the nanoscale. Even in carefully engineered stacks a sizeable device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle spread in set voltage, reset voltage, and achieved conductance remains. The associated non-ideal linearity and asymmetry of the conductance update under identical positive and negative pulses have been repeatedly identified as limiting factors in the numerical accuracy of in-place gradient-based training on metal-oxide crossbars.

Multi-level operation is possible, but in practice is usually limited to a small number of reliably distinguishable states. Getting a high-resolution analogue weight out of a single filamentary cell is still difficult, and the common mitigation strategies fall back on multi-cell unit codes or on hybrid digital–analogue representations. None of this denies the engineering maturity of the metal-oxide platform. It only makes clear that the platform is optimised for retention, endurance, and integration density, and that analogue linearity, symmetric bidirectional update, and fine-grained timescale engineering are secondary concerns within it.

Phase-Change Memory

A conceptually distinct inorganic family operates through reversible switching between the amorphous and crystalline phases of a chalcogenide alloy. The canonical alloy is Ge2Sb2Te5, usually abbreviated GST, and a wide range of closely related compositions (doped variants of GST, Ag-In-Sb-Te, Sb2Te, and so) all show the same essential switching physics. In the crystalline phase the alloy is an electronically conductive narrow-gap semiconductor. In the amorphous phase it is effectively insulating, with a resistance several orders of magnitude higher than the crystalline value. The transition between the two phases is driven thermally. A short, intense reset pulse melts a small volume of the alloy, and is terminated rapidly enough for the melt to vitrify into the amorphous state. A longer, lower-amplitude set pulse holds the material at a temperature above the crystallisation threshold but below the melting point, which lets the disordered region re-crystallise. The resistance, in contrast to that of filamentary metal-oxide memristors, is not the property of a localised channel; it is set by the geometric proportion of crystalline and amorphous phases within the programmed volume.

The technological maturity of phase-change memory is unusually high among memristive device families. It has been developed continuously as a storage-class memory since the 1970s, has run through several commercial product generations, and over the past decade has become the most credible inorganic platform for analogue in-memory computing. Le Gallo and co-workers demonstrated mixed-precision in-memory arithmetic on PCM arrays with sufficient accuracy to support, jointly with a digital co-processor, the solution of linear systems of practical scale. Ambrogio et al. later demonstrated equivalent-accuracy training of modest-scale neural networks using PCM elements as the long-term weight store, paired with a volatile capacitor for fine-grained weight updates. Taken together, these results put PCM forward as the most seriously deployed analogue-memristive substrate for conventional deep-learning workloads, and as the benchmark against which any alternative analogue weight element has to be evaluated for trained-weight applications.

The constraints of phase-change memory matter when the device is viewed as a candidate synapse rather than as a weight cell. The reset operation requires local melting of the active volume, and the associated current densities and thermal loads impose a high per-write energy together with stringent thermal isolation of neighbouring cells. The amorphous phase produced by quenching is not perfectly stable either. Its resistance drifts over time, roughly as a power-law relaxation of the amorphous structure, typically a few per cent of the baseline per decade of time, which has to be compensated by periodic re-reading or by read-time calibration.

Precise analogue programming of intermediate resistance states relies on carefully shaped pulse protocols and on tight thermal control, and is for that reason more sensitive to process and operating-condition variation than the corresponding bistable operation. The underlying physics is thermal and structural, which leaves little room for chemical tuning of the key timescales. The crystallisation and amorphisation kinetics are set by the alloy composition and by the device geometry; both are adjustable within modest bounds, but neither offers the molecular-level handle on ion transport that the soft-matter systems discussed in the next section provide. PCM is the most compelling analogue in-memory-computing platform currently available, yet its strength lies in the high-retention, high-endurance, trained-weight corner, not in the fast, soft, chemically tunable, fading-memory corner pursued here.

Spin-Transfer-Torque Magnetic Tunnel Junctions

The third inorganic memristor family departs more sharply from the ion- or atom-rearrangement mechanisms discussed so far. A magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) is built from two ferromagnetic layers separated by a thin insulating tunnel barrier (typically MgO). The resistance of the junction depends, through the tunnelling magnetoresistance (TMR) effect, on the relative orientation of the magnetisations of the two layers: low when the magnetisations are parallel, high when they are antiparallel. In a spin-transfer-torque (STT) device the free layer can be switched between parallel and antiparallel configurations by a spin-polarised tunnel current. Conduction electrons traversing the barrier from the fixed reference layer acquire a net spin polarisation, and their interaction with the free-layer moment then exerts a torque that, above a threshold current density, is enough to reverse it. Read and write operations therefore share the same electrical port, and the internal state variable is the magnetic configuration of the free layer rather than any chemical or morphological property of the host material.

The STT–MTJ family is of real interest for neuromorphic and unconventional computing for several reasons. Switching times in the nanosecond range, endurance exceeding 10^12 cycles, write energies per bit in the femtojoule range, and compatibility with back-end-of-line CMOS integration together make the device attractive as a low-energy non-volatile element. The magnetic origin of the switching threshold, combined with thermal fluctuations of the free layer in devices close to the superparamagnetic limit, also offers a natural and well-controlled source of stochasticity. That stochasticity has been put to use in probabilistic-computing and neuromorphic demonstrations: Torrejon et al. used spin-torque nano-oscillators as dynamical nodes for physical reservoir computing and reached competitive accuracy on a spoken-digit classification benchmark, while Mizrahi et al. used populations of superparamagnetic MTJs as noisy basis functions for neural-style computation. These results show that the MTJ family is not restricted to digital memory operation, and that its dynamical behaviour can itself be a computational resource.

The limitations of the platform are real once the problem is fine-grained analogue synaptic operation. A simple STT–MTJ is essentially a two-state device, with the ratio of antiparallel to parallel resistance typically in the range from two to a few. Extending the ratio, and adding a larger number of reliably programmable intermediate states, requires more elaborate stack engineering—synthetic antiferromagnetic references, perpendicular-anisotropy stacks, or multi-layer configurations—and tighter process control, with corresponding penalties in yield and integration complexity.

The thermal stability of the free layer (which sets the retention) is in direct tension with the switching current (which sets the write energy), and trading one against the other leaves only a tight envelope within which the device can operate. The materials involved are demanding too: carefully engineered multilayers of ferromagnetic metals, synthetic antiferromagnets, and crystalline MgO tunnel barriers, none of which lend themselves to the solution-processable, substrate-agnostic fabrication available to soft-matter devices. STT–MTJs are best thought, then, as fast, non-volatile, few-state, stochastic elements whose strengths lie in probabilistic computing and in the replacement of small embedded non-volatile memories, rather than as a natural substrate for analogue, multi-timescale synaptic emulation.

General Limitations of Inorganic Memristors

The three families surveyed above differ in mechanism and in their preferred operating regimes, but they share a handful of features that limit their coverage of the neuromorphic hardware problem and that motivate a complementary materials investigation.

The first shared constraint is a limited range of chemical tunability. Each of the three families is anchored in a small number of canonical host materials—the transition-metal oxides, the tellurium-based chalcogenide alloys, and a restricted set of engineered ferromagnetic stacks—and the switching behaviour is determined by the intrinsic physics of those hosts. The design knobs available inside each family are mostly geometric (layer thicknesses, active area, electrode contacts), stoichiometric (within narrow compositional windows), or process-related (annealing conditions, forming protocols). Access to the switching physics itself is mediated, in practice, through a small handful of well-characterised stacks. It is rarely possible to modify the key energetic or kinetic parameters of the device by substituting one mobile species for another with different binding chemistry, or by swapping the host polymer for a chemically distinct analogue with a different glass-transition temperature. This is a statement about the structure of the materials space rather than a criticism of any individual demonstration; molecular-level tunability of the binding and transport chemistry is not among the handles that the inorganic platforms expose.

A second and closely related constraint concerns dynamical timescale engineering. Each of the three inorganic families admits both non-volatile and, in specific implementations, volatile behaviour. Diffusive metal-filament devices in the metal-oxide family supply fading-memory dynamics. Resistance drift in the amorphous phase of PCM provides a (less well-controlled) relaxation. Superparamagnetic MTJs introduce stochastic fluctuations on engineered timescales. What is harder in each case is to systematically sweep a relaxation time constant over several orders of magnitude within a single materials platform by means of a single chemical variable. The underlying kinetic processes (vacancy drift in a crystalline oxide, thermally activated crystallisation in a chalcogenide melt, magnetic relaxation of a ferromagnetic free layer) are set by the intrinsic energy landscape of the host, and the span of accessible timescales inside each family is constrained accordingly. For applications such as the heterogeneous reservoir or the multi-timescale transient filter bank described in the Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors section, where parallel devices with deliberately distinct time constants are required, this constraint is not peripheral but central.

A third constraint concerns fabrication and integration. The inorganic families above rely, with few exceptions, on vacuum-based thin-film deposition, on high-temperature anneals, and on top-down lithographic patterning. These workflows are fully compatible with conventional silicon technology, which is an unambiguous strength for dense crossbar arrays integrated on CMOS, but they constrain the substrates on which devices can be fabricated and the operating temperatures at which they can be processed. Large-area, flexible, biocompatible, or printable platforms (relevant for soft-electronic, implantable, or distributed-sensor neuromorphic systems) are not the natural home of these workflows, and adapting them to such contexts requires substantial process engineering. Solution-processable active layers, by contrast, can in principle be deposited on arbitrary substrates at mild temperatures, and their active chemistry can be tuned by varying the composition of the precursor solution. This is a feature of the fabrication physics, not a deficiency of any specific inorganic demonstration, but it matters for the applications considered in the later chapters.

A fourth constraint is the mechanistic stochasticity inherent to filamentary and phase-change operation. In reservoir computing, stochasticity can usefully contribute to reservoir diversity, provided it shows up as inter-device heterogeneity rather than as intra-device instability (in the sense of the Key Memristive Parameters section). The inorganic families above tend to display both at once, and pulling them apart requires considerable process control. A device class in which inter-device heterogeneity can be introduced deliberately through composition, with intra-device stability preserved by the collective character of the conduction mechanism, would offer a cleaner route into the controlled-variability operating regime.

Taken together, these constraints point at a gap. Analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on soft, solution-processable platforms is simply not where the inorganic families are strongest. Organic Materials for Memristive Applications

The inorganic memristor families validate the broader memristive and neuromorphic programme, and each of them is strong in some part of the problem. What they share is a restricted palette of chemical handles and a workflow best suited to rigid, vacuum-processed, high-temperature-compatible substrates. Analogue multi-state operation, ion-mediated internal dynamics, controllable timescales spanning several orders of magnitude, and compatibility with soft, conformable, or biointerfaced form factors all sit less comfortably in that materials space.

Organic materials become attractive precisely when that combination is required. The point is not that organic devices are automatically cheaper, cleaner, or more reproducible than their inorganic counterparts; such claims are often unsupported, or at best context-dependent. The relevant fact is narrower. In organic and polymeric systems the chemistry that sets electronic transport, ionic transport, redox activity, and relaxation kinetics is directly accessible to molecular design. Tuning these properties is then a question of synthesis and formulation rather than of repeated redesign of a rigid thin-film stack.

The Advantages of Organic Memristors section identifies the features that distinguish organic materials from inorganic platforms for memristive and neuromorphic applications, and is careful about what those features do and do not guarantee. The Mechanisms in Organic Memristors section then surveys the main physical mechanisms invoked to explain organic memristive behaviour, makes clear that they do not amount to a single mechanism class, and gives particular weight to ion-mediated and mixed ionic–electronic operation—the route most relevant to the rest of the thesis.

Advantages of Organic Memristors

The distinctive feature of organic materials, in the context of memristive and neuromorphic device engineering, is the breadth of chemical freedom available at the level of the active layer. A semiconducting polymer can be designed through choice of backbone, side-chain functionalisation, and molecular weight distribution. An ion-transport host can be tuned through choice of chelating motif, polymer topology, and glass-transition temperature. A redox-active moiety can be picked from a large library of molecular and polymeric species with well-characterised oxidation potentials and known redox reversibility. A mobile ionic species can be chosen independently of the host on the basis of charge, radius, and Lewis acidity.

The relevant point is not that any individual organic device is automatically superior to an inorganic equivalent, but that the number of orthogonal chemical handles available for engineering its internal-state physics is considerably larger. In particular, the ionic component of the active layer (the identity of the mobile cation and anion, the nature of the solvating environment, and the segmental dynamics of the host polymer) is directly under synthetic and formulation control in a way that is not available at the equivalent level of granularity in the inorganic platforms discussed in the Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing section.

Solution processability is the second distinguishing feature. A large fraction of organic active layers relevant for memristive operation—conjugated polymers, hyperbranched polyethers, polyelectrolytes, redox-active polymers, and their blends—can be deposited from solution by techniques ranging from spin coating and blade coating in the laboratory to inkjet, spray, and roll-to-roll processes over larger areas. This is a feature of the fabrication physics, not a promise of low cost or industrial scalability by itself, and its practical benefits depend on the material and solvent system used. What it does guarantee is that the composition of the active layer can be varied continuously by mixing precursor solutions in a single step, and that suits composite devices in which an electronic component and an ionic component coexist. Adjusting polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor ratios by changing the recipe, rather than by redesigning a multilayer stack, is the fabrication-level counterpart of the chemical freedom described above.

A closely related feature is the mild thermal budget required for processing. Most organic memristive layers are fabricated at temperatures below one hundred and fifty degrees Celsius—often below one hundred—and the top contacts can be deposited through shadow masks by thermal evaporation without high-temperature post-anneals. The fabrication is compatible with flexible plastic substrates, with paper, with textiles, with elastomers, and even with biological tissues, and it places no strict lower bound on substrate thickness beyond the mechanical requirements of handling. The mechanical properties of the finished device (flexibility under bending, conformability over curved surfaces, and in some cases stretchability) follow naturally from this processing regime rather than from any intrinsic mechanical property of a single active molecule. Demonstrations of flexible and stretchable organic synaptic devices make the point that mechanical compliance is accessible at the device level with standard organic-electronic fabrication, and that compliance matters for the wearable, implantable, or conformal neuromorphic systems envisaged under the nervetronic heading.

Chemical freedom, solution processability, and a mild thermal budget together define a materials space different from the inorganic one. Low cost, reproducibility, biocompatibility, and long lifetime still depend on the specific materials, solvents, contact metals, encapsulation, and operating conditions, and that dependence is not removed by working in organic chemistry. The claim made here is narrower: organic materials expose handles on the internal-state physics of the device that the inorganic class does not, and those handles operate through chemistry rather than through re-engineering of a rigid thin-film stack.

Two consequences matter for the argument developed in the The Memristor: Theory and History and Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing sections. First, organic systems can support finely tunable analogue responses, because the microscopic mechanisms behind the switching behaviour (charge trapping on molecular sites, redox activity of a macromolecular host, displacement of mobile ions in a polymer matrix) are typically distributed over many sites and therefore admit intermediate states rather than a sharp binary transition.

Second, device relaxation timescales can be engineered through chemistry. Ion transport in a polymer electrolyte depends on the binding strength between the mobile ion and the polymer, on the segmental mobility of the host, and on the concentration of mobile species. Each of these parameters is a design variable in an organic composite. Device timescales running from milliseconds to many seconds, matched to biological short-term plasticity and to slow temporal signals, can therefore be reached within one materials family through compositional changes alone—which is the regime the paradigms of the Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors section reward.

Each demonstration in the literature occupies a particular point in this materials space, with its own strengths and its own compromises. The history of the field shows that combining reversibility, endurance, multi-state analogue control, and interpretable ionic physics in a single two-terminal organic device remained difficult well into the last decade. The argument here is not that organic memristors solve every problem left open by their inorganic counterparts, but that ion-mediated polymer composites give access to chemical controls that are not available on the inorganic side.

Mechanisms in Organic Memristors

Unlike phase-change memory or valence-change memory, the label organic memristor does not designate a single mechanism class. Memristive behaviour in organic devices has been credibly attributed to several distinct physical processes, and these differ in the internal state variable that is modulated, in reversibility, in timescales, and in the degree of analogue control achievable. This subsection identifies the main families, indicates what each one offers and what it costs, and places the ion-mediated family among them.

The first mechanism family is charge trapping and detrapping in an insulating or weakly conducting matrix. The active layer in this family contains a dispersed population of deep or shallow traps—associated with molecular defect sites, with embedded nanoparticles, or with the interfaces between incompatible polymer phases—and the device state is set by the occupation of those traps under an applied bias. The resistance change comes from the modulation of electronic transport through the matrix as the local space-charge environment is altered by the trapped carriers.

This family is attractive because trapping is a generic process readily observed in disordered organic semiconductors, and because switching voltages can be kept modest. The downside is limited analogue control. The state space is often effectively binary; the dependence of conductance on trap occupation is non-monotonic in some devices; and the microscopic identity of the traps themselves is frequently hard to pin down, which limits how interpretable the mechanism really is. Retention is set by the depth of the traps relative to the thermal energy, and that depth is difficult to control by composition alone.

The second family is redox switching. Here the active layer, or one component of it, contains a species whose oxidation state can be changed reversibly under bias. The redox-active component can be a discrete molecule embedded in a polymer host, a redox-active polymer that is itself the active layer, or a redox-active moiety covalently bound to a conjugated backbone. The device state is set by the distribution of oxidised and reduced species, which alters the local electronic structure and so the conductance. Redox switching offers a clean mechanistic picture: the internal state variable is the oxidation state of a well-defined chemical species, the electrochemical potentials that drive the transitions are experimentally accessible, and in favourable cases the redox process is reversible over many cycles. Several important organic memristors operate on this principle, including redox-active conjugated polymer systems and early electrochemical demonstrations based on coordination complexes.

The costs, in the organic context, are twofold. First, not every redox process is cleanly reversible. Side reactions, dimerisation, and slow degradation of the active species all limit the endurance of many systems. Second, purely redox-based devices tend to operate as bistable or few-state systems, unless the redox response is distributed over an ensemble of sites. When distributed, the analogue response is recovered, but interpretability begins to slide from the chemistry of an individual switch towards the statistics of the ensemble.

The third family is conformational or structural switching. The active layer contains a molecular species whose geometry, isomerisation state, or aggregation state can be modulated by an applied field, by local heating, or by coupled electrochemical processes. Classical examples include photochromic or electrochromic switches that couple to electrical transport, and bistable molecular architectures designed specifically for the purpose, in which the conductance of the molecule depends on its conformation. Such systems offer very high molecular design control, and in favourable cases very high precision in the switching behaviour, since the state is set by a chemically well-defined geometric rearrangement. The costs are real: the relevant molecular synthesis is frequently demanding, the switching dynamics are tied to the kinetics of one specific molecular rearrangement which may or may not match the timescales of interest, and scaling the behaviour from single-molecule to device-level averages introduces its own set of issues.

The fourth family, and the one most directly relevant to this thesis, is ionic redistribution in mixed ionic–electronic systems. The active layer contains both an electronic conductor (typically a conjugated polymer) and a population of mobile ions carried by a suitable ionic host. Under applied bias the mobile ions migrate and accumulate either near the electrode interfaces or within specific regions of the active layer, and in doing so they modify the local electronic structure of the semiconductor through a combination of interfacial doping, electrostatic band bending, and electrochemical reduction or oxidation of the polymer. The resulting conductance change is continuous, multi-state, and tied directly to the amount and spatial distribution of displaced ionic charge.

The microscopic picture is closely related to the operation of light-emitting electrochemical cells, in which ionic redistribution under a driving bias creates an in-situ doped junction, and to the operation of organic electrochemical transistors (OECTs), in which a gate-driven ion exchange between an electrolyte and a conducting polymer modulates the channel current. What sets the memristive mode apart from its LEC and OECT counterparts is that in a two-terminal memristive device the driving bias itself plays the role of the gate, and the residual state of the ionic distribution after the bias is removed carries the memory.

This fourth family is central to the thesis. The state variable is easy to name (the position of mobile ions in a polymer host), and its dynamics are governed by ionic transport kinetics that depend on the polymer binding environment and on the ion chemistry. When the bias is removed, relaxation is a fading-memory process whose characteristic time constant can be estimated from the physical chemistry of the composite and engineered through cation identity, host polymer, salt concentration, and film morphology. These variables are directly accessible to the materials chemist, in a way that the trap depths or redox overpotentials controlling relaxation in the charge-trapping and redox families are not. The costs are that ionic motion is slower than the purely electronic processes underlying filamentary or charge-trapping switching, and that side reactions between mobile ions and electrodes have to be controlled through chemistry and contact-metal choice.

A fifth family, included for completeness, is filamentary conduction in which metallic species (typically Ag+ or Cu+ from an active top electrode) migrate through the organic matrix to form conductive bridges analogous to those of inorganic CBRAM. Such devices inherit the strengths (large on/off ratios, low switching voltages) and the weaknesses (stochastic filament formation, intra-device instability) of their inorganic counterparts. They illustrate that the same mechanism can be realised on either side of the organic–inorganic boundary; the distinctively organic mechanisms are those in which the chemical specificity of the components contributes to the internal-state physics.

The five families and their relative trade-offs are collected in the table.

Among the organic options, ion-mediated mixed ionic–electronic systems couple an identifiable internal state variable to kinetics that can be engineered through chemistry. Within that subset, polymeric ionic hosts (polymer electrolytes) add a useful feature: ionic transport kinetics are controlled by host segmental motion, and segmental motion can in turn be tuned through backbone choice and glass-transition temperature. Polymer-electrolyte composites, in which a conjugated semiconductor is blended with an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host loaded with a dissociated salt, are for that reason developed in detail in the next section.

Historical Development of Organic Memristors

The organic memristor literature of the last three decades is not a single device lineage. It is a set of parallel streams rooted in different mechanisms and different architectures. The useful question to ask of it is which problems those streams solved, and which combinations of features were still missing at the start of the work reported here.

An early strand repurposed the slow, history-dependent ionic redistribution of light-emitting electrochemical cells as a memristive functionality. Zakhidov and co-workers reported a light-emitting memristor in which an ionic [Ru(bpy)3]^2+ complex supplied both the emissive species and the mobile ionic component. Its significance was mechanistic rather than practical: it identified ionic redistribution in a polymer host as a legitimate route to memristive behaviour, even if the device itself was not a viable memory element. A parallel strand reported resistive switching in simple polymer thin films such as poly(vinyl alcohol). That work established that memristive phenomena were accessible in inexpensive polymer matrices, but the resulting devices were typically binary, mechanistically opaque, and not analogue.

A more productive strand adopted the organic electrochemical transistor as the architectural platform for synaptic emulation, drawing on prior bioelectronics investment in OECT design. Gkoupidenis and colleagues showed that PEDOT:PSS OECTs could reproduce short-term plasticity, paired-pulse facilitation, and long-term potentiation and depression under appropriate pulse protocols, and later extended this to devices with global electrolyte connectivity. Xu and colleagues produced core–sheath nanowire synaptic transistors. Van de Burgt, Fuller, and co-workers developed the non-volatile organic electrochemical neuromorphic device (ENODe), in which a dedicated non-volatile reservoir supplies a linearly updateable synaptic weight. Together these devices showed that organic electrochemistry could support the full range of synaptic learning rules at low operating voltages and with good analogue control, and they made clear the central role of ionic exchange between a polymer electrolyte and a conducting polymer channel. OECT devices with halide-perovskite gates extended the range of accessible timescales.

The three-terminal line is intellectually important but architecturally distinct. An OECT synaptic device consists of a gated channel, a separate electrolyte, and, in the non-volatile variant, a dedicated programming reservoir. Read and write are cleanly decoupled, at the price that each synaptic element carries at least a third terminal and a contacted electrolyte volume. A simpler two-terminal element whose ionic physics is contained within the active layer is therefore still an attractive complement, especially for the heterogeneous reservoir and coincidence-detection paradigms of the Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors section.

The two-terminal line of organic memristor work ran in parallel. Liu and co-workers reported a biomimicking redox organic memristor in which an organic redox system embedded in a polymer matrix produced, in a passive two-terminal device, a short-term-to-long-term memory transition under repeated stimulation, with coexisting short-term and long-term regimes. The same work illustrated the characteristic difficulty of the redox-driven route. Redox processes are not always cleanly reversible, and the long-term regime sometimes reflects partially irreversible transformations rather than controlled state evolution. How to obtain analogue, reversible, multi-state behaviour in a two-terminal organic device without relying on permanent redox changes was, at the time, an open problem.

Two further lines should be noted. Stretchable and mechanically compliant organic synaptic devices (Kim and co-workers' bioinspired flexible artificial afferent nerve; Park and co-workers' review of nervetronic applications) showed that the mechanical advantages of organic processing can be realised at the device level, and framed the conformable, skin-mounted, or biointerfaced applications in which soft-matter devices are well suited. Molecular memristors with engineered redox and conformational behaviour, especially Goswami and co-workers' metal-coordinated azo-aromatic layers with high on/off contrast, robust endurance, and near-analogue multi-state operation, showed what molecular-level design can achieve. The cost is a demanding synthesis that places those devices in a different practical regime from the composite devices considered below.

Hybrid systems also sit alongside these mechanism families. Perovskite–organic hybrids exploit the efficient ionic conduction of halide perovskites together with organic charge-transport layers; conjugated polymer systems with non-volatile resistive-switching character have been explored; and organic electrochemical reservoir computing has shown that mixed ionic–electronic organic devices can act as physical-reservoir elements without losing their volatility. The last result is especially relevant here, because it treats volatility as a useful feature rather than as a defect.

By the early 2020s organic neuromorphic electronics had become a recognised subfield with several active design lines. Ion-mediated mechanisms had been identified in both three-terminal and two-terminal architectures. Analogue, linear, symmetric programming had been demonstrated in the electrochemically gated family. Short-term-to-long-term memory transitions had been demonstrated in two-terminal redox devices. Mechanical compliance had been demonstrated in stretchable synaptic devices, and molecular-level control had been demonstrated in engineered molecular memristors.

What the record did not show was the consolidation of these features into a single two-terminal platform. The three-terminal electrochemical family achieved reversibility and analogue control at the cost of architectural complexity. The two-terminal redox family achieved architectural simplicity at the cost of full reversibility. The molecular family achieved precision at the cost of synthetic tractability. The stretchable family demonstrated mechanical compatibility without committing to a particular mechanism. The gap addressed in this thesis is a two-terminal, reversible, multi-state, ion-mediated device with interpretable chemistry, built from standard organic-electronic components. The route pursued below is a composite of a conjugated semiconducting polymer, an ion-conducting polymer host, and a dissociated alkali-metal salt. Polymer Electrolyte Composites: The Enabling Strategy

The Organic Materials for Memristive Applications section narrowed the field to devices in which ionic redistribution in a polymer host couples to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor, and chose the two-terminal route over the three-terminal electrochemical-transistor one at the end of the Historical Development of Organic Memristors section. The remaining decision is what goes inside the active layer of that two-terminal stack. The strategy adopted here is a composite: a semiconducting conjugated polymer blended with an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host and a dissociated alkali-metal salt, all processed from a common solution onto a standard two-terminal electrode stack.

The case for this strategy has three parts. The Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section recalls the physical chemistry of ion-conducting polymers: oxygen-rich hosts, alkali-cation coordination, transport mechanisms, and composition. The Light-Emitting Electrochemical Cells as Precedent section then brings in polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells as a precedent for stable, reversible, field-driven coupling between ionic migration and electronic transport in a soft organic active layer. Finally, the Rationale for the Composite Approach section gives the rationale for the composite approach: separating electronic and ionic functions, treating the salt as an independent variable, using cation identity and composition as timescale controls, and building a family of chemically distinct composites that can act as a temporal-kernel bank rather than as competing memories.

Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles

An ion-conducting polymer, in the sense used here, is a macromolecular host in which dissolved or dispersed ionic species are mobile under an applied electric field without need of a liquid solvent phase. The field was opened in the late 1970s and early 1980s by the recognition that alkali-metal salts could be dissolved in poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) to form solid complexes that conduct ions at macroscopically measurable rates. Solid polymer electrolytes were developed mainly for lithium batteries. The principles that matter here are not the battery ones but the ones that make polymer electrolytes tunable memristive media: cation coordination, segmental motion, host crystallinity, and composition.

The central chemical feature of a polyether host is the presence of a high density of ether oxygens along the polymer backbone or its side chains. In PEO itself the oxygen atoms are spaced at regular intervals along a -\!CH_2\!-\!CH_2\!-\!O\!- repeat unit, and the resulting linear backbone has a conformation, in the amorphous phase, that lets each cation coordinate simultaneously to several ether oxygens drawn from one or more polymer chains. In the hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts of the Hybrane family, the oxygen density is supplied differently: by a combination of ether and ester side-chain groups grafted onto a branched polymer core.

In both classes of host the relevant chemical role of the oxygen atoms is the same. They act as hard Lewis bases that solvate alkali cations, stabilise the dissociated state of the salt, and define the local binding environment in which the cation sits. The anion is held more weakly, primarily by electrostatic compensation of the cation, and tends to be more mobile than the cation within the same host, especially when it is a soft, charge-delocalised species such as triflate (CF3SO3-) or bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TFSI, N(SO2CF3)2-).

Ion transport in such hosts is most naturally described as a combination of three microscopically distinct mechanisms. In the vehicular picture, a cation together with its primary solvation shell of coordinated oxygens moves through the polymer matrix as an effective ionic vehicle, and long-range transport requires the coordinated oxygens themselves to move with it. Ionic transport is then coupled to polymer motion. In the hopping or site-to-site picture, the cation is transferred between nominally equivalent coordination sites on the host by a local rearrangement of oxygens that does not require macroscopic transport of the host. This process is closely related to the Grotthuss-type description familiar from protonic conductors, even though the chemistry of the transfer event is different.

In the segmental-motion-assisted picture, the reorganisation of short polymer segments around the cation (segmental motion of the backbone or of the side chain) creates and destroys coordination sites near the ion in such a way that the effective diffusion of the ion is enabled by the local dynamics of the host. These three pictures are not mutually exclusive. In a realistic polyether host transport is a hybrid of all three, and their relative weights depend on host chemistry, cation, salt concentration, and temperature. For the devices studied here, what matters is the broader point: ionic transport is set by the coupling between polymer dynamics and ionic chemistry, not by polymer chemistry alone.

This coupling has a direct consequence for device dynamics. The macroscopic ionic mobility in a polymer electrolyte is, to a first approximation, controlled by the segmental mobility of the host. That in turn is controlled by the glass transition temperature T_g and by the degree to which the host stays amorphous under operating conditions. Below T_g segmental motion is effectively frozen and ionic conduction collapses. Above T_g segmental motion is activated and ionic transport becomes measurable, with a characteristic Vogel–Tamman–Fulcher (rather than Arrhenius) temperature dependence in many systems.

Crystalline regions, where they exist, contribute little to conduction and may even trap ions in ordered environments. For that reason, low-crystallinity or fully amorphous hosts are preferred for ion-transport applications. Engineering the host for a low T_g and a low crystalline fraction is a recognised design strategy in solid polymer electrolytes, pursued through copolymerisation, branching, and plasticisation. Hyperbranched architectures such as the Hybrane family are partly motivated by the suppression of crystallinity that branching produces. For memristive operation at room temperature, the host should stay amorphous and segmentally mobile under normal ambient conditions, and those properties should ideally be accessible through synthetic design at the host level rather than through external plasticisers.

The strength of the interaction between a cation and the oxygen donor atoms of the host depends on the chemical identity of the cation, and this is where hard–soft acid–base (HSAB) reasoning becomes a useful intermediate-level framing. The ether and ester oxygens of polyether and polyester-amide hosts are hard Lewis bases: their donor electron pairs are localised, their polarisability is low, and the coordination bonds they form with cations are accordingly dominated by electrostatic rather than covalent contributions. Among alkali cations, Li+ is the hardest Lewis acid (small radius, high charge density, highly localised charge), and so it interacts most strongly with a hard-base oxygen donor. Na+ and K+ are progressively softer along that sequence, with larger radii, lower charge densities, and weaker electrostatic coupling to a given oxygen environment.

The practical consequence is that within a common polyether host at fixed composition, the characteristic residence time of the cation at a given coordination site (and therefore the kinetics of its displacement under an applied bias) is expected to follow the ordering Li+ > Na+ > K+ in coordination strength. In dynamical terms that ordering is expected to map onto a corresponding ordering of the fading-memory timescale of the device, with harder cations giving slower relaxation and softer cations faster relaxation. The HSAB framing is used here as a qualitative organising principle. It anticipates a ranking of timescales without fixing quantitative values; the quantitative confirmation of the ordering against real measurements is left for the experimental chapters.

Composition provides the second independent family of design knobs. A polymer electrolyte is characterised not only by its host chemistry and cation identity, but also by the ratio of salt to host, and, when it sits inside a larger composite, by the ratio of polymer electrolyte to semiconducting phase. These ratios control, respectively, the concentration of mobile ionic species available for redistribution under a bias, and the effective volume fraction of the electronic and the ionic phase within the active layer.

The two ratios are not redundant. The salt-to-host ratio sets the number and the spatial density of occupied cation coordination sites, while the host-to-semiconductor ratio sets the volume available for ionic transport and for electronic percolation through the conjugated phase. In the composite devices considered in later chapters the polymer-to-semiconductor mass fraction and the salt-to-semiconductor mass fraction are treated as two independent axes of composition, and their effects on the dynamical response of the device are distinguished experimentally. Composition is, in other words, a real physical-chemistry knob on the ionic dynamics, not a parameter selected for convenience.

These considerations motivate the specific host choices made in the thesis. Hybrane DEO750 8500, a hyperbranched polyester-amide with a high density of ether and ester oxygens on its side chains, offers an amorphous, low-crystallinity host that has already been validated in the same material family used in the chapter and in its associated light-emitting precedent. It serves here as the ion-transport host in the proof-of-concept Super Yellow (SY)/Hybrane/lithium triflate (LiOTf, LiCF3SO3) composite, where it is paired with a triflate salt to demonstrate the full set of memristive functionalities in a single device.

PEO is introduced as the central polyether host for the broader comparative corpus explored later in the thesis, where it plays a chemically analogous role: its ether oxygens coordinate alkali-metal cations, its segmental motion enables ionic transport, and its compositional and cation-identity dependencies will be varied systematically. The aim is not to argue that one host is superior to the other but to use them for complementary purposes. Hybrane is the host in which the full synaptic functionality is established on a single exemplar; PEO is the host in which composition- and cation-driven dynamics are explored across a broader comparative set. The physical-chemistry principles developed in this subsection apply to both.

Light-Emitting Electrochemical Cells as Precedent

The argument developed so far rests on a physical claim: ionic redistribution in an organic polymer host can be coupled to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor in a way that is reversible, stable, and reproducible. Several decades of work on polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs) support that claim. In LECs the same coupling is used for electroluminescence rather than memory, and the relevant physics has been characterised in detail. LECs are invoked here as a material-level precedent, not because the thesis is concerned with light emission.

An LEC in its canonical form is a two-terminal sandwich device in which a conjugated light-emitting polymer is blended, from a single solution, with an ion-conducting polymer and a dissociated salt. Under an applied bias the ions in the host redistribute. Cations drift toward the cathode and anions toward the anode, producing regions of electrochemically doped polymer near each contact. The doped regions supply electrons and holes respectively to the undoped bulk, and the resulting p-i-n-like profile develops dynamically in response to the applied bias. The intrinsic region between the two doped fronts is where electron–hole recombination occurs, and where the electroluminescence is generated.

Microscopically, the operation of an LEC has been the subject of a long-running debate between an electrochemical-doping picture, in which the doping of the polymer is the dominant effect, and an electrodynamic picture, in which the accumulation of ions at the interfaces acts primarily to reduce charge-injection barriers. Detailed modelling and experimental studies clarified the conditions under which each picture dominates, and a unifying framework later showed that the two views are not contradictory; they correspond to different injection regimes. The electrodynamic picture applies when charge injection is barrier-limited and ionic accumulation restructures the interfaces. The electrochemical-doping picture applies when injection is ohmic and the doped regions themselves dominate the transport. Both involve the same underlying ionic redistribution in the polymer host. They differ in which consequence of that redistribution controls the measurable electrical behaviour. Complementary studies of electrochemical side reactions have identified the chemical routes by which LECs degrade, and have informed strategies to suppress them.

Two aspects of this precedent are directly relevant to the thesis. The first is that LEC operation demonstrates, across several orders of magnitude of applied bias and over operational lifetimes that can reach hundreds or thousands of hours, that ionic redistribution in a polymer host coexists stably with electronic transport in a conjugated polymer phase. The ions do not, in general, undergo irreversible side reactions that destroy the device within a few cycles. The conjugated polymer does not, in general, suffer irreversible electrochemical degradation under the normal operating window. And the interfaces between the polymer composite and the metallic contacts can be engineered, through choice of metal and of operating protocol, to support repeated injection and withdrawal of both charge and coordinated ions.

These empirical findings establish the feasibility of stable mixed ionic–electronic composite operation. For a memristive device built on the same composite design they give a simple plausibility argument: if ionic redistribution in a polymer host is stable under LEC operating conditions, then nothing in the chemistry rules out using it as the internal state variable of a two-terminal memristive device.

The second relevant aspect is the role of the solid electrolyte. In both LECs and memristive composites the electrolyte is not the electroluminescent or the electronically conducting component. Its contribution comes through the redistribution of mobile ions under bias. The difference lies in how that redistribution is used. In an LEC the driving bias is held during operation so that the doped p-i-n profile is maintained and emission is sustained. In a memristive device the bias sets an internal ionic distribution, and what remains of that distribution after the bias is removed carries the memory of the stimulation. The electrolyte chemistry, its coupling to the conjugated host, and its stability under field-driven operation are governed by the same principles in both cases. The LEC literature is therefore a well-characterised testbed for the material chemistry used here.

The strongest precedent is the specific case of LECs built on the same material family used for the proof-of-concept device in the chapter. Mardegan and co-workers reported that hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts of the Hybrane family, blended with a conjugated light-emitting polymer, support LEC operation with long operational lifetimes and well-controlled electroluminescence. That result validates the same semiconducting-polymer / ion-conducting-host / salt composite architecture later used here for a memristive device: constituent polymers, solvent system, processing protocol, and operating conditions are directly comparable. Because the Chapter 2 device is built on the same Hybrane-class host, the feasibility of reversible field-driven ionic redistribution in that host class is carried over from established LEC materials knowledge into a new device function.

The LEC precedent establishes that stable coupling between ionic and electronic functions is achievable in an organic composite. It does not, by itself, guarantee the synaptic behaviour required of a memristive device: analogue multi-state programming, short- and long-term memory regimes, and clean relaxation kinetics. The chapter tests that question for the SY/Hybrane/LiOTf system, and the comparative chapter then extends it to a PEO-based host across compositions and cations, without assuming that every chemically plausible combination will reproduce the full Chapter 2 behaviour.

Rationale for the Composite Approach

The composite already announced in the section preamble can now be motivated directly. The advantage of refusing to ask a single material to supply electronic conduction, ionic transport, and controlled coupling all at once is that one engineering problem becomes three chemistry problems—one per component. Each can be drawn from its own well-stocked chemical series, and each can be varied while the others are held approximately fixed, so that changes in device dynamics can be attributed, with reasonable confidence, to the component that was changed. A single monolithic material that supplied all three functions would be more constrained, because adjusting one property would inevitably drag the others with it.

This decoupling matters most for the salt. Although it occupies only a small fraction of the active layer, the salt strongly affects device dynamics, and the HSAB ordering set out in the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section fixes its qualitative direction: harder cations bind the ether oxygens more tightly, are correspondingly harder to displace, and relax more slowly. The cation is therefore the chemical handle on the slow-component timescale of the composite.

The anion enters the picture differently. As noted in the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section, soft, charge-delocalised anions such as triflate are held by electrostatic compensation of the cation rather than by oxygen coordination, and are typically more mobile than the cation in the same host. The cation–anion mobility asymmetry is, in this host environment, built into the salt chemistry itself, not an accidental property of any particular device.

This asymmetry can be exploited. If the cation relaxes slowly and the anion relaxes quickly, the composite carries two distinguishable memory timescales inside the same active layer: a fast component, associated mainly with anion redistribution at the interfaces, and a slow component, associated mainly with cation redistribution in the bulk of the ion-conducting host. The relative weight of each depends on the amplitude and duration of the applied bias and on the read protocol, and separating the two regimes experimentally is not trivial. The chemical origin, however, is clear enough. The result is a materials-level analogue of the coexistence of short-term and long-term plasticity regimes introduced in the Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning section: not the same microscopic mechanism, but the same functional requirement that a single physical element carry both fast-decaying and slower state variables. In the composite architecture that coexistence follows from the salt chemistry, and does not need an additional device structure.

Composition supplies the second set of handles. The two independent ratios introduced in the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section act on different physical quantities: the salt fraction sets the number of mobile ions and the strength of the ionic response per unit volume, the polyether fraction sets the volume of segmentally-mobile matrix in which those ions can rearrange. In principle the two should help separate the amplitude of the programmable conductance change from the kinetics with which that change develops and relaxes, and the comparative chapter tests the separation experimentally.

One-step blend processing makes systematic compositional investigation practical. The semiconductor, the host polymer, and the salt are all soluble in a common organic solvent, so a composite device of arbitrary composition can be produced by mixing the three stock solutions in the desired proportions and depositing the blend by spin coating or blade coating onto a patterned electrode. Changing composition means changing a mixing ratio, not redesigning the stack. The fabrication effort per composition is therefore modest, and the difference between nominally identical compositions is controlled by a small number of mixing and deposition parameters rather than by the reproducibility of a multi-step process. That makes it practical to build a set of compositions and cations within a common host and to characterise them all on a common experimental basis.

The main chemical variables of the composite (mobile cation, anion, polymer fraction, and salt fraction) are not just materials choices that make a device better or worse. They act on the internal-state physics of the device, and especially on the characteristic timescale of its fading memory. The cation fixes a default timescale through its coordination strength. The composition scales the amplitude and the rate at which the ionic response develops. The resulting device, read with a standard electrical protocol, exhibits a dynamical response whose characteristic time reflects those choices. Cation identity and composition are therefore computational design knobs as well as chemical ones: they select the fading-memory constant that the device presents to its circuit environment. The experimental chapters quantify how composition and cation chemistry map onto measurable device dynamics.

A further consequence is that the composite approach supports a distinctive computational use pattern that would be awkward to obtain from a homogeneous material. Because the three chemical variables above can be varied independently, and because the fabrication is a single-step blend deposition, a set of chemically distinct composites can be prepared, each with a different expected timescale, and assembled together as a small family of devices. The members of the family share the same electronic transport chemistry and the same host polymer but differ in composition or in cation identity. Their dynamical responses, on the physical chemistry above, are expected to span a range of fading-memory timescales.

Such a family, when read out through a common circuit, acts as a bank of temporal kernels with different decay constants. That is the substrate the heterogeneous physical reservoir computing, coincidence-detection, and multi-timescale transient filtering paradigms of the Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors section actually need. The heterogeneity that would be an obstacle for a conventional trained-weight application becomes, here, the mechanism by which temporal-computing functionality is obtained in hardware. The application-oriented chapter develops this point quantitatively.

The experimental chapters that follow test these predictions in two stages—first on a single Hybrane/LiOTf exemplar, then on a PEO-based corpus across composition and cation identity—and the application chapter then uses the resulting timescale spread as a temporal-computing substrate. The detailed scope is set out in the Scope and Objectives of This Thesis section.

This rationale does not target the benchmarks of non-volatile storage, and it does not claim a complete physical-chemistry theory of polymer-composite memristive operation. The narrower link it establishes is between chemical variables and dynamical device behaviour: polymer-electrolyte composites couple ionic dynamics to electronic transport, and their dynamics can be tuned through host chemistry, salt identity, and composition. Scope and Objectives of This Thesis

The preceding sections worked outward from the limits of conventional computing to biological synapses, memristive devices, the inorganic and organic memristor families, and finally polymer-electrolyte composites as a route to chemically tunable ion-mediated dynamics. This closing section turns that background into the scientific questions, the objectives, and the chapter structure of the thesis.

Open Scientific Questions

The arguments developed in the Organic Materials for Memristive Applications and Polymer Electrolyte Composites: The Enabling Strategy sections lead to three groups of questions. They are stated below in the form in which the chapters that follow address them.

The first question is whether a fully reversible two-terminal organic memristive device can be realised from a solution-processed polymer-electrolyte composite using standard organic-electronic fabrication. The historical survey of the Historical Development of Organic Memristors section identified this combination—two-terminal architecture, full reversibility, multi-state analogue control, and a chemically interpretable ionic mechanism—as a gap in the organic memristor field at the start of the present work. The second question is contingent on the first: whether such a device can support a broad set of synaptic functions in a single platform, including analogue switching, distinguishable short- and long-term memory regimes, an excitatory-post-synaptic-current response, spike-timing-dependent plasticity, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism. The third question concerns the physicochemical origin of the memory regimes themselves. Can the coexistence of a fast and a slow memory component in the composite be attributed to the cation–anion mobility asymmetry anticipated in the Rationale for the Composite Approach section? And does the chemistry of the cation–host coordination account for the slow component on a hard-soft acid-base basis?

The next questions broaden the inquiry from a single exemplar to a comparative experimental basis. Within a common polyether host (specifically PEO) and a common alkali-metal triflate salt family, how does composition affect the device dynamics when the polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions are varied around a reference formulation? With the composition held fixed, how does substitution of the cation along the Li+, Na+, K+ series shift the fading-memory timescale measured by a common dynamical protocol? These questions are pursued through three measurements that are abundant across the comparative corpus—I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation—and the answers reported in the thesis are anchored in them. The richer synaptic suite (EPSC, STDP, separated short- and long-term memory retention, impedance) is treated as a Chapter 2 result, and is not propagated as a uniform comparative claim.

The final questions concern use. Can volatile organic memristors of the kind developed here be exploited as temporal-computing primitives in their own right, instead of being judged against the figures of merit of non-volatile memory? Can composition and cation identity be used for application-level timescale matching, so that a deliberately heterogeneous family of devices spans a controlled range of fading-memory time constants? Can device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability, characterised from the same common measurements, contribute to the dynamics of a temporal-computing scheme, rather than only being a defect to be suppressed? These questions are answered in simulation in the data-driven application chapter, using behavioural models extracted from the comparative experimental corpus, with the proof-of-concept device used only as a source of priors and consistency checks.

Thesis Objectives

The scientific questions of the Open Scientific Questions section translate into the ordered objectives below. They structure the remaining chapters and delimit the scope of the work.

Design, fabricate, and characterise a proof-of-concept two-terminal organic memristive device based on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite, processed from a single solution onto a standard ITO/composite/Ag stack, and demonstrate the basic memristive functionality in a single platform.

On that single exemplar device, demonstrate the full set of synaptic functionalities relevant to the thesis: analogue multi-state switching, distinguishable short-term and long-term memory regimes with separately measured retention curves, excitatory-post-synaptic-current behaviour under pulsed stimulation, spike-timing-dependent plasticity with a biologically plausible time constant, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism.

Provide a mechanistic account of the two memory regimes of that exemplar in terms of the displacement of mobile triflate anions and lithium cations within the oxygen-rich ion-conducting host, supported by hard-soft acid-base reasoning on the cation–oxygen interaction.

Build a comparative experimental corpus on a PEO-based polymer-electrolyte platform loaded with alkali-metal triflate salts, fabricated under a common protocol, and characterise it through three common dynamical measurements — I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation — that are abundant across the corpus.

Within that corpus, quantify (a) the composition dependence of the dynamics through a PEO/lithium triflate (PEO/LiOTf) concentration series in which the polymer and salt mass fractions are varied around a reference composition, and (b) the cation dependence through a PEO/LiOTf, PEO/sodium triflate (PEO/NaOTf, NaCF3SO3), PEO/potassium triflate (PEO/KOTf, KCF3SO3) set at a fixed reference composition, on the same three common measurements.

Treat TMPE-based composites and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (LiN(SO2CF3)2, NaN(SO2CF3)2, KN(SO2CF3)2) systems as exploratory side evidence with a clearly delimited dataset, used to motivate or cross-check specific trends, and not as parallel main platforms.

Construct compact, experimentally grounded behavioural models per composition and per cation from the three common dynamical measurements of the comparative corpus, with the proof-of-concept device used only as a source of priors and consistency checks where its richer synaptic data are directly relevant; the models capture a fading-memory time constant, a nonlinear pulse-update rule, a device-to-device variability envelope, and a read transfer function.

Demonstrate, in simulation and from those models alone, three application-level temporal-computing schemes that use the composition- and cation-driven timescale spread of the comparative corpus: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector built on the measured variable-delay depotentiation kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants.

The objectives mirror the evidence base of the thesis. Objectives 1–3 are exemplar-level and rely on the full synaptic measurement suite of the proof-of-concept device. Objectives 4–6 are comparative; they rely on the three common dynamical measurements and do not assume that the richer synaptic metrics are available across the comparative corpus. Objectives 7–8 are computational, and use measurements already acquired in the experimental chapters. No further fabrication is required to fulfil them.

Thesis Outline

The thesis is organised in five chapters that follow the objective list.

Chapter 1 — Introduction. The present chapter: conceptual and materials background, ending in the polymer-electrolyte composite as the materials strategy for the rest of the thesis.

Chapter 2 — Proof of Concept: Polymer-Based Composite Organic Memristive Devices. The proof-of-concept chapter, on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite device. It is the only device of the thesis on which the complete synaptic suite (I–V hysteresis, potentiation/depression, EPSC, separated short- and long-term retention, STDP, and impedance) is treated as primary evidence, and the only place where the two-memory-regime mechanism is developed in terms of triflate-anion and lithium-cation displacement under the HSAB framing of the Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles section. The chapter is built on the work originally reported in Prado-Socorro et al. and carries the most complete synaptic dataset of the thesis.

Chapter 3 — PEO–Triflate Polymer-Electrolyte Memristive Devices: Composition and Ion-Identity Dependence. The comparative-experimental chapter. It reformulates the proof-of-concept architecture on a PEO host loaded with alkali-metal triflates, sweeping composition through a PEO/LiOTf concentration series and cation identity through a PEO/LiOTf, PEO/NaOTf, PEO/KOTf set at fixed reference composition. Both studies are anchored on the three common measurements (I–V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, variable-delay depotentiation), which form the comparative basis of this chapter and of the application chapter that follows. TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems are reported separately as exploratory side evidence. Variability is quantified from replicates and treated as a device-family property rather than as a defect.

Chapter 4 — Data-Driven Temporal Computing with Volatile Polymer-Electrolyte Memristors. The application chapter, which turns the experimental measurements of the previous two chapters into temporal-computing functionality without further fabrication. Compact behavioural models are extracted per composition and per cation from the three common measurements, and three schemes are then demonstrated in simulation: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector grounded in the measured variable-delay kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants. Circuit-level integration constraints (1T1M addressing, current compliance, read/write protocols, variability envelopes) are treated explicitly; the chapter is grounded in measured data throughout, not detached from it.

Chapter 5 — Conclusions and Outlook. The closing chapter synthesises the device-chemistry axis (the chapter and the comparative-experimental chapter) with the application axis (the temporal-computing chapter). It benchmarks the work against other temporal, reservoir, and event-driven neuromorphic hardware rather than against digital memory technologies. It states the limitations of the evidence base—in particular the restriction of the richer synaptic metrics to the proof-of-concept device, and the exploratory status of the TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems—and it identifies the future directions that follow most directly from the work.

The thesis does not aim at a competitive non-volatile memory. It links polymer-electrolyte composition and cation chemistry to measured ion-mediated device dynamics, and from there to temporal-computing primitives. The next chapter takes up the first link in that chain: the proof-of-concept SY/Hybrane/LiOTf device, its memristive and synaptic functionality, and the mechanistic interpretation that anchors the comparative and computational work to come.
