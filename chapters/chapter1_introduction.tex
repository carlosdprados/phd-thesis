\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:intro}
%% ============================================================

This thesis studies organic memristive devices based on ion-containing polymer composites, with an emphasis on using their dynamics as a computational resource. The devices sit at the intersection of solid-state electronics, polymer physical chemistry, and computational neuroscience. The chapter therefore moves from the limits of contemporary computing (\cref{sec:crisis}) to the biological analogy behind neuromorphic engineering, the physics and taxonomy of memristive devices, and finally the organic polymer-composite devices studied here.


%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Crisis in Modern Computing}\label{sec:crisis}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

Modern digital hardware is approaching a structural limit, which motivates the search for different electronic primitives. \cref{subsec:vonneumann} traces the limits of the stored-program architecture through the \textit{memory wall}, the \textit{energy wall}, and the end of classical transistor scaling. \cref{subsec:paradigms} then surveys two responses: integrating computation and storage within one physical substrate, and replacing clocked synchronous arithmetic with event-driven, temporally structured operation.

\subsection{Von Neumann Architecture and Its Limitations}\label{subsec:vonneumann}

The dominant architectural model underlying modern digital computers was articulated by von~Neumann in the ``First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC''~\cite{VonNeumann1993}, circulated in 1945. Its defining feature is the \textit{stored-program} concept: instructions and data are represented in the same memory and drawn from it, one word at a time, by a central processing unit under the control of an instruction pointer. This unification of code and data was the enabling abstraction of general-purpose computing, allowing a single physical machine to implement any computable function by loading a different sequence of binary words into memory. The resulting architecture --- a central processing unit, a main memory holding both code and data, and a bus connecting them --- is so deeply embedded in contemporary digital systems that its constituent elements (cores, caches, DRAM, register file) are treated as unquestioned primitives. More than seventy years of engineering effort have gone into optimising every stage of this pipeline while leaving its basic topology essentially untouched.

A direct consequence of the stored-program model is that processor and memory are \textit{physically distinct} subsystems, separated by a communication channel whose bandwidth is a primary determinant of system performance. Every computational step --- instruction fetch, operand load, result write-back --- requires one or more traversals of this channel. In a modern processor, the channel is a hierarchy of caches bridging register files clocked at several gigahertz and DRAM operating one or two orders of magnitude slower. Data must be moved along interconnect metal between the transistors that perform arithmetic and the cells that hold state; no arithmetic can proceed without transporting operands, and no result can be preserved without transporting it back. This pattern is known as the \textit{processor--memory dichotomy}.

The single-port form of the architecture permits only one word to be read or written per clock cycle, producing the \textit{von~Neumann bottleneck}~\cite{NatureBottleneck2018}. Decades of architectural innovation --- pipelined fetch, multi-level caches, speculative execution, prefetching, out-of-order issue, banked memory subsystems, and coprocessors from vector units to GPUs --- have progressively mitigated this bottleneck without replacing the underlying topology. Each innovation preserves the compute--storage distinction while amortising data movement over larger blocks or deeper queues. None removes the asymmetry, and the bottleneck has been displaced rather than dissolved; it reappears at whichever interface is slowest in each architectural generation.

The quantitative severity of the processor--memory imbalance was diagnosed by Wulf and McKee~\cite{WulfMcKee1995} in 1995 as the \textit{memory wall}. Processor performance was improving at approximately 60\% per year, whereas the latency and bandwidth of commodity DRAM improved nearly an order of magnitude more slowly. Extrapolating the two curves forward leads within a decade or two to a regime in which the average cycle is dominated almost entirely by memory access. Empirical evidence over the following decades confirmed the projection in detail. Modern microprocessors allocate between 30\% and 60\% of their silicon area, and an even larger fraction of their power budget, to caches, prefetch logic, memory controllers, and interconnect, simply to keep the arithmetic units supplied. Even heavily cache-optimised codes spend a non-negligible fraction of cycles stalled on memory, and the situation is worse for workloads with irregular access patterns or with working sets exceeding the on-chip cache. The bandwidth and energy facets of this imbalance are summarised in \cref{fig:vonneumann_bottleneck}.

The memory wall is a performance argument; a second, more fundamental argument concerns energy. Horowitz's 2014 survey of elementary-operation costs in a representative CMOS process~\cite{Horowitz2014} showed that a 32-bit integer addition costs on the order of \SI{0.1}{\pico\joule} and a register-file read a few picojoules, whereas an on-chip SRAM fetch costs tens of picojoules and a DRAM fetch several hundred picojoules to nanojoules --- three to four orders of magnitude more than the arithmetic operation it enables. In any data-movement-dominated workload, the total energy budget is therefore set by the cost of shuffling operands rather than by the cost of computing results. This asymmetry is the \textit{energy wall}. It reflects the fact that moving charge across a wire dissipates energy in the wire's parasitic capacitance, and the total wire length in a memory subsystem greatly exceeds that within an arithmetic unit. Any architecture that spatially separates computation from storage is therefore bounded in both speed and energy by interconnect physics, and that bound does not scale away with transistor miniaturisation.

For several decades, these architectural inefficiencies were absorbed rather than eliminated by the exponential improvement of the underlying silicon. Moore's observation~\cite{Moore1965} that transistor counts double at regular intervals was accompanied by Dennard scaling~\cite{Dennard1974}: when every linear MOSFET dimension and the supply voltage are scaled by \(1/k\), the transistor switches \(k\) times faster, consumes \(1/k^{2}\) the energy per switch, and occupies \(1/k^{2}\) the area, so that power density at constant activity is invariant. While the two laws held together, each generation delivered both more transistors and faster, cooler ones, and the memory and energy walls were perceived as distant rather than binding.

Dennard scaling broke down from around 2005, when gate-oxide thinning drove leakage currents into a regime that precluded further proportional voltage scaling~\cite{Hennessy2019}. Transistor count has continued to grow, but per-transistor performance and energy gains have slowed and power density has begun to rise with each generation --- the \textit{post-Dennard} or \textit{dark-silicon} regime. Under these conditions, the inefficiencies of the von~Neumann architecture become primary constraints: the cost of data movement can no longer be masked by a new process node, and must be addressed architecturally. At the same time, workloads have shifted from cache-friendly scientific and business codes to machine-learning kernels dominated by repeated matrix multiplications over parameter tensors resident in memory~\cite{LeCun2015}, intensifying the data-movement bottleneck just as silicon scaling no longer compensates for it. The Turing-Award lecture of Hennessy and Patterson~\cite{Hennessy2019} articulates this as an inflection point comparable to the transition from vacuum tubes to transistors, arguing that the next decade of computer architecture will be defined by domain-specific hardware that co-locates memory and computation.

\subsection{In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms}\label{subsec:paradigms}

Two broad responses to the situation described in \cref{subsec:vonneumann} have emerged over the past decade. Both abandon the strict separation of computation and storage, but along different axes. The first retains the clocked, arithmetic character of digital computing but performs operations directly within the memory array; it is known as \textit{in-memory}, \textit{near-memory}, or \textit{processing-in-memory} computing. The second abandons the synchronous arithmetic model in favour of operation driven by temporally structured event streams and exploits the intrinsic dynamical behaviour of physical devices as part of the computation itself; it is known as \textit{event-driven} or \textit{temporal} computing.

In-memory computing replaces the classical \textit{fetch--compute--write-back} loop with operations implemented physically within a memory array. The canonical example is the analogue matrix--vector multiplication performed on a resistive crossbar: memory cells hold conductances that encode matrix entries, the input vector is presented as row voltages, and column currents --- by Kirchhoff's laws --- yield the sums of products in a single clock period, at an energy cost dominated by steady-state dissipation rather than by per-operation data transfer. Demonstrations in metal-oxide resistive memories~\cite{Prezioso2015}, phase-change materials~\cite{Koelmans2015, Sebastian2014}, and organic ionic devices~\cite{Fuller2019, VanDeBurgt2017, VanDeBurgt2018} have shown matrix--vector products at energy efficiencies well beyond digital accelerators, and the mechanism generalises to convolutional and attention primitives. The memory array is therefore an active computational substrate.

The device-level requirements of in-memory computing nevertheless remain those of a memory: each cell should store a quasi-static conductance that encodes a trained weight, set once and read reliably without drift, with device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability below the numerical precision demanded by the application. Non-volatility over seconds to years is a design goal. Against this yardstick, polymer-composite ionic devices compare poorly: their device-to-device variability is substantial, their conductance drifts during repeated reads, and their retention is seconds to hours. They are, under this criterion, flawed competitors to metal-oxide or phase-change memories.

The second paradigm begins from the observation that many tasks of interest --- processing natural auditory and visual signals, controlling continuous motor systems, classifying biomedical waveforms, extracting features from streaming sensor data --- are defined on inputs that carry information in their temporal structure. For such tasks, the natural computational model represents inputs as streams of events in continuous time and lets the system state evolve continuously in response rather than stepping through clock cycles. This is realised at the algorithmic level by spiking neural networks, at the circuit level by event-driven asynchronous logic, and at the device level by substrates whose intrinsic relaxation dynamics match the temporal structure of the inputs they process. The present thesis operates at the device level.

The inspiration for this second paradigm is biological. Mammalian nervous systems perform demanding sensorimotor and cognitive tasks at power budgets orders of magnitude below artificial counterparts, and a significant part of this efficiency is attributable to architectural choices that differ systematically from the von~Neumann model. Neurons emit discrete events sparsely; synapses have conductances that are themselves dynamical variables; state is maintained and modified through slow, graded electrochemical processes; and the ionic medium is part of the computation. A direct transcription into silicon is neither feasible nor desirable, but several lessons are transferable: sparse-event representations~\cite{IndiveriLiu2015}, slow device physics as a computational primitive, and device heterogeneity as functional diversity. These constitute the conceptual basis of neuromorphic engineering.

A clean theoretical articulation of the computational value of such devices is due to Boyd and Chua~\cite{BoydChua1985}, who showed that any time-invariant nonlinear operator with \textit{fading memory} on a suitable function space can be uniformly approximated by a finite Volterra series. A physical system whose present state carries a smoothly decaying trace of its past inputs can therefore, combined with a trainable linear readout, implement a broad class of time-varying computations. This insight was operationalised by Jaeger's \textit{echo state network}~\cite{Jaeger2001ESN, JaegerHaas2004} and by Maass, Natschläger and Markram's \textit{liquid state machine}~\cite{Maass2002LSM}, and subsequently unified as \textit{reservoir computing}~\cite{LukoseviciusJaeger2009, Verstraeten2007}. An untrained dynamical system --- the reservoir --- is driven by the input stream, and only a linear readout is trained. When the reservoir is realised in hardware, the paradigm is called \textit{physical reservoir computing}~\cite{Tanaka2019}; demonstrations have used optical, mechanical, and spintronic systems as well as volatile memristive~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021} and organic electrochemical~\cite{Cucchi2021} substrates.

This changes how a memristive device is judged. Finite retention, a defect for weight storage, becomes useful in a reservoir because it sets the window over which past inputs influence the present output --- the fading-memory timescale. Device-to-device variability, penalised under weight storage, can also be useful: a bank of devices with different intrinsic timescales, read by a common linear layer, resolves temporal structure over a wider range of scales than any single device could~\cite{Tanaka2019}.

The same physical property --- for example the ionic relaxation time of a polymer electrolyte --- that makes a device a poor non-volatile memory can make it useful for temporal computing. The devices studied here are therefore not treated as candidate non-volatile weights. The experimental programme has three layers: a single device characterised deeply enough to establish its physical mechanism (\cref{ch:poc}); a comparative corpus across composition and cation identity that quantifies how chemistry tunes fading-memory behaviour; and a data-driven simulation study that uses those dynamics as temporal-computing primitives.

\section{The Biological Synapse as the Engineering Template}\label{sec:bio_synapse}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

If the separation of storage and computation is the structural obstacle in digital computing, the next question is what a replacement primitive should do. Biology gives a useful answer. In a biological circuit, the same physical element that propagates a signal stores the history that gates its propagation, and that element is itself modified by the signals passing through it. The element is the synapse. The biological details needed here are therefore those of synaptic signalling and plasticity, because they define the functional requirements for a hardware synapse.

\subsection{Neural Signal Transmission: A Brief Overview}\label{subsec:neural_signal}

The neuron is a morphologically polarised cell specialised for the reception, integration, and transmission of electrical signals~\cite{Drubach2000}. Its dendrites receive signals from other cells; the soma integrates those signals and, if the integrated membrane potential crosses a threshold, initiates an outgoing signal at the axon hillock; the axon propagates that signal over distances from millimetres to metres; and the axon terminals release a chemical messenger onto downstream cells. A mature mammalian neuron typically receives inputs from \(10^{3}\)--\(10^{4}\) other neurons through contacts on its dendrites; each such contact is a \textit{synapse}. The cell's computational role is defined almost entirely by the pattern, strength, and dynamics of its synaptic contacts rather than by properties of the soma in isolation.

The signal that travels along the axon is the \textit{action potential}, a stereotyped millisecond-long depolarisation generated by voltage-gated \ce{Na+} opening followed by \ce{K+}-mediated repolarisation, regenerated at each internodal patch. Action potentials are all-or-nothing: their waveform is essentially independent of the input strength that elicited them, and information is carried in the temporal properties of spike trains --- rate, precise timing, and coincidence across neurons. The physical substrate of neural computation is therefore event-driven in the sense introduced in \cref{subsec:paradigms}. Between spikes, a neuron consumes only a leakage current; during a spike, it dissipates a small, nearly fixed amount of energy. Useful computation is carried out by the patterned arrival of events at the many synapses, not by a clocked update of a shared register.

Transmission at the synapse is predominantly chemical. When an action potential reaches an axon terminal, the depolarisation opens voltage-gated \ce{Ca^{2+}} channels; the \ce{Ca^{2+}} influx triggers fusion of vesicles containing a neurotransmitter (most commonly glutamate, the dominant excitatory transmitter in the cortex) with the pre-synaptic membrane. The transmitter diffuses across the \(\sim\)\SI{20}{\nano\metre} synaptic cleft and binds to receptor proteins on the post-synaptic membrane, opening ion channels directly or via second-messenger cascades. The resulting ionic current --- the excitatory or inhibitory post-synaptic current (EPSC or IPSC) --- transiently shifts the post-synaptic membrane potential, and its amplitude and kinetics define the \textit{strength} of the synapse at that moment. The process is probabilistic and quantal, and the same machinery that propagates the signal can, through modulation of its components, change the strength of subsequent transmissions.

The synapse is thus simultaneously the point of signal transmission, the point of signal modulation (through the regulated amplitude and time course of the post-synaptic current), and the point of memory (through long-lived changes in that amplitude in response to the cell's own activity). This co-localisation of transmission, modulation, and memory in a single physical element makes the synapse, rather than the neuron, the most informative biological template for post-von-Neumann electronic devices. The anatomy, together with the plasticity regimes developed next, is summarised in \cref{fig:synapse_plasticity}.

\subsection{Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning}\label{subsec:plasticity}

That experience leaves a physical trace in the nervous system, and that this trace is preferentially localised at synapses, is one of the oldest and most thoroughly supported hypotheses in modern neuroscience. Its conceptual formulation is due to Hebb~\cite{Hebb1949}, whose 1949 monograph argued that if a pre-synaptic cell repeatedly participates in firing a post-synaptic cell, the connection between the two should be strengthened. The modern précis of this idea --- ``cells that fire together, wire together'' --- captures its central structural claim: the relevant learning rule is \textit{local} (it depends only on the activity of the two cells directly coupled by the synapse) and \textit{correlation-sensitive} (it is driven by joint, rather than independent, activity). As a physiological hypothesis, Hebb's postulate remained without direct experimental test for more than two decades, but it has since come to organise essentially every subsequent account of synaptic learning, and the various forms of \textit{synaptic plasticity} described below can all be read as mechanistic instantiations of Hebbian, anti-Hebbian, or Hebbian-like rules operating at different timescales.

The canonical demonstration that synaptic strength can be persistently altered by patterned activity was provided by Bliss and Lømo~\cite{Bliss1973}, who showed that a brief, high-frequency tetanic stimulation of the perforant path of the anaesthetised rabbit hippocampus produced an increase in the amplitude of the population post-synaptic potential that persisted for hours and, in later studies, for days to weeks. This phenomenon, \textit{long-term potentiation} (LTP), has become the paradigm of persistent, experience-dependent synaptic change. Its mechanism in the hippocampus involves two classes of ionotropic glutamate receptor on the post-synaptic membrane: AMPA receptors (\(\alpha\)-amino-3-hydroxy-5-methyl-4-isoxazolepropionic acid), which mediate the fast EPSC in response to glutamate release, and NMDA receptors (\(N\)-methyl-\(D\)-aspartate), which open only when the post-synaptic membrane is already depolarised and therefore act as \ce{Ca^{2+}}-permeable coincidence detectors for simultaneous pre- and post-synaptic activity.

NMDA-gated \ce{Ca^{2+}} influx triggers a signalling cascade that leads to the regulated insertion of additional AMPA receptors into the post-synaptic membrane, increasing the EPSC elicited by subsequent pre-synaptic spikes. A complementary phenomenon, \textit{long-term depression} (LTD), produces a persistent decrease in synaptic strength in response to low-frequency or anti-correlated activity and involves the reverse trafficking of AMPA receptors. Together, LTP and LTD provide the bidirectional, long-lasting modification of synaptic amplitude widely regarded as the physiological substrate of declarative and procedural learning.

Superimposed on these long-lasting changes is a family of rapid and reversible modulations known collectively as \textit{short-term plasticity}~\cite{Zucker1989}. Following a pre-synaptic action potential, the immediate strength of the synapse depends on the recent history of its own activity: \textit{short-term facilitation} is a transient increase in EPSC amplitude in response to a second spike closely following the first, attributable to residual pre-synaptic \ce{Ca^{2+}} that has not yet been cleared from the terminal; \textit{short-term depression} is a transient decrease attributable to vesicle depletion or receptor desensitisation after recent heavy release. Characteristic time constants for these effects range from tens of milliseconds to several seconds, with different synapses exhibiting different mixes of facilitation and depression.

Short-term plasticity implements temporal filtering in the individual synapse: the same sequence of pre-synaptic spikes produces different post-synaptic responses depending on the inter-spike intervals within that sequence. It turns the synapse into a nonlinear, history-dependent temporal filter through transient, self-resetting state changes that do not consolidate into a permanent modification of the connection.

A third and conceptually unifying form of plasticity is \textit{spike-timing-dependent plasticity} (STDP), described in cortical pyramidal cells by Markram and co-workers~\cite{Markram1997} and subsequently characterised in hippocampal cultures in quantitative detail by Bi and Poo~\cite{Bi1998}. STDP sharpens Hebb's postulate into a timing-sensitive form: whether a given synapse is potentiated or depressed by a pairing of pre- and post-synaptic spikes depends on the sign and magnitude of the interval between them, typically on a window of a few tens of milliseconds. When the pre-synaptic spike reliably precedes the post-synaptic spike, the synapse is potentiated; when the order is reversed, it is depressed; when the interval is long in either direction, no lasting change is induced. The STDP window lets a single synapse act as a directional correlation detector whose output is a long-lived change in efficacy. The rule is inherently temporal: the synapse must retain a short-lived trace of its own recent pre-synaptic activity long enough to be compared to the post-synaptic response, and then convert that comparison into a persistent modification of amplitude.

The synapse that emerges from these lines of evidence is not a passive ``weight'' but a stateful element with coexisting timescales. On the millisecond timescale, it transmits individual spikes through quantal release; on tens-to-thousands of milliseconds, short-term facilitation and depression modulate that transmission according to recent activity; on minutes to hours and longer, LTP and LTD produce persistent changes in baseline amplitude. These layers coexist at chemical synapses in the mammalian CNS and are supported by partially overlapping but distinct molecular machinery~\cite{AbbottRegehr2004}. Abbott and Regehr~\cite{AbbottRegehr2004} argued on this basis that the synapse is better understood as a computational element than as a memory element: short-term plasticity lets a single synapse act as a high-, low-, or band-pass temporal filter depending on its mix of facilitation and depression, and the trained circuit exploits such filters in parallel. A hardware synapse should therefore support both durable and decaying state changes within the same device class.

\subsection{Why Hardware Emulation of the Synapse?}\label{subsec:why_hw}

The need for a hardware embodiment of synaptic function follows directly from \cref{sec:crisis}. A software neural network is executed as arithmetic on values drawn from a memory hierarchy physically distinct from the arithmetic units, and every forward pass pays the memory and energy wall costs catalogued above; those costs scale with the number of synapses simulated. The cortex of a small mammal contains of order \(10^{12}\)--\(10^{14}\) synapses and its nervous system operates on of order \SI{10}{\watt}. No near-term von~Neumann substrate is likely to close that gap, because the cost of fetching a weight before multiplying it by an activation is bounded by interconnect physics rather than by microarchitectural tuning. The synapse is the most compact low-power example of co-localised analogue memory and computation known, which makes it a useful physical template.

The research programme that pursues this observation in circuit form is \textit{neuromorphic engineering}, a field whose programmatic origin is usually attributed to Mead~\cite{Mead1990}, and whose defining ambition is to build electronic circuits whose devices, topology, and dynamics physically instantiate the computational primitives of the nervous system rather than emulate them in software. Within this programme, the synapse has emerged as the key device-level target, and the search for a physical ``artificial synapse'' has driven a substantial body of work across device physics, materials science, and circuit design over the past two decades.

A hardware synapse should support an analogue, multi-state conductance that can be set reversibly by applied electrical stimuli, read non-destructively through a small probe current, and arrayed at high density so that many thousands of such elements can be addressed from a manageable number of input and output lines~\cite{Kuzum2013, IndiveriLiu2015}. Its response to applied stimuli should be nonlinear in a biologically meaningful sense --- for instance, exhibiting a pulse-number or pulse-amplitude threshold below which no change is induced --- and its dynamics should be compatible with the timescales of the signals it is meant to process, which span several orders of magnitude from milliseconds to seconds and beyond.

Among these requirements, the demand for \textit{analogue}, rather than binary, multi-state conductance modulation deserves particular emphasis. A binary synapse encodes one bit of information per element and, by the standard argument of rate coding, requires prohibitively many elements to represent the graded synaptic weights of biological circuits. An analogue synapse, by contrast, can in principle encode several bits per element through the available range of conductance values. For the ionic-conductance devices considered here, the write energy can also be several orders of magnitude below the energy cost of an equivalent digital multi-bit write.

Reversibility is equally important: a synapse whose conductance can be potentiated but not reliably depressed cannot support the bidirectional Hebbian rules discussed in \cref{subsec:plasticity}, and is useful only as a programmable weight in a feed-forward classifier, not as a substrate for on-chip learning. The basic specification is therefore analogue, reversible, multi-state, low-energy conductance modulation under electrical pulses. That specification motivates the devices considered in the remaining chapters.

The biology of \cref{subsec:plasticity} sharpens the specification beyond retention-centric accounts of artificial synapses. Biological synapses are simultaneously quasi-stable stores (LTP/LTD) and transient, decaying filters (short-term plasticity, STDP), coexisting in the same physical element and contributing jointly to circuit computation. A useful hardware template therefore has two device roles: persistent or quasi-persistent programmable weights for the long-term component, and dynamic fading-memory elements for the short-term component. The fading-memory role, the one pursued here, mirrors the reservoir-computing picture developed in \cref{subsec:paradigms}: a bank of devices with a spread of time constants forms the heterogeneous reservoir that physical reservoir computing requires~\cite{Tanaka2019, Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021}.

A hardware synapse should support analogue, history-dependent, reversible conductance modulation at low energy per update, should integrate into dense arrays, and should include variants whose state decays on biologically relevant timescales as well as variants whose state persists. Chemical tunability is also important: biological short-term plasticity timescales are set by \ce{Ca^{2+}} buffering, vesicle recycling, and receptor desensitisation, and these differ across synapse types. A useful hardware family should offer an analogous way to tune electronic timescales. No classical transistor-based memory meets this list; the next section asks whether any physical device class does.

\section{The Memristor: Theory and History}\label{sec:memristor_history}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

Circuit theory provides a useful answer. A memristive device has an instantaneous current--voltage relation that depends on an internal state imposed by past activity. The concept is the \textit{memristor}; the broader experimental family is \textit{memristive devices}. This section develops the theory, reviews the first canonical experimental realisation, classifies the main mechanistic families, and then reinterprets the usual device parameters in terms of both trained-weight storage and volatile temporal computing. The formal definition accommodates both quasi-stable and relaxing internal states; the later chapters use the relaxing-state sector of that definition.

\subsection{Theoretical Foundation}\label{subsec:memristor_theory}

Classical linear circuit theory describes a two-terminal passive element through the relation it imposes between two of the four fundamental lumped variables: the voltage \(v\), the current \(i\), the electric charge \(q = \int i \, \mathrm{d}t\), and the flux linkage \(\varphi = \int v \, \mathrm{d}t\). Three of the six possible pairings among these four variables are occupied by the familiar elements of elementary electromagnetism. The resistor \(R\) is defined by the direct \(v\)--\(i\) relation \(v = R\,i\); the capacitor \(C\) by the \(q\)--\(v\) relation \(q = C\,v\); and the inductor \(L\) by the \(\varphi\)--\(i\) relation \(\varphi = L\,i\). Two further pairings, between \(q\) and \(i\) and between \(\varphi\) and \(v\), are trivial consequences of the definitions of charge and flux linkage as time integrals. The remaining non-trivial pairing, that between charge \(q\) and flux linkage \(\varphi\), has no classical element associated with it.

The gap was filled by Chua~\cite{Chua1971} in 1971, who observed that completeness of the passive-element taxonomy required a fourth fundamental two-terminal element relating charge to flux linkage. He named it the \textit{memristor}, a contraction of ``memory resistor'', and defined it by the constitutive relation \(\varphi = \hat{\varphi}(q)\) or, in an equivalent form used more widely in the subsequent device literature, by \(v = M(q)\,i\), where the \textit{memristance} \(M(q) = \mathrm{d}\hat{\varphi}/\mathrm{d}q\) has the units of a resistance but depends functionally on the charge that has flowed through the device.

The operational content of this equation is that a memristor is a resistance whose present value is determined by the history of the current that has passed through it. If \(M\) is a constant, the device reduces to an ordinary resistor; if \(M\) depends non-trivially on \(q\), the device retains a memory of past activity in its present electrical response. The universal fingerprint of such behaviour in the \(i\)--\(v\) plane is a \textit{pinched hysteresis loop} passing through the origin under periodic excitation, reflecting the fact that zero current requires zero voltage instantaneously while the device nonetheless carries a history that biases its response to subsequent stimuli. This hysteretic fingerprint is the elementary experimental criterion by which memristive behaviour is diagnosed throughout the device literature.

The ideal memristor defined by \(\varphi = \hat{\varphi}(q)\) is, however, a restrictive mathematical object, and very few physical devices satisfy it exactly. Chua and Kang~\cite{ChuaKang1976} therefore generalised the concept in 1976 to that of a \textit{memristive system}, specified by the pair of equations
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot{\mathbf{x}} &= f(\mathbf{x}, u, t), \\
y &= g(\mathbf{x}, u, t)\, u,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:memristive_system}
\end{equation}
in which \(u\) and \(y\) are the electrical input and output, \(\mathbf{x}\) is an internal state vector, and \(f\) and \(g\) are generally nonlinear. The state equation \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = f(\mathbf{x}, u, t)\) may include relaxation terms that drive the state toward a rest value once the input is removed; a device whose state relaxes is, by this definition, a memristive system even though it is not an ideal memristor in the strict sense of \cite{Chua1971}. Volatile devices are therefore members of the same family, and this broader framework is the one used for the devices reported here.

\subsection{The First Physical Realisation: TiO\texorpdfstring{$_2$}{2} Memristor}\label{subsec:tio2_memristor}

For more than three decades after Chua's original proposal, the memristor existed principally as a theoretical construct. Thin-film metal-oxide resistive switches exhibiting hysteretic current--voltage characteristics had been described in the device-physics literature since the late 1960s, and in retrospect many of these systems can be classified as memristive. None, however, had been interpreted explicitly within Chua's formalism, and the field lacked a canonical experimental realisation tying the formal concept to a concrete thin-film device.

The identification was made by Strukov, Snider, Stewart and Williams~\cite{Strukov2008} in 2008, working at HP Laboratories. Their device consisted of a thin film of titanium dioxide (\ce{TiO2}) sandwiched between two platinum electrodes, in which a narrow, heavily reduced \ce{TiO_{2-x}} sublayer acted as an oxygen-deficient, electronically conductive region and the adjacent stoichiometric \ce{TiO2} layer acted as a resistive barrier. Under an applied voltage, the oxygen vacancies populating the sublayer drift along the electric field, and the boundary between the conductive and resistive regions is displaced through the film. Because the overall resistance of the stack is determined by the geometric proportion of the two regions, the device resistance is modulated continuously by the displacement of that internal boundary. In the language of \cref{eq:memristive_system}, the position of the boundary plays the role of the internal state \(\mathbf{x}\), the drift of oxygen vacancies provides the state equation \(f\), and the pinched hysteresis predicted by Chua's theory is recovered quantitatively from the resulting \(i\)--\(v\) response.

The impact of this result was disproportionate to the incremental physics it reported. By identifying an explicit thin-film device with a previously formal theoretical construct, Strukov and co-workers converted the memristor from a mathematical curiosity into a concrete experimental object and catalysed a large body of subsequent work on resistive-switching and memristive devices. A two-terminal resistance that can be set to a quasi-continuous range of values by electrical pulses and read non-destructively at low voltage was quickly recognised as a physical realisation of the analogue weight element required for neuromorphic hardware~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013}, and crossbar arrays operating as in-memory matrix--vector multipliers followed within a few years~\cite{Prezioso2015, IelminiWong2018}. The 2008 \ce{TiO2} device is one specific resistive-switching mechanism --- oxygen-vacancy drift in a transition-metal oxide --- and the memristive formalism accommodates several mechanistically distinct families. The constitutive argument that defines the memristor, together with the pinched-hysteresis fingerprint by which it is experimentally diagnosed, is summarised in \cref{fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint}.

\subsection{Classification of Memristive Mechanisms}\label{subsec:memristor_classes}

The term \textit{memristor}, as currently used in the device-physics literature, designates a phenomenological category rather than a microscopic mechanism. A device is called memristive if its current--voltage characteristic exhibits the pinched hysteresis loop predicted by Chua's theory and if its internal state can be set and read electrically; it is not required to rely on any specific physical process to do so. Review articles have accordingly identified several broad mechanistic families~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013, IelminiWong2018}, each with different operational strengths and weaknesses. A brief taxonomy clarifies the physical diversity hidden behind the umbrella term and situates the ionic-polymer devices studied here within the broader experimental landscape.

The first and historically most visible family is \textit{filamentary} resistive switching. Under an applied voltage, a narrow conductive path --- a filament --- is formed across an otherwise insulating thin film, and is subsequently ruptured or reformed by further electrical stimuli. In \textit{conductive-bridge} random-access memories (CBRAM), the filament is composed of metal atoms electrochemically displaced from an active electrode (typically silver or copper) through a solid electrolyte. In \textit{valence-change} memories, often based on binary transition-metal oxides, the filament is composed of ordered oxygen vacancies or partially reduced metal-cation states within the oxide lattice~\cite{Yang2008}. Filamentary devices typically exhibit high ON/OFF ratios, short switching times, and non-volatile retention; their principal drawbacks are the stochastic nature of filament nucleation and dissolution, which translates into substantial device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability~\cite{Sun2019}, and the restricted number of reliably distinguishable intermediate conductance states that this stochasticity imposes. Filamentary devices are therefore well suited to binary storage and to coarse multi-level weights, but they are a less natural platform for dense analogue representations.

A second family operates through \textit{interface} or \textit{non-filamentary} switching, in which the active area of the device modulates its conductance uniformly rather than through a localised filament. Typical implementations modulate the height or width of a Schottky barrier at a metal--semiconductor junction through the accumulation or depletion of mobile ionic species near the interface, or, in certain mixed ionic--electronic systems, through a distributed electrochemical doping of the bulk semiconductor~\cite{VanDeBurgt2017, Fuller2019}. Because the modulation is spatially distributed rather than localised, interface-type devices usually support a quasi-continuous range of intermediate conductance values and therefore finer analogue gradation than their filamentary counterparts. Their ON/OFF ratios tend to be smaller, but their variability is typically lower and their current scales predictably with the junction area, which simplifies array integration. Several organic electrochemical devices surveyed later in this chapter fall into this class, as do, in large part, the ionic polymer-composite devices studied experimentally here.

A third family consists of \textit{phase-change} memories, in which the active element is a chalcogenide alloy (most commonly \ce{Ge2Sb2Te5} or closely related compositions) whose electrical resistance depends on whether it is in a crystalline or amorphous phase. Programming is performed by Joule-heating pulses that melt and rapidly quench, or partially anneal, the material~\cite{Sebastian2014, Koelmans2015, LGallo2018}. Phase-change memories typically achieve excellent retention, large ON/OFF ratios, and high endurance, at the cost of substantial per-write energies and a tendency towards binary or few-state behaviour unless specifically engineered to support analogue intermediate states. They have become a mature technology for storage-class-memory applications and are increasingly deployed in in-memory analogue-computing demonstrations.

A fourth family, the \textit{spintronic} or magnetic resistive device, implements memristive behaviour through the magnetic configuration of a tunnel junction whose resistance depends on the relative orientation of two ferromagnetic layers~\cite{Torrejon2017}. Spintronic devices operate at very high speed and low per-write energy, and they are the basis of commercial spin-transfer-torque magnetic random-access memory. Their principal limitations for neuromorphic applications are a relatively small ON/OFF ratio (often of order two) and a limited range of stable intermediate states, although recent work has shown that their intrinsic stochastic dynamics can itself be exploited as a computational resource within appropriately designed architectures~\cite{Mizrahi2018, Torrejon2017}.

A fifth family, and the one most relevant here, is that of \textit{ionic} memristors, in which the internal state is the distribution of one or more mobile ionic species within a solid matrix. Ionic memristors can be further subdivided by the nature of the host matrix --- inorganic oxide, chalcogenide, mixed ionic--electronic conductor, or polymer electrolyte --- and by the identity of the mobile species, which range from small cations such as \ce{Li+}, \ce{Na+}, or \ce{Ag+} to protons and a variety of small anions. What distinguishes ionic memristors as a class is that the internal state is a chemical configuration of mobile species, not a morphological or magnetic configuration of the host, and that the kinetics of that state are governed by ionic transport and binding within the matrix rather than by electronic processes alone.

Two consequences follow. First, ionic systems exhibit intrinsically history-dependent dynamics, because the distribution of mobile species at any given instant reflects the cumulative effect of past electric fields on their migration. Second, ionic systems typically exhibit spontaneous \textit{relaxation} in the absence of a driving field, because the same intermolecular forces and entropic gradients that oppose the non-equilibrium distribution imposed under bias drive the system back towards equilibrium once the bias is removed. Volatility is therefore not a pathology of ionic memristors but an intrinsic feature of the underlying physics, and its timescales are set by ionic transport kinetics, which can be tuned chemically through the host matrix. This fifth family, and especially its organic polymer-electrolyte subfamily, is the platform used in the experimental work.

\subsection{Key Memristive Parameters}\label{subsec:memristor_params}

The mechanistic heterogeneity surveyed in \cref{subsec:memristor_classes} is reflected in the specialised vocabulary that has grown up around the evaluation of memristive devices. A typical specification report will quote several parameters. The \textit{high-resistance state} (HRS) and \textit{low-resistance state} (LRS) define the extremes of the accessible conductance range; their ratio is the \textit{ON/OFF ratio}, a figure of merit for the dynamic range of the device. The \textit{set} and \textit{reset voltages} specify the magnitude of electrical stimuli required to program the device between its extreme states, and the associated \textit{write energy} sets the per-operation energetic cost. The \textit{switching speed} describes how rapidly the device responds to a programming pulse. \textit{Retention} quantifies the time for which a given state is preserved without re-programming, and \textit{endurance} the number of programming cycles the device can support before its behaviour degrades. Parameters relevant specifically to analogue operation include the \textit{linearity} and \textit{symmetry} of the conductance update under identical positive and negative pulses, and the \textit{device-to-device} and \textit{cycle-to-cycle variability} of all of the above.

These parameters have their natural setting in the evaluation of devices intended for \textit{trained-weight} storage in analogue in-memory-computing arrays~\cite{IelminiWong2018, Prezioso2015}. In that setting, the target application is a fixed conductance matrix implementing a trained neural network; retention must be long enough to preserve trained weights between re-programming events, typically of order seconds to years depending on the deployment scenario; endurance must support occasional re-training and periodic write--verify cycles; linearity and symmetry are central to the numerical accuracy of gradient-based training performed in place on the array; and variability translates directly into a loss of inference accuracy at deployment. A large fraction of the memristor-device literature is written from this standpoint, and it is one of the principal contexts in which organic and polymer-composite devices have been judged --- frequently unfavourably, because their retention times are short, their endurance is limited, and their device-to-device variability is high.

Not all entries in this parameter list are unambiguously desirable when maximised. Retention is the clearest example: for a trained-weight device a short retention is a defect, but for a fading-memory device it \textit{is} the computational parameter that sets the effective window over which past inputs are integrated, and the design target becomes a retention matched to the signal timescale rather than ``as long as possible''. Analogous shifts apply to switching speed, write energy, linearity, and symmetry, whose optima differ between the two regimes and cannot in general be simultaneously satisfied by a single device.

Variability requires a slightly finer distinction. Intra-device instability --- a given device failing to reproduce its own response under the same protocol --- remains a defect under either regime. Inter-device heterogeneity, by contrast, is penalised under weight storage but useful under reservoir operation: a reservoir of elements with a spread of time constants and nonlinear transfer characteristics is more expressive than a set of identical elements, because the spread provides independent nonlinear projections of the input onto different readout axes~\cite{Tanaka2019}. The distinction between intra-device instability and inter-device heterogeneity will recur in the evaluation of polymer-composite devices in the later chapters.

\subsection{Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors}\label{subsec:volatile_paradigms}

The reframing of \cref{subsec:memristor_params} reflects a set of computational paradigms that have emerged over the past decade specifically for volatile, heterogeneous, history-dependent devices. Within these paradigms, a memristor whose state decays on a well-defined timescale is the natural physical instantiation of a \textit{leaky integrator} --- the elementary dynamical building block of temporal computation. Its computational meaning is fixed not by how long it holds a given value but by what it does to an incoming temporal signal. The three paradigms surveyed below will be revisited in a later chapter, where the polymer-electrolyte devices are evaluated against the metrics these paradigms imply.

The most prominent of the three paradigms is \textit{physical reservoir computing}~\cite{Tanaka2019}, introduced at the conceptual level in \cref{subsec:paradigms}. In a volatile-memristor reservoir, the input signal is encoded as a stream of voltage pulses presented to an array of memristive devices whose internal states evolve under the applied stimuli and relax between them. At any instant, the collective state of the array provides a nonlinear projection of the recent history of the input onto a high-dimensional feature space, and a linear readout trained by ordinary least squares on this space is sufficient to solve a broad class of time-varying classification, regression, and prediction tasks. Experimental demonstrations of this principle in inorganic volatile memristors have been reported for diffusive metal-filament devices~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019} and dynamic oxide memristors~\cite{Zhong2021}, and demonstrations in organic electrochemical systems have begun to appear~\cite{Cucchi2021}. In all of these cases, the short retention of the individual device is not a limitation of the method but its enabling physical property: it is the retention that supplies each device with a well-defined fading-memory time constant.

A second paradigm exploits volatile memristors as \textit{coincidence detectors} and \textit{temporal-correlation extractors}. Because the internal state of such a device relaxes in the absence of a driving pulse, a pulse train whose inter-pulse intervals are short compared with the relaxation timescale produces a progressive accumulation of state, while a train whose intervals are long produces essentially no accumulation. The integrated response of the device therefore carries direct information about the temporal proximity of successive events, and can be used to discriminate coincident or near-coincident inputs from temporally dispersed ones. Architectures based on this principle have been proposed and partially demonstrated in combination with STDP-like plasticity rules, in which the sign and magnitude of the long-term response depend on the relative timing of pre- and post-synaptic events~\cite{Bichler2012}. The approach constitutes, in effect, a direct hardware transcription of the spike-timing-sensitive plasticity introduced in \cref{subsec:plasticity}, and it relies essentially on the relaxation dynamics provided by the volatile memristor.

A third paradigm employs banks of volatile memristors, engineered to exhibit distinct relaxation time constants, as \textit{multi-timescale transient filter banks} for streaming signals. In such an architecture, a single input is processed in parallel by devices of different intrinsic timescales: elements with short time constants respond sharply to fast transients and return quickly to baseline, while elements with long time constants respond more gradually and integrate over longer windows. The ensemble of outputs is then read by a downstream classifier to detect onsets, bursts, rhythmic patterns, or slow envelopes that could not be extracted from any single time constant acting in isolation. The chemical tunability of ionic memristors, and in particular their capacity to span several orders of magnitude in relaxation timescale through modest changes in host composition or mobile-species identity, makes them especially well matched to this architecture.

The performance metrics relevant to these three paradigms differ systematically from the trained-weight metrics catalogued in \cref{subsec:memristor_params}. For reservoir computing, the principal figure of merit is the \textit{memory capacity} of the device ensemble, a quantitative measure of how faithfully the reservoir state reconstructs past inputs at various lag times, together with the \textit{class separability} it provides on a representative benchmark task and its \textit{robustness} to the intrinsic variability of the constituent devices~\cite{Dambre2012}.

For coincidence detection and temporal-correlation extraction, the relevant metrics include the width and symmetry of the effective timing window, the discrimination margin between coincident and dispersed inputs, and the energy cost per detected event. For multi-timescale filtering, the central quantities are the span of accessible time constants across the ensemble, the linearity and isolation of each channel, and the system-level power consumption under realistic input statistics. None of these metrics is meaningfully captured by HRS, LRS, ON/OFF ratio, or retention in isolation. They are \textit{task-matched}: they reward a device whose physical timescales are aligned with the temporal structure of the signals that the device is required to process.

The memristor is simultaneously a concept in circuit theory, a heterogeneous family of physical devices, and a bridge between the synaptic template of \cref{sec:bio_synapse} and the dynamical computational paradigms above. Among the mechanistic families surveyed, ionic memristors stand out because their internal state is a chemical configuration of mobile species and because relaxation and history dependence are intrinsic to their operation. The next section benchmarks the inorganic memristor families that dominate present-day neuromorphic hardware, identifying which device requirements they meet well and which ones motivate a complementary organic route. The two computational modes that the memristive family supports --- static programmable weights and volatile fading-memory elements --- are contrasted in \cref{fig:static_vs_fading}.

\section{Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing}\label{sec:inorganic_memristors}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

The abstract taxonomy of \cref{subsec:memristor_classes} can now be narrowed to the three inorganic families that account for most present-day neuromorphic-hardware activity: metal-oxide resistive memories, phase-change memories, and spin-transfer-torque magnetic tunnel junctions. Each occupies a distinct and technologically powerful region, and inorganic memristors have firmly established the credibility of memristive hardware as a post-von-Neumann substrate. The specific combination pursued here --- analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on solution-processable soft substrates --- lies outside the regions in which these families are strongest, and motivates a complementary investigation in organic and polymeric materials.

\subsection{Metal-Oxide Memristors}\label{subsec:metal_oxide_memristors}

The single broadest and most technologically developed inorganic memristor family operates through resistive switching in transition-metal-oxide thin films. Following the identification of the \ce{TiO2} device of Strukov and co-workers~\cite{Strukov2008} with Chua's memristor concept, research activity shifted rapidly to a wider palette of oxide systems, of which the best-studied are hafnium dioxide (\ce{HfO2}), tantalum oxide (\ce{TaO_x}), nickel oxide (\ce{NiO}), tungsten oxide (\ce{WO_x}), and titanium dioxide itself. Many of these materials had been used for years as gate-dielectric or interconnect-related layers in conventional silicon technology, and their integration into back-end-of-line processes was therefore immediately feasible. This compatibility with standard CMOS flows is the most important practical reason for the prominence of metal-oxide memristors in neuromorphic-hardware demonstrations: arrays of thousands to millions of such devices can be fabricated on top of existing addressing electronics without disruption of the underlying silicon, and large-scale integrated demonstrations --- including the metal-oxide crossbar of Prezioso et al.~\cite{Prezioso2015} --- have followed accordingly.

At the device level, metal-oxide memristors are usually classified mechanistically as either \textit{valence-change memories} (VCM) or \textit{electrochemical metallisation} / \textit{conductive-bridge} memories (CBRAM), according to which mobile species carries the switching behaviour. In VCM devices, the mobile species is an oxygen vacancy, whose field-driven migration produces local reduction and oxidation of the host cation and a corresponding change in the electronic conductance of a confined filamentary region~\cite{Yang2008}. In CBRAM devices, the mobile species is a metal cation originating from an electrochemically active electrode (typically \ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+}), which migrates across the oxide layer under the applied field and plates out into a metallic filament that shorts the stack.

Both mechanisms commonly require an initial \textit{forming} step, in which a localised dielectric soft breakdown establishes the filament geometry that subsequent set and reset pulses can then modulate. Once formed, the device exhibits a bistable or, depending on the compliance current and pulse protocol, a graded multi-state resistance. A third, intermediate class of metal-oxide devices operates through non-filamentary \textit{interface-type} modulation, in which the conductance of the entire device area is adjusted through distributed oxygen-vacancy redistribution at a metal--oxide Schottky interface, and which tends to support finer analogue gradation at the cost of lower ON/OFF ratios.

The strengths of metal-oxide memristors are, in outline, those of a mature binary- or few-level non-volatile memory. Retention times of years at room temperature are routinely reported; switching times can reach the nanosecond range; endurance can exceed \(10^{9}\) cycles in the best-engineered stacks; and densities compatible with sub-\SI{10}{\nano\metre} crossbars are in principle achievable~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013, IelminiWong2018}. These properties have made metal-oxide resistive memory the canonical platform for commercial and pre-commercial in-memory computing demonstrations, and the one against which most other families are implicitly benchmarked. Volatile subspecies of the same family are known as well: diffusive metal-filament devices, in which the filament spontaneously disrupts by Ag or Cu nanocluster diffusion after the programming bias is removed, have been used explicitly as fading-memory reservoir elements~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021}. Metal-oxide memristors are therefore not uniformly non-volatile; the literature supports either persistent or relaxing behaviour through controlled choice of materials, stack geometry, and programming protocol.

The weaknesses of the family for high-precision analogue synaptic operation are equally well documented. The conductance modulation in filamentary devices is intrinsically stochastic: the nucleation of the filament, its lateral position within the active area, and the exact cross-section through which it conducts are determined by thermally and electrically driven fluctuations at the nanoscale, and even in carefully engineered stacks a substantial device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle spread in set voltage, reset voltage, and achieved conductance remains~\cite{Sun2019}. The associated non-ideal linearity and asymmetry of the conductance update under identical positive and negative pulses have been repeatedly identified as limiting factors in the numerical accuracy of in-place gradient-based training on metal-oxide crossbars~\cite{IelminiWong2018}.

Multi-level operation is possible but is usually restricted, in practice, to a small number of reliably distinguishable states; obtaining a high-resolution analogue weight from a single filamentary cell remains difficult, and common mitigation strategies rely on multi-cell unit codes or on hybrid digital--analogue representations. These observations do not deny the engineering maturity of the metal-oxide platform. They clarify that the platform is optimised for retention, endurance, and integration density, while analogue linearity, symmetric bidirectional update, and fine-grained timescale engineering are secondary concerns.

\subsection{Phase-Change Memory}\label{subsec:pcm}

A conceptually distinct inorganic family operates through reversible switching between the amorphous and crystalline phases of a chalcogenide alloy. The canonical alloy is \ce{Ge2Sb2Te5}, often abbreviated GST, and a wide range of closely related compositions (doped variants of GST, \ce{Ag-In-Sb-Te}, \ce{Sb2Te}, and so on) exhibit the same essential switching physics. In the crystalline phase, the alloy is an electronically conductive narrow-gap semiconductor; in the amorphous phase it is effectively insulating, with resistance several orders of magnitude higher than the crystalline value. The transition between the two phases is driven thermally: a short, intense \textit{reset} pulse melts a small volume of the alloy and is terminated rapidly enough for the melt to vitrify into the amorphous state, while a longer, lower-amplitude \textit{set} pulse holds the material at a temperature above the crystallisation threshold but below the melting point, allowing the disordered region to re-crystallise~\cite{Sebastian2014, Koelmans2015}. The resulting resistance is not, as in filamentary metal-oxide memristors, the property of a localised channel but of the geometric proportion of crystalline and amorphous phases within the programmed volume.

The technological maturity of phase-change memory is unusually high among memristive device families. It has been under sustained development as a storage-class memory since the 1970s, has been the subject of multiple commercial product generations, and has, over the past decade, become the most credible inorganic platform for analogue in-memory computing. Le Gallo and co-workers demonstrated mixed-precision in-memory arithmetic on PCM arrays with sufficient accuracy to support, jointly with a digital co-processor, the solution of linear systems of practical scale~\cite{LGallo2018}. Ambrogio et al.~\cite{Ambrogio2018} subsequently demonstrated equivalent-accuracy training of modest-scale neural networks using PCM elements as the long-term weight store, paired with a volatile capacitor for fine-grained weight updates. These demonstrations, taken together, establish PCM as the most seriously deployed analogue-memristive substrate for conventional deep-learning workloads, and the benchmark against which any alternative analogue weight element must be evaluated for trained-weight applications.

The constraints of phase-change memory are significant when the device is viewed as a candidate synapse rather than as a weight cell. The reset operation requires local melting of the active volume, and the associated current densities and thermal loads impose both a high per-write energy and a stringent requirement on thermal isolation of neighbouring cells. The amorphous phase produced by quenching is not perfectly stable: its resistance drifts over time according to an approximately power-law relaxation of the amorphous structure, typically of order a few per cent of the baseline per decade in time, which must be compensated by periodic re-reading or by read-time calibration.

Precise analogue programming of intermediate resistance states relies on carefully shaped pulse protocols and on tight thermal control, and is therefore more sensitive to process and operating-condition variation than the corresponding bistable operation. The underlying physics is thermal and structural, which leaves only limited room for chemical tuning of the key timescales: the crystallisation and amorphisation kinetics are set by the alloy composition and the device geometry, both adjustable within modest bounds but neither providing the molecular-level handle on ion transport that the soft-matter systems discussed in the next section offer. PCM is the most compelling analogue in-memory-computing platform currently available, but its strength lies in the high-retention, high-endurance, trained-weight corner, not in the fast, soft, chemically tunable, fading-memory corner pursued here.

\subsection{Spin-Transfer-Torque Magnetic Tunnel Junctions}\label{subsec:stt_mtj}

The third inorganic memristor family departs more sharply from the ion- or atom-rearrangement mechanisms discussed so far. A magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) consists of two ferromagnetic layers separated by a thin insulating tunnel barrier (typically \ce{MgO}). The resistance of the junction depends, through the tunnelling magnetoresistance (TMR) effect, on the relative orientation of the magnetisations of the two layers: it is low when the magnetisations are parallel and high when they are antiparallel. In a spin-transfer-torque (STT) device, the free layer can be switched between the parallel and antiparallel configurations by a spin-polarised tunnel current: conduction electrons traversing the barrier from the fixed reference layer acquire a net spin polarisation, and their subsequent interaction with the free-layer moment exerts a torque sufficient, above a threshold current density, to reverse the free layer. Read and write operations therefore share the same electrical port, and the internal state variable is the magnetic configuration of the free layer rather than a chemical or morphological property of the host material.

The STT--MTJ family is of considerable interest for neuromorphic and unconventional computing for several reasons. Switching times in the nanosecond range, endurance exceeding \(10^{12}\) cycles, write energies per bit in the femtojoule range, and compatibility with back-end-of-line CMOS integration combine to make the device attractive as a low-energy non-volatile element. The magnetic origin of the switching threshold, combined with thermal fluctuations of the free layer in devices close to the superparamagnetic limit, also offers a natural and well-controlled source of stochasticity, which has been exploited in probabilistic-computing and neuromorphic demonstrations: Torrejon et al.~\cite{Torrejon2017} used spin-torque nano-oscillators as dynamical nodes for physical reservoir computing and achieved competitive accuracy on a spoken-digit classification benchmark, and Mizrahi et al.~\cite{Mizrahi2018} used populations of superparamagnetic MTJs as noisy basis functions for neural-style computation. These demonstrations show that the MTJ family is not restricted to digital memory operation and that its dynamical behaviour can itself be a computational resource.

The limitations of the platform are real in the context of fine-grained analogue synaptic operation. A simple STT--MTJ is essentially a two-state device, with the ratio of antiparallel to parallel resistance typically in the range of two to a few; extending this ratio and introducing a larger number of reliably programmable intermediate states requires more elaborate stack engineering (synthetic antiferromagnetic references, perpendicular-anisotropy stacks, or multi-layer configurations) and tighter process control, with corresponding penalties in yield and integration complexity.

The thermal stability of the free layer, which sets the retention, is in direct tension with the switching current, which sets the write energy, and trading one against the other imposes a tight envelope within which the device must operate. The materials involved --- carefully engineered multilayers of ferromagnetic metals, synthetic antiferromagnets, and crystalline \ce{MgO} tunnel barriers --- are demanding in their growth requirements and do not lend themselves to the solution-processable, substrate-agnostic fabrication available to soft-matter devices. STT--MTJs are therefore best thought of as fast, non-volatile, few-state, stochastic elements whose strengths lie in probabilistic computing and in the replacement of small embedded non-volatile memories, rather than as a natural substrate for analogue, multi-timescale synaptic emulation.

\subsection{General Limitations of Inorganic Memristors}\label{subsec:inorganic_limits}

The three families surveyed above differ in mechanism and in their preferred operating regimes, but they share features that constrain their coverage of the neuromorphic hardware problem and motivate a complementary materials investigation.

The first shared constraint is a limited range of \textit{chemical tunability}. Each of the three families is anchored in a small number of canonical host materials --- the transition-metal oxides, the tellurium-based chalcogenide alloys, and a restricted set of engineered ferromagnetic stacks --- whose switching behaviour is determined by the intrinsic physics of these hosts. The available design knobs within each family are primarily geometric (layer thicknesses, active area, electrode contacts), stoichiometric (within narrow compositional windows), or process-related (annealing conditions, forming protocols). Access to the switching physics itself is therefore mediated, in practice, through a handful of well-characterised stacks; it is rarely possible to modify the key energetic or kinetic parameters of the device by substituting one mobile species for another with different binding chemistry, or by exchanging the host polymer for a chemically distinct analogue with a different glass-transition temperature. This is a statement about the structure of the materials space rather than a criticism of any individual demonstration; molecular-level tunability of the binding and transport chemistry is not among the handles that the inorganic platforms expose.

A second and closely related constraint concerns \textit{dynamical timescale engineering}. Each of the three inorganic families admits both non-volatile and, in specific implementations, volatile behaviour: diffusive metal-filament devices in the metal-oxide family supply fading-memory dynamics~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021, Ohno2011}, resistance drift in the amorphous phase of PCM provides a (less well-controlled) relaxation~\cite{Sebastian2014}, and superparamagnetic MTJs introduce stochastic fluctuations on engineered timescales~\cite{Mizrahi2018}. What is more difficult, in each case, is to \textit{systematically} sweep a relaxation time constant over several orders of magnitude within a single materials platform by way of a single chemical variable. The underlying kinetic processes --- vacancy drift in a crystalline oxide, thermally activated crystallisation in a chalcogenide melt, or magnetic relaxation of a ferromagnetic free layer --- are determined by the intrinsic energy landscape of the host, and the span of accessible timescales within each family is correspondingly constrained. For applications such as the heterogeneous reservoir or the multi-timescale transient filter bank described in \cref{subsec:volatile_paradigms}, in which parallel devices with deliberately distinct time constants are required, this constraint is not peripheral but central.

A third constraint concerns \textit{fabrication and integration}. The inorganic families above rely, with few exceptions, on vacuum-based thin-film deposition, on high-temperature anneals, and on top-down lithographic patterning. These workflows are fully compatible with conventional silicon technology, which is an unambiguous strength for dense crossbar arrays integrated on CMOS, but they constrain the substrates on which the devices can be fabricated and the operating temperatures at which they can be processed. Large-area, flexible, biocompatible, or printable platforms --- relevant for soft-electronic, implantable, or distributed-sensor neuromorphic systems --- are not the natural home of these workflows, and adapting them to such contexts requires substantial process engineering. Solution-processable active layers, by contrast, can in principle be deposited on arbitrary substrates at mild temperatures, and their active chemistry can be tuned by varying the composition of the precursor solution. This is a feature of the fabrication physics, not a deficiency of any specific inorganic demonstration, but it matters for the applications considered in the later chapters.

A fourth constraint is the mechanistic stochasticity inherent to filamentary and phase-change operation. In reservoir computing, stochasticity can usefully contribute to reservoir diversity provided it manifests as inter-device heterogeneity rather than intra-device instability (in the sense of \cref{subsec:memristor_params}). The inorganic families above tend to exhibit both, and disentangling them requires considerable process control. A device class in which inter-device heterogeneity can be introduced deliberately through composition, with intra-device stability preserved by the collective character of the conduction mechanism, would offer a cleaner route to the controlled-variability operating regime.

Taken together, these constraints point to a gap: analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on soft, solution-processable platforms is not where the inorganic families are strongest. The next section asks whether organic and polymeric ion-mediated materials can fill that gap. \cref{fig:inorganic_comparison_matrix} summarises the position of each inorganic family and forward-references the soft-ionic route.

\section{Organic Materials for Memristive Applications}\label{sec:organic_memristors}

The inorganic memristor families validate the broader memristive and neuromorphic programme, and each is strong in part of the problem. What they share is a restricted palette of chemical handles and a workflow best suited to rigid, vacuum-processed, high-temperature-compatible substrates. Analogue multi-state operation, ion-mediated internal dynamics, controllable timescales across several orders of magnitude, and compatibility with soft, conformable, or biointerfaced form factors sit less comfortably in that materials space.

Organic materials are attractive where that combination is required. The point is not that organic devices are automatically cheaper, cleaner, or more reproducible than their inorganic counterparts; such claims are often unsupported or context-dependent. The relevant fact is narrower: in organic and polymeric systems, the chemistry that sets electronic transport, ionic transport, redox activity, and relaxation kinetics is directly accessible to molecular design. Tuning these properties is a matter of synthesis and formulation rather than repeated redesign of a rigid thin-film stack.

\cref{subsec:organic_advantages} identifies the features that distinguish organic materials from inorganic platforms for memristive and neuromorphic applications, and states what those features do and do not guarantee. \cref{subsec:organic_mechanisms} surveys the main physical mechanisms invoked to explain organic memristive behaviour, clarifies that they do not form a single mechanism class, and gives particular weight to ion-mediated and mixed ionic--electronic operation, the route most relevant to the remainder of the thesis.

\cref{subsec:organic_history} provides a concise historical survey from the early electrochemical and polymer-film demonstrations through three-terminal electrochemical-transistor synapses, two-terminal redox-based biomimetic devices, and stretchable and molecular implementations of the last decade. The aim is to identify what the field had solved, and which combinations of features were still missing, when this work began. The section closes on the question that motivates the next one: within the organic memristor literature, which architecture is most suitable for controllable, reversible, ion-mediated neuromorphic dynamics?

\subsection{Advantages of Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_advantages}

The distinctive feature of organic materials, in the context of memristive and neuromorphic device engineering, is the breadth of chemical freedom available at the level of the active layer. A semiconducting polymer can be designed by choice of backbone, side-chain functionalisation, and molecular weight distribution; an ion-transport host can be tuned by choice of chelating motif, polymer topology, and glass-transition temperature; a redox-active moiety can be selected from a large library of molecular and polymeric species with characterised oxidation potentials and known redox reversibilities; a mobile ionic species can be chosen independently of the host on the basis of charge, radius, and Lewis acidity.

The relevant point is not that any individual organic device is automatically superior to an inorganic equivalent, but that the number of orthogonal chemical handles available for engineering its internal-state physics is considerably larger. In particular, the ionic component of the active layer --- the identity of the mobile cation and anion, the nature of the solvating environment, and the segmental dynamics of the host polymer --- is directly under synthetic and formulation control in a way that is not available, at the equivalent level of granularity, in the inorganic platforms discussed in \cref{sec:inorganic_memristors}.

Solution processability is the second distinguishing feature. A large fraction of organic active layers relevant for memristive operation --- conjugated polymers, hyperbranched polyethers, polyelectrolytes, redox-active polymers, and their blends --- can be deposited from solution by techniques ranging from spin coating and blade coating in the laboratory to inkjet, spray, and roll-to-roll processes on larger areas. This is a feature of the fabrication physics, not a promise of low cost or industrial scalability by itself, and its practical benefits depend on the material and solvent system used. What it does guarantee is that the active layer composition can be varied continuously by mixing precursor solutions in a single step, which is well suited to composite devices in which an electronic component and an ionic component coexist. Adjusting polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor ratios by changing the solution recipe, rather than redesigning a multilayer stack, is the fabrication-level counterpart of the chemical freedom described above.

A closely related feature is the mild thermal budget required for processing. Most organic memristive layers are fabricated at temperatures below one hundred and fifty degrees Celsius, frequently below one hundred, and the top contacts can be deposited through shadow masks by thermal evaporation without high-temperature post-anneals. This is compatible with flexible plastic substrates, paper, textiles, elastomers, and biological tissues, and it places no strict lower bound on the substrate thickness beyond the mechanical requirements of handling. The corresponding properties of the finished device --- mechanical flexibility under bending, conformability over curved surfaces, and in some cases stretchability --- follow naturally from this processing regime rather than from any intrinsic mechanical property of a single active molecule. Demonstrations of flexible and stretchable organic synaptic devices~\cite{Kim2018, Park2020} illustrate that mechanical compliance is accessible at the device level with standard organic-electronic fabrication, which is relevant for wearable, implantable, or conformal neuromorphic systems of the kind envisaged under the \emph{nervetronic}~\cite{Park2020} heading.

These three features --- chemical freedom, solution processability, mild thermal budget --- define a materials space different from the inorganic one. Low cost, reproducibility, biocompatibility, and long lifetime still depend on the specific materials, solvents, contact metals, encapsulation, and operating conditions. The claim is only that organic materials expose handles on the internal-state physics of the device that the inorganic class does not, and that those handles operate through chemistry rather than through re-engineering of a rigid thin-film stack.

Two consequences matter for the argument developed in \cref{sec:memristor_history,sec:inorganic_memristors}. First, organic systems can support finely tunable analogue responses because the microscopic mechanisms underlying the switching behaviour --- charge trapping on molecular sites, redox activity of a macromolecular host, displacement of mobile ions in a polymer matrix --- are typically distributed over many sites and therefore admit intermediate states rather than a sharp binary transition.

Second, device relaxation timescales can be engineered through chemistry. Ion transport in a polymer electrolyte depends on the binding strength between the mobile ion and the polymer, on the segmental mobility of the host, and on the concentration of mobile species; each of these parameters is a design variable in an organic composite. Device timescales ranging from milliseconds to many seconds, matched to biological short-term plasticity and slow temporal signals, can therefore be reached within one materials family by compositional changes. This feature makes organic ion-mediated systems attractive for the volatile, fading-memory paradigms introduced in \cref{subsec:volatile_paradigms}, where controllable relaxation rather than maximal retention is the relevant figure of merit.

Each demonstration occupies a specific point in this materials space with its own strengths and compromises. The history of the field (\cref{subsec:organic_history}) shows that combining reversibility, endurance, multi-state analogue control, and interpretable ionic physics in a single two-terminal organic device remained difficult well into the last decade. The argument here is not that organic memristors solve every problem left open by inorganic ones, but that ion-mediated polymer composites give access to chemical controls that are not available on the inorganic side.

\subsection{Mechanisms in Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_mechanisms}

Unlike \emph{phase-change memory} or \emph{valence-change memory}, the label \emph{organic memristor} does not designate a single mechanism class. Memristive behaviour in organic devices has been credibly attributed to several distinct physical processes, differing in the internal state variable modulated, in reversibility, in timescales, and in the degree of analogue control achievable. This subsection identifies the main families, states what each one offers and costs, and locates the ion-mediated family among them.

The first mechanism family is charge trapping and detrapping in an insulating or weakly conducting matrix. In this family, the active layer contains a dispersed population of deep or shallow traps --- associated with molecular defect sites, with embedded nanoparticles, or with the interfaces between incompatible polymer phases --- and the device state is set by the occupation of those traps under an applied bias. The resistance change is produced by the modulation of the electronic transport through the matrix as the local space-charge environment is altered by the trapped carriers.

This family is attractive because trapping is a generic process readily observed in disordered organic semiconductors, and because switching voltages can be kept modest. It typically offers limited analogue control: the state space is often effectively binary, the dependence of conductance on trap occupation is non-monotonic in some devices, and the microscopic identity of the traps is frequently hard to pin down, which constrains the interpretability of the mechanism. Retention is determined by the depth of the traps relative to the thermal energy, and is difficult to set from composition alone.

The second family is redox switching, in which the active layer or a component of it contains a species whose oxidation state is changed reversibly under bias. The redox-active component can be a discrete molecule embedded in a polymer host, a redox-active polymer used as the active layer itself, or a redox-active moiety covalently bound to a conjugated backbone. The device state is set by the distribution of oxidised and reduced species, which modifies the local electronic structure and therefore the conductance. Redox switching offers a clear mechanistic picture: the internal state variable is the oxidation state of a well-defined chemical species, the electrochemical potentials that drive the transitions are experimentally accessible, and in favourable cases the redox process is reversible over many cycles. Several important organic memristors operate on this principle, including the family of redox-active conjugated polymer systems~\cite{Liu2016, Goswami2017, Goswami2020} and early electrochemical demonstrations based on coordination complexes~\cite{Zakhidov2010}.

The costs, in the organic context, are twofold. First, not every redox process is cleanly reversible: side reactions, dimerisation, and slow degradation of the active species limit the endurance of many systems. Second, purely redox-based devices tend to operate as bistable or few-state systems unless the redox response is distributed over an ensemble of sites; when distributed, the analogue response is recovered, but interpretability begins to shade into the statistics of the ensemble rather than into the chemistry of an individual switch.

The third family is conformational or structural switching, in which the active layer contains a molecular species whose geometry, isomerisation state, or aggregation state can be modulated by the applied field, by local heating, or by coupled electrochemical processes. Classical examples include photochromic or electrochromic switches that couple to the electrical transport, and specifically designed bistable molecular architectures~\cite{Goswami2017, Goswami2020} in which the conductance of the molecule depends on its conformation. These systems offer very high levels of molecular design control and in favourable cases very high precision in the switching behaviour, since the state is set by a chemically well-defined geometric rearrangement. The costs are that the relevant molecular synthesis is frequently demanding, that the switching dynamics are tied to the kinetics of a specific molecular rearrangement which may or may not match the timescales of interest, and that scaling the behaviour from single-molecule to device-level averages introduces its own set of issues.

The fourth family, and the one most directly relevant to the thesis, is ionic redistribution in mixed ionic--electronic systems. Here the active layer contains both an electronic conductor --- typically a conjugated polymer --- and a population of mobile ions carried by a suitable ionic host. Under applied bias, the mobile ions migrate and accumulate near the electrode interfaces or within specific regions of the active layer, where they modify the local electronic structure of the semiconductor by a combination of interfacial doping, electrostatic band bending, and electrochemical reduction or oxidation of the polymer. The resulting conductance change is continuous, multi-state, and tied directly to the amount and spatial distribution of displaced ionic charge.

The microscopic picture is closely related to the operation of light-emitting electrochemical cells~\cite{Pei1995, deMello1998, deMello2002, Matyba2009, vanReenen2010, Edman2008, Mardegan2021}, in which ionic redistribution under a driving bias creates an in-situ doped junction, and to the operation of organic electrochemical transistors (OECTs), in which a gate-driven ion exchange between an electrolyte and a conducting polymer modulates the channel current. What distinguishes the memristive mode from its LEC and OECT counterparts is that, in a memristive two-terminal device, the driving bias itself plays the role of the gate, and the residual state of the ionic distribution after the bias is removed carries the memory.

This fourth family is central to the thesis. The state variable is simple to identify --- the position of mobile ions in a polymer host --- and its dynamics are governed by ionic transport kinetics that depend on the polymer binding environment and on the ion chemistry. Relaxation, when the bias is removed, is a fading-memory process with a characteristic time constant that can be estimated from the physical chemistry of the composite and engineered through cation identity, host polymer, salt concentration, and film morphology. These variables are directly accessible to the materials chemist, unlike the trap depths or redox overpotentials that control relaxation in the charge-trapping and redox families. The costs are that ionic motion is slower than the purely electronic processes underlying filamentary or charge-trapping switching, and that side reactions between the mobile ions and the electrodes must be controlled through chemistry and contact-metal choice.

A fifth family, included for completeness, is filamentary conduction in which metallic species --- typically \ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+} from an active top electrode --- migrate through the organic matrix to form conductive bridges analogous to those of inorganic CBRAM. Such devices inherit both the strengths (large on/off ratios, low switching voltages) and weaknesses (stochastic filament formation, intra-device instability) of their inorganic counterparts (\cref{subsec:metal_oxide_memristors}), and illustrate that the same mechanism can be realised on either side of the organic--inorganic boundary; the distinctively organic mechanisms are those in which the chemical specificity of the components contributes to the internal-state physics.

Among the organic options, ion-mediated mixed ionic--electronic systems couple an identifiable internal state variable to kinetics that can be engineered through chemistry. Within that subset, polymeric ionic hosts --- polymer electrolytes --- add a useful feature: ionic transport kinetics are controlled by host segmental motion, which can itself be tuned through backbone choice and glass-transition temperature. Polymer-electrolyte composites, in which a conjugated semiconductor is blended with an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host loaded with a dissociated salt, are therefore developed in detail in the next section.

\subsection{Historical Development of Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_history}

The organic memristor literature of the last three decades is not a single device lineage. It is a set of parallel streams rooted in different mechanisms and architectures. The useful question is which problems those streams solved, and which combinations of features were still missing when the present work began.

An early strand repurposed the slow, history-dependent ionic redistribution of light-emitting electrochemical cells as a memristive functionality. Zakhidov and co-workers reported a \emph{light-emitting memristor}~\cite{Zakhidov2010} in which an ionic \(\ce{[Ru(bpy)3]^{2+}}\) complex supplied both the emissive species and the mobile ionic component; its significance was mechanistic --- it identified ionic redistribution in a polymer host as a legitimate route to memristive behaviour --- rather than as a practical memory element. A parallel strand reported resistive switching in simple polymer thin films such as poly(vinyl alcohol)~\cite{Lei2014}, establishing that memristive phenomena were accessible in inexpensive polymer matrices but typically binary, mechanistically opaque, and not analogue.

A more productive strand adopted the organic electrochemical transistor as the architectural platform for synaptic emulation, benefiting from prior bioelectronics investment in OECT design. Gkoupidenis and colleagues showed that PEDOT:PSS OECTs could reproduce short-term plasticity, paired-pulse facilitation, and long-term potentiation and depression under appropriate pulse protocols~\cite{Gkoupidenis2015}, and later extended this to devices with global electrolyte connectivity~\cite{Gkoupidenis2017}. Xu and colleagues produced core--sheath nanowire synaptic transistors~\cite{Xu2016}, and van de Burgt, Fuller, and co-workers developed the non-volatile organic electrochemical neuromorphic device (ENODe)~\cite{VanDeBurgt2017, Fuller2019}, in which a dedicated non-volatile reservoir supplied a linearly updateable synaptic weight. Taken together, these devices demonstrated that organic electrochemistry could support the full range of synaptic learning rules with low operating voltages and good analogue control, and clarified the central role of ionic exchange between a polymer electrolyte and a conducting polymer channel~\cite{VanDeBurgt2018}. OECT devices with halide-perovskite gates~\cite{Seo2019} extended the accessible timescales.

The three-terminal line is intellectually important but architecturally distinct: an OECT synaptic device comprises a gated channel, a separate electrolyte, and, in the non-volatile variant, a dedicated programming reservoir, so read and write are cleanly decoupled but each synaptic element carries at least a third terminal and a contacted electrolyte volume. A simpler two-terminal element whose ionic physics is contained within the active layer remains an attractive complement, especially for the heterogeneous reservoir and coincidence-detection paradigms of \cref{subsec:volatile_paradigms}.

The two-terminal line of organic memristor work proceeded in parallel. Liu and co-workers reported a biomimicking redox organic memristor~\cite{Liu2016} in which an organic redox system embedded in a polymer matrix produced, in a passive two-terminal device, a short-term-to-long-term memory transition under repeated stimulation, with coexisting short-term and long-term regimes. The same work illustrated the characteristic difficulty of the redox-driven route: redox processes are not always cleanly reversible, and the long-term regime sometimes reflects partially irreversible transformations rather than controlled state evolution. How to obtain analogue, reversible, multi-state behaviour in a two-terminal organic device without relying on permanent redox changes was, at the time, an open problem.

Two further lines should be noted. Stretchable and mechanically compliant organic synaptic devices --- Kim and co-workers' bioinspired flexible artificial afferent nerve~\cite{Kim2018} and Park and co-workers' review of \emph{nervetronic} applications~\cite{Park2020} --- showed that the mechanical advantages of organic processing can be realised at device level, and framed conformable, skin-mounted, or biointerfaced applications where soft-matter devices are well suited. Molecular memristors with engineered redox and conformational behaviour --- Goswami and co-workers' metal-coordinated azo-aromatic layers with high on/off contrast, robust endurance, and near-analogue multi-state operation~\cite{Goswami2017, Goswami2020} --- showed what molecular-level design can achieve, at the cost of demanding synthesis that places them in a different practical regime from the composite devices considered below.

Hybrid systems also sit alongside these mechanism families. Perovskite--organic hybrids~\cite{Xiao2016} exploit the efficient ionic conduction of halide perovskites together with organic charge-transport layers, conjugated polymer systems with non-volatile resistive-switching character have been explored~\cite{Shih2017}, and organic electrochemical reservoir computing~\cite{Cucchi2021} has shown that mixed ionic--electronic organic devices can act as physical-reservoir elements without eliminating their volatility. This last result is especially relevant here because it treats volatility as a useful feature rather than a defect.

By the early 2020s, organic neuromorphic electronics had become a recognised subfield with several active design lines~\cite{VanDeBurgt2018}. Ion-mediated mechanisms had been identified in both three-terminal and two-terminal architectures; analogue, linear, symmetric programming had been demonstrated in the electrochemically gated family; short-term-to-long-term memory transitions had been demonstrated in two-terminal redox devices; mechanical compliance had been demonstrated in stretchable synaptic devices; and molecular-level control had been demonstrated in engineered molecular memristors.

What the record did \emph{not} show was the consolidation of these features into a single two-terminal platform. The three-terminal electrochemical family achieved reversibility and analogue control at the cost of architectural complexity; the two-terminal redox family achieved architectural simplicity at the cost of full reversibility; the molecular family achieved precision at the cost of synthetic tractability; the stretchable family demonstrated mechanical compatibility without committing to a particular mechanism. The gap addressed here is a two-terminal, reversible, multi-state, ion-mediated device with interpretable chemistry, built from standard organic-electronic components. The route pursued below is a composite of a conjugated semiconducting polymer with an ion-conducting polymer host and a dissociated alkali-metal salt. The parallel mechanism-family streams up to the early 2020s are collected in \cref{fig:organic_timeline}.

\section{Polymer Electrolyte Composites: The Enabling Strategy}\label{sec:polymer_electrolyte_composites}

\cref{sec:organic_memristors} narrowed the organic memristor field to devices in which ionic redistribution in a polymer host couples to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor. The remaining design decision is architectural: how should the ionic and electronic functions be organised inside the active layer? Three-terminal electrochemical transistors place the ion reservoir in a separate, contacted electrolyte volume and gate the channel through an independent electrode. Two-terminal devices must integrate both functions into a single stack driven by a single bias. The strategy used here is a \emph{composite}: a semiconducting conjugated polymer blended with an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host and a dissociated alkali-metal salt, processed from a common solution onto a standard two-terminal electrode stack.

The case for this strategy has three parts. \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers} recalls the physical chemistry of ion-conducting polymers: oxygen-rich hosts, alkali-cation coordination, transport mechanisms, and composition. \cref{subsec:lec_precedent} introduces polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells as precedent for stable, reversible, field-driven coupling between ionic migration and electronic transport in a soft organic active layer. \cref{subsec:composite_rationale} then gives the rationale for the composite approach: separating electronic and ionic functions, treating the salt as an independent variable, using cation identity and composition as timescale controls, and building a family of chemically distinct composites that can act as a temporal-kernel bank rather than as competing memories.

\subsection{Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles}\label{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}

An ion-conducting polymer, in the sense used here, is a macromolecular host in which dissolved or dispersed ionic species are mobile under an applied electric field without requiring a liquid solvent phase. The field was opened by the recognition, in the late 1970s and early 1980s, that alkali-metal salts could be dissolved in poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) to form solid complexes that conduct ions at macroscopically measurable rates~\cite{Armand1989, Berthier1983, Gray1991}. Solid polymer electrolytes were developed mainly for lithium batteries, but the principles relevant here are the ones that make polymer electrolytes tunable memristive media: cation coordination, segmental motion, host crystallinity, and composition.

The central chemical feature of a polyether host is the presence of a high density of ether oxygens along the polymer backbone or its side chains. In PEO itself, the oxygen atoms are spaced at regular intervals along a \(-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{O}\!-\) repeat unit, producing a linear backbone whose conformation in the amorphous phase allows each cation to coordinate simultaneously to several ether oxygens drawn from one or more polymer chains~\cite{Berthier1983, Lightfoot1993}. In hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts of the Hybrane family, the oxygen density is provided instead by a combination of ether and ester side-chain groups grafted onto a branched polymer core.

In both classes of host, the relevant chemical role of the oxygen atoms is the same: they act as hard Lewis bases that solvate alkali cations, stabilise the dissociated state of the salt, and determine the local binding environment in which the cation resides. The anion is held more weakly, primarily by electrostatic compensation of the cation, and typically exhibits higher mobility than the cation within the same host, especially when it is a soft, charge-delocalised species such as triflate (\ce{CF3SO3-}) or bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TFSI, \ce{N(SO2CF3)2-}).

Ion transport in such hosts is most naturally described by a combination of three microscopically distinct mechanisms. In the \emph{vehicular} picture, a cation together with its primary solvation shell of coordinated oxygens moves through the polymer matrix as an effective ionic vehicle; long-range transport requires the coordinated oxygens themselves to move, which couples ionic transport to polymer motion. In the \emph{hopping} or site-to-site picture, the cation is transferred between nominally equivalent coordination sites on the host by a local rearrangement of the oxygens that does not require macroscopic transport of the host itself; this process is closely related to the Grotthuss-type description familiar from protonic conductors, although the chemistry of the transfer event is different.

In the \emph{segmental-motion-assisted} picture, the reorganisation of short polymer segments around the cation --- segmental motion of the backbone or of the side chain --- generates and destroys coordination sites near the ion in such a way that the effective diffusion of the ion is enabled by the local dynamics of the host. These pictures are not mutually exclusive; in a realistic polyether host, transport is a hybrid of all three, and their relative weights depend on the host chemistry, on the cation, on the salt concentration, and on temperature~\cite{Berthier1983, Gray1991}. For the devices studied here, the important point is that ionic transport is set by the coupling between polymer dynamics and ionic chemistry, not by the polymer chemistry alone.

This coupling has a direct consequence for device dynamics. The macroscopic ionic mobility in a polymer electrolyte is, to a first approximation, controlled by the segmental mobility of the host, which is in turn controlled by the glass transition temperature \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\) and by the degree to which the host remains amorphous under operating conditions. Below \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\), segmental motion is effectively frozen and ionic conduction collapses; above \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\), segmental motion is activated and ionic transport becomes measurable, with a characteristic Vogel--Tamman--Fulcher rather than Arrhenius temperature dependence in many systems~\cite{Gray1991}.

Crystalline regions, where they exist, contribute little to conduction and may trap ions in ordered environments; for this reason, low-crystallinity or fully amorphous hosts are preferred for ion-transport applications. Engineering the host for a low \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\) and a low crystalline fraction is a recognised design strategy in solid polymer electrolytes, pursued through copolymerisation, branching, and plasticisation, and hyperbranched architectures such as the Hybrane family are explicitly motivated in part by the suppression of crystallinity that branching entails. For memristive operation at room temperature, the host should remain amorphous and segmentally mobile under normal ambient conditions, and these properties should be accessible through synthetic design at the host level without recourse to external plasticisers.

The strength of the interaction between a cation and the oxygen donor atoms of the host is determined by the chemical identity of the cation, and it is here that hard--soft acid--base (HSAB) reasoning~\cite{Pearson1963} becomes a useful intermediate-level framing. The ether and ester oxygens of polyether and polyester-amide hosts are hard Lewis bases: their donor electron pairs are localised, their polarisability is low, and the coordination bonds they form with cations are correspondingly dominated by electrostatic rather than covalent contributions. Among alkali cations, \ce{Li+} is the hardest Lewis acid --- small radius, high charge density, highly localised charge --- and therefore interacts most strongly with a hard-base oxygen donor. \ce{Na+} and \ce{K+} are progressively softer in this sequence, with larger radii, lower charge densities, and weaker electrostatic coupling to a given oxygen environment.

The practical consequence is that, within a common polyether host at a fixed composition, the characteristic residence time of the cation at a given coordination site, and therefore the kinetics of its displacement under an applied bias, are expected to follow the ordering \ce{Li+} > \ce{Na+} > \ce{K+} in terms of coordination strength. In dynamical terms, this ordering is expected to map onto a corresponding ordering of the fading-memory timescale of the device, with harder cations producing slower relaxation and softer cations producing faster relaxation. The HSAB framing is used here as a qualitative organising principle: it anticipates a ranking of timescales without fixing quantitative values. The quantitative confirmation of this ordering against real measurements is reserved for the experimental chapters.

Composition provides the second independent family of design knobs. A polymer electrolyte is characterised not only by its host chemistry and its cation identity, but also by the ratio of salt to host and, when it is embedded in a larger composite, by the ratio of polymer electrolyte to semiconducting phase. These ratios control, respectively, the concentration of mobile ionic species available for redistribution under a bias, and the effective volume fraction of the electronic and ionic phases within the active layer.

The two ratios are not redundant: the salt-to-host ratio sets the number and the spatial density of cation coordination sites that are occupied, while the host-to-semiconductor ratio sets the volume available for ionic transport and for electronic percolation through the conjugated phase. In the composite devices considered in later chapters, the polymer-to-semiconductor mass fraction and the salt-to-semiconductor mass fraction are treated as two independent axes of composition, and their effects on the dynamical response of the device are distinguished experimentally. Composition is therefore a real physical-chemistry knob on the ionic dynamics, not a parameter selected for convenience.

The combination of these considerations motivates the specific host choices made in the thesis. Hybrane DEO750 8500, a hyperbranched polyester-amide with a high density of ether and ester oxygens on its side chains, offers an amorphous, low-crystallinity host that has already been validated in the same material family used in \cref{ch:poc} and in its associated light-emitting precedent~\cite{Mardegan2021}; it is used as the ion-transport host in the proof-of-concept Super Yellow (SY)/Hybrane/lithium triflate (LiOTf, \ce{LiCF3SO3}) composite, where it is paired with a triflate salt to demonstrate the full set of memristive functionalities in a single device.

PEO is introduced as the central polyether host for the broader comparative corpus explored later in the thesis, where it plays a chemically analogous role: its ether oxygens coordinate alkali-metal cations, its segmental motion enables ionic transport, and its compositional and cation-identity dependencies will be varied systematically. The intention of the thesis is not to argue that one of these hosts is superior to the other, but to use them for complementary purposes: Hybrane as the host in which the full synaptic functionality is established on a single exemplar, and PEO as the host in which composition- and cation-driven dynamics are explored across a broader comparative set. The shared physical-chemistry principles developed in this subsection apply to both.

\subsection{Light-Emitting Electrochemical Cells as Precedent}\label{subsec:lec_precedent}

The argument developed so far relies on a physical claim: ionic redistribution in an organic polymer host can be coupled to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor in a reversible, stable, and reproducible way. Several decades of work on polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs) support that claim. In LECs, the same coupling is used for electroluminescence rather than memory, and the relevant physics has been characterised in detail. LECs are invoked here as a \emph{material-level precedent}, not because the thesis is concerned with light emission.

An LEC in its canonical form~\cite{Pei1995} is a two-terminal sandwich device in which a conjugated light-emitting polymer is blended, from a single solution, with an ion-conducting polymer and a dissociated salt. Under an applied bias, ions in the host redistribute: cations drift toward the cathode and anions toward the anode, producing regions of electrochemically doped polymer in the neighbourhood of each contact. The doped regions supply, respectively, electrons and holes to the undoped bulk, and the resulting p-i-n-like profile develops dynamically in response to the applied bias. The intrinsic region between the two doped fronts is where electron--hole recombination occurs and where electroluminescence is generated.

Microscopically, the operation of an LEC has been the subject of long-running debate between an electrochemical-doping picture, in which the doping of the polymer is the dominant effect, and an electrodynamic picture, in which the accumulation of ions at the interfaces acts primarily to reduce the charge-injection barriers. Detailed modelling~\cite{deMello1998, deMello2002} and experimental studies~\cite{Matyba2009, Edman2008, Wagberg2008} clarified the conditions under which each picture dominates, and a unifying framework~\cite{vanReenen2010} subsequently showed that the two views are not contradictory but correspond to different injection regimes: the electrodynamic picture applies when charge injection is barrier-limited and ionic accumulation restructures the interfaces, while the electrochemical-doping picture applies when injection is ohmic and the doped regions themselves dominate the transport. Both mechanisms involve the same underlying ionic redistribution in the polymer host; they differ in which consequence of that redistribution controls the measurable electrical behaviour. Complementary studies of electrochemical side reactions~\cite{Fang2008} have identified the chemical routes by which LECs degrade and have informed strategies to suppress them.

Two aspects of this precedent are directly relevant to the thesis. The first is that LEC operation demonstrates, across several orders of magnitude of applied bias and over operational lifetimes that can reach hundreds or thousands of hours, that ionic redistribution in a polymer host coexists stably with electronic transport in a conjugated polymer phase. The ions do not, in general, undergo irreversible side reactions that destroy the device within a few cycles; the conjugated polymer does not, in general, suffer irreversible electrochemical degradation under the normal operating window; and the interfaces between the polymer composite and the metallic contacts can be engineered, through choice of metal and of operating protocol, to support repeated injection and withdrawal of both charge and coordinated ions.

These empirical findings establish the feasibility of stable mixed ionic--electronic composite operation. For a memristive device based on the same composite design, they give a simple plausibility argument: if ionic redistribution in a polymer host is stable under LEC operating conditions, then chemistry does not rule out its use as the internal state variable of a two-terminal memristive device.

The second relevant aspect is the role of the solid electrolyte. In both LECs and memristive composites, the electrolyte is not the electroluminescent or electronically conducting component; its contribution comes through the redistribution of mobile ions under bias. The difference lies in how that redistribution is used. In an LEC, the driving bias is held during operation so that the doped p-i-n profile is maintained and emission is sustained. In a memristive device, the bias sets an internal ionic distribution whose residual after the bias is removed carries the memory of the stimulation. The electrolyte chemistry, its coupling to the conjugated host, and its stability under field-driven operation are governed by the same principles in both cases. The LEC literature therefore provides a well-characterised testbed for the material chemistry used here.

The strongest precedent is the specific case of LECs built on the same material family used for the proof-of-concept device in \cref{ch:poc}. Mardegan and co-workers~\cite{Mardegan2021} reported that hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts of the Hybrane family, blended with a conjugated light-emitting polymer, support LEC operation with long operational lifetimes and well-controlled electroluminescence. That result validates the same semiconducting-polymer / ion-conducting-host / salt composite architecture later used here for a memristive device: the constituent polymers, solvent system, processing protocol, and operating conditions are directly comparable. The Chapter 2 device is built on the same Hybrane-class host, so the feasibility of reversible field-driven ionic redistribution in that host class is transferred from established LEC materials knowledge into a new device function.

The LEC precedent shows that stable coupling between ionic and electronic functions is achievable in an organic composite. It does not guarantee the synaptic behaviour required of a memristive device: analogue multi-state programming, short- and long-term memory regimes, and clean relaxation kinetics. \cref{ch:poc} tests that question for the SY/Hybrane/LiOTf system; the comparative chapter extends it to a PEO-based host across compositions and cations without assuming that every chemically plausible combination reproduces the full Chapter 2 behaviour.

\subsection{Rationale for the Composite Approach}\label{subsec:composite_rationale}

The two preceding subsections set out the physical-chemistry principles of ion-conducting polymers and the LEC precedent for stable field-driven ionic redistribution in a soft organic device. The central design decision is to avoid a single material that must supply electronic conduction, ionic transport, and controlled coupling at once. Instead, the active layer is a \emph{composite} in which each function is supplied by a different component.

A conjugated polymer provides the electronic transport pathway; an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host provides the ionic-transport matrix and the coordination environment for the mobile cation; and a dissociated alkali-metal salt provides the mobile ionic species itself. The three components are blended from a common solution and deposited as a single thin film between two electrodes. The memristive response of the device is then produced by the coupling between the electronic transport of the conjugated phase and the residual ionic distribution in the ion-conducting host, and its dynamics are set by the chemistry of each component and by the composition of the blend.

The advantage is that the engineering problem becomes three chemistry problems. The conjugated polymer supplies electronic transport and can be chosen from the established set of conjugated semiconductors used in organic electronics. The oxygen-rich polymer host supplies ionic transport and brings the coordination chemistry and segmental dynamics discussed in \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}. The salt supplies the mobile ions, with cation and anion chosen independently from their respective chemical series.

Seeking a single monolithic material that supplies all three functions at once is more constrained: adjusting one property tends to change the others. The composite approach restores some independence. Each component can be varied while the others are held approximately fixed, and the resulting changes in device dynamics can be attributed, with reasonable confidence, to the component that was changed.

This decoupling matters most for the salt. Although it occupies only a small fraction of the active layer, it strongly affects device dynamics. In a composite with a common host polymer, the choice of cation sets the strength of the coordination environment that the mobile ion experiences, and therefore the kinetics of its redistribution under bias. Hard Lewis acid cations such as \ce{Li+} are coordinated strongly to the ether oxygens of the host and are therefore difficult to displace and slow to relax; softer cations such as \ce{Na+} and \ce{K+} are coordinated less strongly and are therefore expected to respond on shorter timescales, as anticipated in \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}.

The anion enters differently. In an oxygen-rich ion-conducting host at a fixed cation, the anion is held mainly by electrostatic compensation of the cation and is typically a soft, charge-delocalised species. Its mobility is usually higher than that of the cation and its residence time on any given host site is correspondingly shorter. The cation--anion mobility asymmetry is therefore built into the salt chemistry in this host environment, rather than being an accidental property of a particular device.

This asymmetry can be used. If the cation relaxes slowly and the anion relaxes quickly, the composite carries two distinguishable memory timescales within the same active layer: a fast component associated mainly with anion redistribution at the interfaces, and a slow component associated mainly with cation redistribution in the bulk of the ion-conducting host. The relative contribution of each component depends on the amplitude and duration of the applied bias and on the read protocol, and separating the two regimes experimentally is not trivial. The chemical origin, however, is clear. The result is a materials-level analogue of the coexistence of short-term and long-term plasticity regimes introduced in \cref{subsec:plasticity}: not the same microscopic mechanism, but the same functional requirement that a single physical element carry both fast-decaying and slower state variables. In the composite architecture, that coexistence follows from the salt chemistry and does not require an additional device structure.

Composition adds another set of variables. Within a given host--salt combination, the polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions independently control the ionic content and the ionic-transport volume, and therefore affect both the amplitude and the rate of ionic redistribution under a given bias. A composite with a higher salt fraction provides more mobile ions and a stronger ionic response per unit volume; a composite with a higher polyether fraction provides a larger segmentally-mobile matrix in which ions can rearrange. These two knobs are not equivalent, and in principle they should help separate the amplitude of the programmable conductance change from the kinetics with which that change develops and relaxes. The comparative chapter tests that separation experimentally; here the point is that composition is an independent variable of the composite architecture, not a nuisance parameter to be tuned to a single optimum.

One-step blend processing makes systematic compositional investigation practical. Because the semiconductor, the host polymer, and the salt are all soluble in a common organic solvent, a composite device of arbitrary composition can be produced by mixing the three stock solutions in the desired proportions and depositing the blend by spin coating or blade coating onto a patterned electrode. Changing composition means changing a mixing ratio, not redesigning the stack. The fabrication effort per composition is therefore modest, and the difference between nominally identical compositions is controlled by a small number of mixing and deposition parameters rather than by the reproducibility of a multi-step process. This makes it practical to build a set of compositions and cations within a common host and characterise them on a common experimental basis.

The main chemical variables of the composite --- mobile cation, anion, polymer fraction, and salt fraction --- are not just materials choices that make a device better or worse. They act on the internal-state physics of the device, especially on the characteristic timescale of its fading memory. The cation sets a default timescale through its coordination strength; the composition scales the amplitude and the rate at which the ionic response develops; the resulting device, read with a standard electrical protocol, exhibits a dynamical response whose characteristic time reflects those choices. Cation identity and composition are therefore computational design knobs as well as chemical ones: they select the fading-memory constant that the device presents to its circuit environment. The experimental chapters quantify how composition and cation chemistry map onto measurable device dynamics.

A further consequence is that the composite approach supports a distinctive computational use pattern that would be awkward to obtain from a homogeneous material. Because the three chemical variables above can be varied independently and because the fabrication is a single-step blend deposition, a set of chemically distinct composites can be prepared, each with a different expected timescale, and assembled together as a small family of devices. The members of the family share the same electronic transport chemistry and the same host polymer but differ in composition or in cation identity; their dynamical responses, as anticipated by the physical chemistry above, are expected to span a range of fading-memory timescales.

Such a family, when read out through a common circuit, acts as a bank of temporal kernels with different decay constants. That is the substrate needed for the heterogeneous physical reservoir computing, coincidence-detection, and multi-timescale transient filtering paradigms discussed in \cref{subsec:volatile_paradigms}. The heterogeneity that would be an obstacle for a conventional trained-weight application becomes the mechanism by which temporal-computing functionality is obtained in hardware. The application-oriented chapter develops this point quantitatively.

The proof-of-concept (\cref{ch:poc}) first demonstrates, on a single composite device built on a hyperbranched polyester-amide host with LiOTf, that the composite architecture supports a complete set of memristive functionalities and that the two-timescale behaviour anticipated from the cation--anion asymmetry is observed.

The comparative experimental chapter then fixes the electronic component and host chemistry, and sweeps composition and cation identity within a PEO-based polyether host across the alkali-metal triflate series. The measurements test whether composition and cation act as predicted on the device timescale. The application-oriented chapter uses the resulting family of timescale-distinct devices as the substrate for temporal-computing demonstrations.

This rationale does not target the benchmarks of non-volatile storage, does not extend the full synaptic dataset of the proof-of-concept device to every composition, and does not claim a complete physical-chemistry theory of polymer-composite memristive operation. It establishes a narrower link between chemical variables and dynamical device behaviour: polymer-electrolyte composites couple ionic dynamics to electronic transport, and their dynamics can be tuned through host chemistry, salt identity, and composition. \section{Scope and Objectives of This Thesis}\label{sec:scope_objectives}

The preceding sections moved from the limits of conventional computing to biological synapses, memristive devices, inorganic and organic memristor families, and finally polymer-electrolyte composites as a route to chemically tunable ion-mediated dynamics. This closing section turns that background into the scientific questions, objectives, and chapter structure of the thesis.

\subsection{Open Scientific Questions}\label{subsec:open_questions}

The arguments developed in \cref{sec:organic_memristors,sec:polymer_electrolyte_composites} lead to three groups of questions. They are stated in the form addressed in the chapters that follow.

The first question is whether a fully reversible two-terminal organic memristive device can be realised from a solution-processed polymer-electrolyte composite using standard organic-electronic fabrication. The historical survey of \cref{subsec:organic_history} identified this combination --- two-terminal architecture, full reversibility, multi-state analogue control, and a chemically interpretable ionic mechanism --- as a gap in the organic memristor field at the start of the present work. The second question, contingent on the first, is whether such a device can support a broad set of synaptic functions in a single platform: analogue switching, distinguishable short- and long-term memory regimes, an excitatory-post-synaptic-current response, spike-timing-dependent plasticity, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism. The third question concerns the physicochemical origin of the memory regimes themselves: whether the coexistence of a fast and a slow memory component in the composite can be attributed to the cation--anion mobility asymmetry anticipated in \cref{subsec:composite_rationale}, and whether the chemistry of the cation--host coordination accounts for the slow component on a hard-soft acid-base basis.

The next questions broaden the inquiry from a single exemplar to a comparative experimental basis. Within a common polyether host --- specifically PEO --- and a common alkali-metal triflate salt family, how does composition affect the device dynamics when the polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions are varied around a reference formulation? Holding the composition fixed, how does substitution of the cation along the \ce{Li+}, \ce{Na+}, \ce{K+} series shift the fading-memory timescale measured by a common dynamical protocol? These questions are pursued through three measurements that are abundant across the comparative corpus --- I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation --- and the answers reported in the thesis are anchored in those measurements. The richer synaptic suite (EPSC, STDP, separated short- and long-term memory retention, impedance) is treated as a Chapter 2 result and is not propagated as a uniform comparative claim.

The final questions concern use. Can volatile organic memristors of the kind developed here be exploited as temporal-computing primitives in their own right, rather than judged against the figures of merit of non-volatile memory? Can composition and cation identity be used for application-level timescale matching, so that a deliberately heterogeneous family of devices spans a controlled range of fading-memory time constants? Can device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle variability, characterised from the same common measurements, contribute to the dynamics of a temporal-computing scheme rather than be treated only as a defect? These questions are answered in simulation in the data-driven application chapter, using behavioural models extracted from the comparative experimental corpus and using the proof-of-concept device only as a source of priors and consistency checks.

\subsection{Thesis Objectives}\label{subsec:objectives}

The scientific questions of \cref{subsec:open_questions} translate into the following ordered objectives, which structure the remaining chapters and delimit the scope of the work.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Design, fabricate, and characterise a proof-of-concept two-terminal organic memristive device based on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite, processed from a single solution onto a standard ITO/composite/Ag stack, and demonstrate the basic memristive functionality in a single platform.
    \item On that single exemplar device, demonstrate the full set of synaptic functionalities relevant to the thesis: analogue multi-state switching, distinguishable short-term and long-term memory regimes with separately measured retention curves, excitatory-post-synaptic-current behaviour under pulsed stimulation, spike-timing-dependent plasticity with a biologically plausible time constant, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism.
    \item Provide a mechanistic account of the two memory regimes of that exemplar in terms of the displacement of mobile triflate anions and lithium cations within the oxygen-rich ion-conducting host, supported by hard-soft acid-base reasoning on the cation--oxygen interaction.
    \item Build a comparative experimental corpus on a PEO-based polymer-electrolyte platform loaded with alkali-metal triflate salts, fabricated under a common protocol, and characterise it through three common dynamical measurements --- I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation --- that are abundant across the corpus.
    \item Within that corpus, quantify (a) the composition dependence of the dynamics through a PEO/lithium triflate (PEO/LiOTf) concentration series in which the polymer and salt mass fractions are varied around a reference composition, and (b) the cation dependence through a PEO/LiOTf, PEO/sodium triflate (PEO/NaOTf, \ce{NaCF3SO3}), PEO/potassium triflate (PEO/KOTf, \ce{KCF3SO3}) set at a fixed reference composition, on the same three common measurements.
    \item Treat TMPE-based composites and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (\ce{LiN(SO2CF3)2}, \ce{NaN(SO2CF3)2}, \ce{KN(SO2CF3)2}) systems as exploratory side evidence with a clearly delimited dataset, used to motivate or cross-check specific trends, and not as parallel main platforms.
    \item Construct compact, experimentally grounded behavioural models per composition and per cation from the three common dynamical measurements of the comparative corpus, with the proof-of-concept device used only as a source of priors and consistency checks where its richer synaptic data are directly relevant; the models capture a fading-memory time constant, a nonlinear pulse-update rule, a device-to-device variability envelope, and a read transfer function.
    \item Demonstrate, in simulation and from those models alone, three application-level temporal-computing schemes that use the composition- and cation-driven timescale spread of the comparative corpus: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector built on the measured variable-delay depotentiation kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants.
\end{enumerate}

These objectives follow the evidence base of the thesis. Objectives~1--3 are exemplar-level and rely on the full synaptic measurement suite of the proof-of-concept device. Objectives~4--6 are comparative and rely on the three common dynamical measurements; they do not assume that the richer synaptic metrics are available across the comparative corpus. Objectives~7--8 are computational and use measurements already acquired in the experimental chapters; no further fabrication is required to fulfil them.

\subsection{Thesis Outline}\label{subsec:outline}

The thesis is organised in five chapters that follow the objective list.

\paragraph{Chapter 1 --- Introduction.} This chapter provides the conceptual and materials background for the rest of the thesis. It reviews the limits of conventional computing architectures, identifies the biological synapse as the engineering template for the relevant hardware, introduces the memristor as the device abstraction, surveys inorganic and organic memristor families, and identifies polymer electrolyte composites as the material strategy for chemically tunable ion-mediated operation.

\paragraph{Chapter 2 --- Proof of Concept: Polymer-Based Composite Organic Memristive Devices.} The second chapter presents the experimental proof of concept of the thesis on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite device, fabricated from a single solution onto an ITO/composite/Ag stack, and demonstrates the full set of memristive and synaptic functionalities in that single platform. This is the only device of the thesis on which the complete characterisation suite --- I--V hysteresis, potentiation and depression curves, EPSC, separated short- and long-term memory retention, spike-timing-dependent plasticity, and impedance spectroscopy --- is treated as primary evidence.

The chapter develops the mechanistic interpretation of the two memory regimes in terms of mobile triflate anions and lithium cations within the oxygen-rich ion-conducting host, and supports it with the hard-soft acid-base reasoning introduced in \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}. The chapter is built on the work originally reported in Prado-Socorro et al.~\cite{PradoSocorro2022} and contains the most complete synaptic dataset of the thesis.

\paragraph{Chapter 3 --- PEO--Triflate Polymer-Electrolyte Memristive Devices: Composition and Ion-Identity Dependence.} The third chapter takes the validated proof-of-concept architecture forward and reformulates it on a PEO-based polyether host loaded with alkali-metal triflate salts, in order to test the central design rationale of \cref{subsec:composite_rationale} comparatively rather than on a single exemplar. The chapter is built around a core PEO/LiOTf concentration series, in which the polymer and salt mass fractions are varied around a reference composition, and a secondary PEO/LiOTf, PEO/NaOTf, PEO/KOTf set held at the reference composition for cation comparison.

Both studies share three common dynamical measurements --- I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation --- and these three measurements define the comparative basis of the chapter and of the application-oriented chapter that follows. A short, clearly delimited section reports a small set of TMPE-based and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide composites as exploratory side evidence and is presented as such. Variability is quantified directly from replicates within the three common datasets and is treated as a property of the device family rather than as a defect to be suppressed.

\paragraph{Chapter 4 --- Data-Driven Temporal Computing with Volatile Polymer-Electrolyte Memristors.} The fourth chapter converts the experimental dynamical measurements of the previous two chapters into application-level functionality, without further fabrication. Compact behavioural models are extracted, per composition and per cation, from the three common dynamical measurements of the comparative experimental chapter, and the proof-of-concept device of Chapter 2 is used only as a source of priors and consistency checks where its richer synaptic data are directly relevant.

The chapter then demonstrates, in simulation, three application-level schemes in which the composition- and cation-driven timescale spread of the polymer-electrolyte family is the active resource: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector grounded directly in the measured variable-delay depotentiation kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants. Circuit-level integration constraints --- 1T1M addressing, current compliance, read and write protocols, variability envelopes --- are treated explicitly. The chapter is grounded in measured experimental data throughout; it is not a detached theory chapter.

\paragraph{Chapter 5 --- Conclusions and Outlook.} The closing chapter synthesises the device-chemistry axis (\Cref{ch:poc} and the comparative-experimental chapter) and the application axis (the temporal-computing chapter), benchmarks the work against other temporal, reservoir, and event-driven neuromorphic hardware rather than against digital memory technologies, states the limitations of the evidence base --- in particular the restriction of the richer synaptic metrics to the proof-of-concept device and the exploratory status of the TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems --- and identifies the future directions that follow most directly from the work.

The thesis does not target a competitive non-volatile memory. It links polymer-electrolyte composition and cation chemistry to measured ion-mediated device dynamics, and then to temporal-computing primitives. The next chapter takes up the first link in that chain: the proof-of-concept SY/Hybrane/LiOTf device, its memristive and synaptic functionality, and the mechanistic interpretation that anchors the comparative and computational work that follows.
