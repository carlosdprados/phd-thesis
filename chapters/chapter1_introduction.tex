\chapter{Introduction}\label{ch:intro}
%% ============================================================

This thesis examines organic memristive devices built from ion-containing polymer composites and explores how their internal dynamics can serve as a computational resource. The work lies at the intersection of three fields: solid-state electronics, polymer physical chemistry, and computational neuroscience. This chapter connects those fields in the order in which they become necessary. It begins with the limits of contemporary computing (\cref{sec:crisis}), then turns to the biological synapse as an engineering model, introduces the memristor as the relevant device concept, and finally develops the polymer-composite strategy used in the experimental chapters.


%% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{The Crisis in Modern Computing}\label{sec:crisis}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

Modern digital computers still depend on an architecture that has proven highly successful, but its limitations are now increasingly apparent. Simply making transistors smaller and increasing clock speeds no longer fully addresses the issues. This section first examines the stored-program architecture and the three primary constraints that now define its practical limits: the \textit{memory wall}, the \textit{energy wall}, and the slowing of classical transistor scaling (\cref{subsec:vonneumann}). It then discusses two ways to overcome these constraints: integrating computation and storage within the same physical substrate, and adopting event-driven dynamics instead of traditional clock-based synchronous arithmetic (\cref{subsec:paradigms}).

\subsection{Von Neumann Architecture and Its Limitations}\label{subsec:vonneumann}

The architectural model of modern digital computers was proposed by von~Neumann in 1945's ``First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC''~\cite{VonNeumann1993}. Its main idea is the \textit{stored-program} concept, in which instructions and data are stored in the same memory and fetched one word at a time by a central processing unit. This combination of code and data led to the creation of general-purpose computers, enabling single machines to execute diverse tasks by loading different binary instruction sequences. Said architecture, comprised of a CPU, a main memory containing code and data, and a connecting bus, became the starting point for digital systems. Various engineering optimisations have refined this model (from registers and caches to branch prediction and memory controllers), but its overall topology has remained basically unchanged.

Ironically, the strength of this model also creates its bottleneck, given that the processor and memory are \textit{physically distinct} subsystems. They communicate through a channel whose bandwidth sets a practical limit on performance. Every instruction fetch, operand load, and result write-back requires data to move across that channel. In a modern processor, the channel is not a single wire but a hierarchy of registers, caches, controllers, and DRAM, built to bridge units that operate on very different timescales. Still, the main issue remains: the transistors that perform the arithmetic and the cells that hold the state are separated. No arithmetic can proceed before the operands arrive, and no result can be preserved until it is moved back. This is the \textit{processor--memory dichotomy}.

In the single-port architecture, only one word can be read or written per clock cycle, a limitation known as the \textit{von~Neumann bottleneck}~\cite{NatureBottleneck2018}. Many architectural decisions have helped in reducing its effects, for instance, by implementing solutions such as pipelined fetch, multi-level caches, speculative execution, prefetching, out-of-order issue, banked memory systems, and coprocessors like vector units and GPUs. However, all these methods maintain the separation between computation and storage, merely hiding or distributing data movement instead of eliminating it. As a result, the bottleneck is shifted but not removed, and it reappears at the slowest interface in each generation.

Wulf and McKee~\cite{WulfMcKee1995} gave this imbalance a quantitative form in 1995 and named it the \textit{memory wall}. At that time, processor performance was improving at about 60\% per year, while DRAM latency and bandwidth improved at a tenth of that pace. If both trends are extrapolated at this point, it is no wonder that the resulting machine will show an average cycle dominated by memory access. The following decades confirmed this prediction, and nowadays microprocessors devote between 30\% and 60\% of their silicon area and an even larger fraction of their power budget to caches, prefetch logic, memory controllers, and interconnect~\cite{HennessyPatterson2017}. Much of the chip is spent keeping the arithmetic units supplied with data. Plus, the problem becomes especially harsh for workloads with irregular access patterns or working sets larger than the on-chip cache. The bandwidth and energy aspects of this imbalance are summarised in \cref{fig:vonneumann_bottleneck}.

In 2014, Horowitz~\cite{Horowitz2014} provided a comparison of costs for elementary operations in a representative CMOS process where an addition of 32-bit integers costs on the order of \SI{0.1}{\pico\joule}, but an SRAM access can cost tens of picojoules, while a DRAM access could cost several hundred picojoules to nanojoules (\cref{tab:energy_per_op}). In other words, when workloads are dominated by data movement, the energy budget will be spent more on operand transport and less on arithmetic. This is the \textit{energy wall}, which comes from the parasitic capacitance of interconnects and from the fact that the total wire length of a memory subsystem is much larger than the wire length inside an arithmetic unit. Given this, if computation and storage remain spatially separated, the architecture will be limited by the physics of moving charge through wires.

For several decades, these architectural inefficiencies were rendered practically invisible by silicon scaling. Moore had already observed that transistor counts double at regular intervals~\cite{Moore1965}, but this was paired with Dennard scaling~\cite{Dennard1974}: if every linear MOSFET dimension and the supply voltage are scaled by \(1/k\), the transistor switches \(k\) times faster, consumes \(1/k^{2}\) of the energy per switch, and occupies \(1/k^{2}\) of the area. This meant that if both trends continued, each technology generation would deliver more transistors that were also faster and cooler. The memory and energy walls were present, but they did not yet hinder progress.

Dennard scaling stopped occurring at around 2005, when gate-oxide thinning produced leakage currents that no longer allowed proportional voltage scaling~\cite{Hennessy2019}. Transistor counts continued to increase, but the performance per transistor and the energy gains have slowed, while power density has become the main constraint. This is the \textit{post-Dennard} or \textit{dark-silicon} regime~\cite{Esmaeilzadeh2011}. Under these conditions, a new process node can no longer hide the inefficiencies of the von~Neumann model. They have to be addressed at the architectural and materials level. Meanwhile, workloads have been shifting increasingly toward machine-learning kernels, which are dominated by repeated matrix operations on parameters stored in memory~\cite{LeCun2015}. This shift worsens the data-movement bottleneck right when silicon scaling stops compensating for it. Hennessy and Patterson~\cite{Hennessy2019} describe this moment as an inflection point, and argue that the next stage of computer architecture will rely heavily on domain-specific hardware in which memory and computation are brought much closer together.

\subsection[In-Memory and Event-Driven Computing Paradigms]{In-Memory and Event-Driven\protect\\ Computing Paradigms}\label{subsec:paradigms}

Two leading responses to this situation have emerged over the past decade. Both try to solve the strict separation of computation and storage, but in different ways. The first response doesn't dramatically deviate from traditional computing and aims to perform operations directly within (or very close to) the memory array. This route is described as \textit{in-memory}, \textit{near-memory}, or \textit{processing-in-memory} computing. The second response goes further, using temporally structured event streams and treating the intrinsic dynamics of the physical device as part of the computation itself. This route is known as \textit{event-driven} or \textit{temporal} computing. The two responses, set against the von~Neumann baseline they address, are summarised in \cref{fig:paradigms_responses}.

\begin{figure}[!htb]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{../figures/chapter1/ch1_fig2_paradigms_responses.png}
  \caption{Two architectural responses to the von~Neumann bottleneck. (a) In the classical stored-program architecture, every operation traverses a CPU--memory bus that fixes the bandwidth and energy ceilings of the system. (b) In the in-memory paradigm, computation is performed inside the memory array: a resistive crossbar with programmed conductances executes an analogue matrix--vector multiplication by Ohm's and Kirchhoff's laws, so the dot product no longer crosses the CPU--memory boundary. (c) In the event-driven paradigm, dense clocked data are replaced by a sparse stream of asynchronous events that drive a dynamical substrate whose intrinsic relaxation is itself the computation; only a linear readout layer downstream is trained.}
  \label{fig:paradigms_responses}
\end{figure}

In-memory computing bypasses the classical \textit{fetch--compute--write-back} cycle by executing operations directly inside the memory array. This is demonstrated by implementing analogue matrix--vector multiplication using a resistive crossbar architecture. In this setup, each memory cell contains a conductance value that reflects a matrix entry. Then, voltages are applied across the rows, representing the input vector. Finally, the resulting column currents naturally represent the sum of products, in accordance with Kirchhoff's laws. This changes the array from a passive storage device into an active computing tool. Demonstrations using metal-oxide resistive memories~\cite{Prezioso2015}, phase-change materials~\cite{Koelmans2015, Sebastian2014}, and organic ionic devices~\cite{Fuller2019, VanDeBurgt2017, VanDeBurgt2018} have achieved matrix--vector operations with energy efficiencies that surpass those of traditional digital accelerators, and similar principles can be extended to convolutional~\cite{Yao2020} and attention-like processes (the matrix-multiplication kernels at the core of transformer architectures).

At the device level, however, this version of in-memory computing still requires memory properties. Each cell should hold a quasi-static conductance that encodes a trained weight, and which should be set once, read many times, and remain stable enough to meet the application's numerical precision requirements. Non-volatility over seconds to years becomes a design goal.

Event-driven computing is motivated by the fact that many signals of interest carry information in time. Natural sounds, visual transients, motor commands, biomedical waveforms, and streaming sensor outputs are all beautiful, non-static examples. For such signals, the computational model should be a system that receives events in continuous time and evolves in response, given that information is not only present in the amplitude values but also in the waveform's evolution and timing. At the algorithmic level, this is the domain of spiking neural networks~\cite{Roy2019}. At the circuit level, it corresponds to event-driven asynchronous logic. At the device level, which is the level of this thesis, it requires materials whose intrinsic relaxation dynamics are matched to the signals they process.

This framework mirrors biological nervous systems, which efficiently handle complex sensorimotor and cognitive tasks while consuming much less power than artificial systems. This efficiency partly results from architectural choices that diverge significantly from the von~Neumann model. Neurons share information through sparse events, and synapses pass signals that change depending on recent activity. Information is stored and altered through slow, gradual electrochemical processes, in which the ionic medium actively participates in computation. Although directly translating the brain into silicon isn't practical or useful, several principles do carry over: sparse-event coding~\cite{IndiveriLiu2015}, the use of slow device physics as a fundamental computing element, and viewing device heterogeneity as a feature for functional diversity rather than a flaw. These principles form the basis of neuromorphic engineering~\cite{Mead1990}.

Boyd and Chua~\cite{BoydChua1985} showed that any time-invariant nonlinear operator with \textit{fading memory}, in an appropriate function space, can be approximated by a finite Volterra series (a power-series expansion in time-shifted inputs). This means that a system whose current state retains a decaying record of past inputs can perform various temporal computations when paired with a trainable linear readout. This concept was later formalised through algorithms such as Jaeger's \textit{echo state network}~\cite{Jaeger2001ESN, JaegerHaas2004} and Maass, Natschläger, and Markram's \textit{liquid state machine}~\cite{Maass2002LSM}, which were combined under the umbrella of \textit{reservoir computing}~\cite{LukoseviciusJaeger2009, Verstraeten2007}. In this framework, an untrained dynamical system---the reservoir---is driven by the input stream, with only the readout being trained. When the reservoir is realised in a physical substrate, the approach is known as \textit{physical reservoir computing}~\cite{Tanaka2019}, with implementations in optical, mechanical, spintronic, volatile-memristive, and organic electrochemical systems~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021, Cucchi2021}.

This redefines how a working device should be evaluated. If the goal is to maintain a trained weight permanently, a device that can't retain memory for long periods will be considered unsuitable. However, in a reservoir architecture, this finite memory retention \emph{is} what defines the period during which previous inputs influence the current output, a concept referred to as the fading-memory timescale. Variability between devices can also play a different role: while it's a disadvantage for weight storage, a controlled variation in intrinsic timescales can enable a common readout to interpret temporal structures over a wider range than any individual device could achieve~\cite{Tanaka2019}.

\section{The Biological Synapse as the Engineering Template}\label{sec:bio_synapse}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

The division between storage and computation, along with the argument for time-structured device dynamics, prompts the same core question: which physical primitive should we focus on? Biology offers a valuable example. In neural circuits, a single physical element both transmits signals and retains part of the history that influences future transmissions, being modified by the signals that pass through it. This element is the synapse. The essential biological context here isn't the entire brain's complexity but the signalling and plasticity of the synapse, as these processes define the target functions for artificial synaptic devices.

\subsection{Neural Signal Transmission: A Brief Overview}\label{subsec:neural_signal}

A neuron is a polarised cell specialised to receive, integrate, and transmit electrical signals~\cite{Drubach2000}. Dendrites (the branching extensions of a neuron) collect inputs from other cells. The soma (the neuron cell body) then integrates those inputs and, once the membrane potential crosses a certain threshold, the axon hillock (the cone-shaped region of the soma) fires an outgoing signal. The axon then propagates that signal over distances that range from millimetres to metres, and its terminals release a chemical messenger onto downstream cells. A mature animal neuron typically receives signals from \(10^{3}\)--\(10^{4}\) other neurons through their dendritic contacts~\cite{Kandel2013}. Each such contact is a \textit{synapse}. The way a neuron computes information is then influenced heavily by the number and strength of its synaptic contacts, and by how they change over time, more than by the soma itself.

The axon's signal is the \textit{action potential}: a stereotyped depolarisation lasting a few milliseconds caused by the opening of voltage-gated \ce{Na+} channels, followed by \ce{K+}-mediated repolarisation~\cite{HodgkinHuxley1952}. Action potentials are all-or-nothing events that carry little information about stimulus strength in their waveform. Instead, information is encoded in the timing, rate, and coincidence of spike trains~\cite{Rieke1997}. In this sense, neural computation is event-driven (\cref{subsec:paradigms}). Between spikes, a neuron only consumes leakage current. During a spike, it expends a small, relatively constant amount of energy~\cite{AttwellLaughlin2001}. Computation then arises from the patterns of signals arriving at many synapses. The neuron's anatomy and the action potential waveform are summarised in \cref{fig:neuron_action_potential}.

(a) A neuron collects synaptic inputs through its dendrites, integrates them at the soma, and fires an outgoing electrical event when the membrane potential at the axon hillock crosses a threshold; the resulting waveform propagates unidirectionally along the axon to the synaptic terminals, where it triggers chemical transmission onto downstream cells (the zoom shown in \cref{fig:synapse_plasticity}).   \label{fig:neuron_action_potential}
\end{figure}

Synaptic transmission is mostly a chemical process. When an action potential reaches an axon terminal, it causes depolarisation, opening voltage-gated \ce{Ca^{2+}} channels. The incoming \ce{Ca^{2+}} then triggers vesicles filled with neurotransmitters like glutamate in excitatory cortical synapses to fuse with the presynaptic membrane~\cite{Sudhof2004}. The neurotransmitter then diffuses across the \(\sim\)\SI{20}{\nano\metre} synaptic cleft and binds to specific receptors on the postsynaptic membrane. These receptors can open ion channels directly or through second-messenger cascades, leading to an ionic current, either an excitatory or inhibitory postsynaptic current (EPSC or IPSC), that briefly modifies the postsynaptic membrane potential, and whose amplitude and kinetics define the \textit{strength} of the synapse at that moment. It is finally worth noting that the same molecular mechanisms that carry the signal can also influence the strength of future transmissions.

The synapse is a transmission element, a modulator, and a memory element at the same time, given that it transmits a signal, adjusts the amplitude and time course of that signal, and stores everlasting changes produced by previous activity. This co-localisation of transmission, modulation, and memory makes the synapse, more than the neuron as a whole, the most useful biological template for post-von-Neumann electronic devices. The anatomy and the plasticity regimes of the synapse discussed next are summarised in \cref{fig:synapse_plasticity}.

\subsection{Synaptic Plasticity: The Physical Basis of Learning}\label{subsec:plasticity}

One important concept in neuroscience is that experience leaves a physical trace in the nervous system, and that this trace is stored mainly at synapses. Hebb spearheaded this idea in 1949, suggesting that if a presynaptic cell repeatedly helps a postsynaptic cell fire, the connection between them should be strengthened~\cite{Hebb1949}, resulting in the well-known summary, ``cells that fire together, wire together''. This shows two key aspects of the rule: It is \textit{local}, as it relies on the two cells linked by the synapse, and it is \textit{sensitive to correlation}, since joint activity has greater significance than independent activity. Hebb's postulate was not tested directly for more than two decades, but it now organises much of the language of synaptic learning. In this sense, the synaptic plasticity mechanisms to be described next can be interpreted as Hebbian, anti-Hebbian, or Hebbian-like rules functioning on different timescales.

Bliss and Lømo~\cite{Bliss1973} were the first to experimentally demonstrate that brief, high-frequency stimulation in a rabbit's hippocampus increases the postsynaptic response, with these effects lasting for hours. Subsequent research revealed that this enhancement persisted for days to weeks~\cite{BlissCollingridge1993, Abraham2003}. This led to the term \textit{long-term potentiation} (LTP), which is now the main example of persistent, activity-dependent synaptic change. In the hippocampus, it involves two key postsynaptic glutamate receptors: AMPA receptors, which mediate the fast EPSC after glutamate release; and NMDA receptors, which open only when the postsynaptic membrane is already depolarised due to a voltage-dependent \ce{Mg^{2+}} block~\cite{MayerWestbrookGuthrie1984, Nowak1984}, so they act as \ce{Ca^{2+}}-permeable coincidence detectors for simultaneous pre- and postsynaptic activity.

The \ce{Ca^{2+}} influx through NMDA receptors triggers signalling pathways that insert additional AMPA receptors into the postsynaptic membrane~\cite{MalinowMalenka2002, LuscherMalenka2012}. Later presynaptic spikes then produce a larger EPSC. The complementary process, \textit{long-term depression} (LTD)~\cite{DudekBear1992, MulkeyMalenka1992}, produces a persistent decrease in synaptic strength under low-frequency or anti-correlated activity, principally through the reverse trafficking of AMPA receptors~\cite{MalenkaBear2004}. Together, LTP and LTD provide the bidirectional, long-lasting modification of synaptic amplitude that is considered a primary physiological basis for learning.

These long-lived changes coexist with faster and reversible modulations known as \textit{short-term plasticity}~\cite{Zucker1989, ZuckerRegehr2002}. After a presynaptic action potential, the immediate strength of the synapse depends on its recent activity. \textit{Short-term facilitation} is a transient increase in EPSC amplitude that occurs when there is a second spike that follows the first after a short delay, which is usually attributed to residual presynaptic \ce{Ca^{2+}}. \textit{Short-term depression} is a transient decrease caused by vesicle depletion or receptor desensitisation after a recent release. The time constants range from tens of milliseconds to several seconds, and different synapses display different combinations of facilitation and depression.

This way, short-term plasticity acts as a temporal filter at a single synapse, causing identical presynaptic spike sequences to generate varying postsynaptic responses based on the timing between spikes. Essentially, the synapse operates as a nonlinear, history-dependent filter that relies on transient state changes, which reset after some time.

With all this being said, the temporal character of synaptic learning is further exemplified through \textit{spike-timing-dependent plasticity} (STDP), a biological process described in cortical pyramidal cells by Markram and co-workers~\cite{Markram1997} and characterised quantitatively in hippocampal cultures by Bi and Poo~\cite{Bi1998}. In this rule, the sign and magnitude of the change depend on the delay between pre- and postsynaptic spikes, usually within a window of a few tens of milliseconds. If the presynaptic spike precedes the postsynaptic spike, the synapse is typically potentiated. If, on the contrary, the order is reversed, then the synapse is depressed. Finally, if the delay is too long in either direction, no lasting change is produced. All in all, the synapse acts as a detector that retains a short memory of recent presynaptic activity, compares it with postsynaptic firing, and converts this temporal relationship into a longer-lasting change in amplitude.

It is then vital to recognise that the synapse described by these results is not merely a passive storage of ``weight''. Instead, it operates across multiple timescales at once. At the millisecond level, it conveys individual spikes. Over tens to thousands of milliseconds, short-term facilitation and depression adjust its response based on recent activity. Over minutes, hours, and longer periods, LTP and LTD alter the baseline amplitude. These layers exist together at chemical synapses and are driven by partially overlapping yet distinct molecular mechanisms~\cite{AbbottRegehr2004}. Abbott and Regehr~\cite{AbbottRegehr2004} argued that, from this perspective, a synapse is better understood as a computational unit rather than just a memory store. The key takeaway for hardware is that both long-lasting and decaying state changes are important.

\subsection{Why Hardware Emulation of the Synapse?}\label{subsec:why_hw}

The motivation for hardware synapses follows directly from \cref{sec:crisis}. A software neural network runs as arithmetic on values fetched from a memory hierarchy that is physically separated from the arithmetic units. Every forward pass pays the data-movement and energy costs described above, and those costs scale with the number of simulated synapses. A small mammalian cortex contains roughly \(10^{12}\)--\(10^{14}\) synapses and operates on the order of \SI{10}{\watt}. A conventional von~Neumann substrate cannot close this gap simply by being better engineered, because the cost of fetching a weight before multiplying it by an activation is bounded by interconnect physics. The synapse is the most compact low-power example of co-localised analogue memory and computation available in nature, which makes it a useful physical template.

The research programme built around this idea is \textit{neuromorphic engineering}. Its programmatic origin is usually attributed to Mead~\cite{Mead1990}. The goal is not only to run neural algorithms faster, but to build circuits in which the device physics, topology, and dynamics directly implement nervous-system-like computational primitives. Within this programme, the synapse has become the central device-level target. Over the past two decades, the search for a physical ``artificial synapse'' has linked device physics, materials science, and circuit design.

A hardware synapse should provide an analogue, multi-state conductance that can be set reversibly by electrical stimuli and read non-destructively with a small probe current. It should also be compact enough for large arrays, so that many thousands of elements can be addressed with a manageable number of input and output lines~\cite{Kuzum2013, IndiveriLiu2015}. Its response should be nonlinear in a meaningful way, for example by displaying a pulse-number or pulse-amplitude threshold below which little change is induced. Finally, its dynamics should match the signals it is meant to process, whose relevant timescales span from milliseconds to seconds and beyond.

Among these requirements, the distinction between binary and \textit{analogue} conductance is especially important. A binary synapse stores one bit per element. Representing graded synaptic weights with binary elements would then require many devices per connection. An analogue synapse can instead encode several distinguishable states in a single physical element through its conductance range. For the ionic devices considered in this thesis, the write energy can also be low because the state is changed by moving ions over short distances rather than by charging and discharging large digital circuits.

Reversibility matters just as much. A device whose conductance can be potentiated but not reliably depressed cannot implement the bidirectional rules described in \cref{subsec:plasticity}. It may still work as a programmable weight in a feed-forward classifier, but it cannot emulate the full learning behaviour of a synapse. The basic specification becomes analogue, reversible, multi-state, low-energy conductance modulation under electrical pulses. This is the specification that motivates the devices studied in the rest of the thesis.

The biology of \cref{subsec:plasticity} also shows why retention alone is not the right measure of a synaptic device. Biological synapses combine quasi-stable memory, through LTP and LTD, with transient filtering, through short-term plasticity and STDP. Both roles exist in the same physical element. A hardware platform can be useful in two complementary ways: as a persistent or quasi-persistent programmable weight, and as a dynamic fading-memory element. The second role is the one developed in this thesis, following the reservoir-computing picture introduced in \cref{subsec:paradigms}~\cite{Tanaka2019, Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021}.

This leads to a materials question. In biology, the timescales of short-term plasticity are set by \ce{Ca^{2+}} buffering, vesicle recycling, receptor desensitisation, and other local processes that differ from one synapse type to another. A hardware family for this role should offer an analogous way to adjust its electrical timescale. Classical transistor-based memories do not provide that kind of analogue, history-dependent element. The next section asks whether memristive devices do.

\section{The Memristor: Theory and History}\label{sec:memristor_history}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

Circuit theory provides the device concept needed for this role. A memristive device has a current--voltage response that depends on an internal state, and that internal state depends on the previous electrical history of the device. The ideal concept is the \textit{memristor}; the broader experimental class is the family of \textit{memristive devices}. This section introduces the theory, reviews the first canonical physical realisation, surveys the main mechanistic families, and then revisits the usual device parameters from two points of view: trained-weight storage and volatile temporal computing. What matters for the rest of the thesis is that the formal definition includes both quasi-stable and relaxing internal states.

\subsection{Theoretical Foundation}\label{subsec:memristor_theory}

Classical linear circuit theory describes a two-terminal passive element by the relation it imposes between two of four fundamental variables: voltage \(v\), current \(i\), charge \(q = \int i \, \mathrm{d}t\), and flux linkage \(\varphi = \int v \, \mathrm{d}t\). The familiar elements occupy three of the non-trivial pairings. The resistor \(R\) relates \(v\) and \(i\) through \(v = R\,i\). The capacitor \(C\) relates \(q\) and \(v\) through \(q = C\,v\). The inductor \(L\) relates \(\varphi\) and \(i\) through \(\varphi = L\,i\). Two other pairings are simply the definitions of charge and flux linkage as time integrals. The remaining non-trivial relation, between charge \(q\) and flux linkage \(\varphi\), had no classical element associated with it.

Chua~\cite{Chua1971} filled this gap in 1971. He argued that the passive-element taxonomy was incomplete without a fourth two-terminal element relating charge to flux linkage, and he named it the \textit{memristor}, a contraction of ``memory resistor''. The element is defined by the constitutive relation \(\varphi = \hat{\varphi}(q)\), or in the form most often used in the device literature, by \(v = M(q)\,i\). The \textit{memristance} \(M(q) = \mathrm{d}\hat{\varphi}/\mathrm{d}q\) has the units of resistance, but it depends on the charge that has previously flowed through the element.

Put more simply, a memristor is a resistance whose present value depends on the history of current that has passed through it. If \(M\) is constant, the device is simply a resistor. If \(M\) depends on \(q\), the device carries a memory of past activity in its present electrical response. The characteristic experimental signature is a \textit{pinched hysteresis loop} in the \(i\)--\(v\) plane under periodic excitation. The loop passes through the origin because zero voltage gives zero current instantaneously, but its shape depends on the internal state left by previous stimulation. This pinched hysteresis is the elementary diagnostic used to identify memristive behaviour in the device literature.

The ideal memristor defined by \(\varphi = \hat{\varphi}(q)\) is, however, a restrictive mathematical object. Very few physical devices satisfy it exactly. Chua and Kang~\cite{ChuaKang1976} answered this by generalising the concept in 1976 to a \textit{memristive system}, specified by the pair of equations
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\dot{\mathbf{x}} &= f(\mathbf{x}, u, t), \\
y &= g(\mathbf{x}, u, t)\, u,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:memristive_system}
\end{equation}
where \(u\) and \(y\) are the electrical input and output, \(\mathbf{x}\) is an internal state vector, and \(f\) and \(g\) are generally nonlinear. The state equation \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = f(\mathbf{x}, u, t)\) may include relaxation terms that drive the state back toward equilibrium after the input is removed. A volatile device whose state decays still belongs to this memristive-system framework, even though it is not an ideal memristor in the strict sense of \cite{Chua1971}. This broader framework is the one used for the polymer-composite devices reported here.

\subsection{The First Physical Realisation: TiO\texorpdfstring{$_2$}{2} Memristor}\label{subsec:tio2_memristor}

For more than three decades after Chua's proposal, the memristor remained mostly a theoretical construct. Thin-film metal-oxide resistive switches with hysteretic current--voltage characteristics had been reported since the late 1960s, and many of them can now be recognised as memristive systems. At the time, however, they were not interpreted within Chua's formalism. The field still lacked a canonical physical device that connected the theoretical concept to a concrete thin-film structure.

That identification was made in 2008 by Strukov, Snider, Stewart and Williams~\cite{Strukov2008} at HP Laboratories. Their device consisted of a thin titanium dioxide (\ce{TiO2}) layer between two platinum electrodes. Inside the film, a reduced \ce{TiO_{2-x}} region acted as an oxygen-deficient conductive layer, while the adjacent stoichiometric \ce{TiO2} region acted as a resistive barrier. Under an applied voltage, oxygen vacancies drifted and displaced the boundary between both regions. Since the total resistance depends on the relative thickness of the conductive and resistive parts, the device resistance could be modulated continuously by moving this internal boundary. In the language of \cref{eq:memristive_system}, the boundary position is the internal state \(\mathbf{x}\), oxygen-vacancy drift supplies the state equation \(f\), and the resulting \(i\)--\(v\) response reproduces the pinched hysteresis predicted by the theory.

The impact of this result was larger than the novelty of the oxide physics alone. It turned the memristor from a formal circuit element into a concrete experimental object, and it triggered a large body of work on resistive-switching and memristive devices. A two-terminal resistance that can be programmed by electrical pulses, tuned over several conductance values, and read at low voltage is close to the analogue weight element required by neuromorphic hardware~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013}. Crossbar demonstrations of in-memory matrix--vector multiplication followed soon after~\cite{Prezioso2015, IelminiWong2018}. The 2008 \ce{TiO2} device is only one mechanism, based on oxygen-vacancy drift in a transition-metal oxide, but the memristive formalism is broader. The constitutive argument and the pinched-hysteresis fingerprint are collected in \cref{fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint}.

\subsection{Classification of Memristive Mechanisms}\label{subsec:memristor_classes}

In current device physics, \textit{memristor} is used mostly as a phenomenological label, not as the name of a single microscopic mechanism. A device is called memristive if its current--voltage response shows the pinched hysteresis expected from Chua's theory and if its internal state can be set and read electrically. It can achieve this through many different physical processes. Reviews group memristive devices into several broad mechanistic families~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013, IelminiWong2018}. The taxonomy below makes that diversity explicit and locates the ionic-polymer devices of this thesis within the wider field.

Historically, the most visible family is \textit{filamentary} resistive switching. Under an applied voltage, a narrow conductive path forms through an otherwise insulating thin film and can later be ruptured or reformed. In \textit{conductive-bridge} random-access memories (CBRAM), the filament is built from metal atoms displaced electrochemically from an active electrode, usually silver or copper, through a solid electrolyte. In \textit{valence-change} memories, usually based on binary transition-metal oxides, the filament is associated with oxygen vacancies or partially reduced metal-cation states inside the oxide lattice~\cite{Yang2008}. These devices can show high ON/OFF ratios, short switching times, and non-volatile retention. Their main limitation for analogue synaptic operation is stochasticity: filament nucleation, position, and rupture vary from device to device and from cycle to cycle~\cite{Sun2019}. As a result, filamentary devices are powerful binary or coarse multi-level memories, but they are less natural platforms for dense analogue representations.

A second family operates through \textit{interface} or \textit{non-filamentary} switching. In these devices, conductance changes over a distributed region rather than through a single localised filament. Some implementations modulate the height or width of a Schottky barrier by accumulating or depleting mobile ions near a metal--semiconductor interface. Others, especially mixed ionic--electronic systems, drive distributed electrochemical doping of the semiconductor~\cite{VanDeBurgt2017, Fuller2019}. Because the state change is spatially extended, these devices can support a quasi-continuous range of conductance values. The ON/OFF ratio is often smaller than in filamentary devices, but variability is usually lower and current scales more predictably with device area. This distributed-switching picture is the closest broad class for several organic electrochemical devices, including the polymer-composite devices studied here.

A third family consists of \textit{phase-change} memories. The active material is a chalcogenide alloy, usually \ce{Ge2Sb2Te5} or a related composition, whose resistance depends on whether it is crystalline or amorphous. Joule-heating pulses are used either to melt and quench the material into the amorphous state, or to anneal it back toward the crystalline state~\cite{Sebastian2014, Koelmans2015, LGallo2018}. Phase-change memories provide excellent retention, large ON/OFF ratios, and high endurance. The cost is appreciable write energy and, unless the device is carefully engineered, a tendency toward binary or few-state operation. They are mature storage-class memories and are now among the most important platforms for analogue in-memory computing demonstrations.

A fourth family is formed by \textit{spintronic} or magnetic resistive devices. Their resistance depends on the magnetic configuration of a tunnel junction, usually the relative orientation of two ferromagnetic layers~\cite{Torrejon2017}. Spintronic devices can switch very quickly, with low write energy, and they form the basis of spin-transfer-torque magnetic random-access memory. Their limitations for analogue synaptic use are a relatively small ON/OFF ratio, often of order two, and a limited number of stable intermediate states. Even so, their intrinsic stochastic dynamics have been used as a computational resource in appropriately designed architectures~\cite{Mizrahi2018, Torrejon2017}.

A fifth family, and the one most relevant to this thesis, is that of \textit{ionic} memristors. Their internal state is the distribution of one or more mobile ionic species inside a solid matrix. The host can be an inorganic oxide, a chalcogenide, a mixed ionic--electronic conductor, or a polymer electrolyte. The mobile species can be a small cation such as \ce{Li+}, \ce{Na+}, or \ce{Ag+}, a proton, or a small anion. What distinguishes this family is that the state variable is a chemical distribution of mobile species, not only a structural, morphological, or magnetic configuration of the host. Its kinetics are governed by ionic transport, coordination, and relaxation within the matrix.

Two consequences are central. First, ionic systems are naturally history-dependent, because the distribution of mobile species at any moment reflects the previous electric fields applied to the device. Second, they often relax after the field is removed. The intermolecular forces and concentration gradients that oppose the biased, non-equilibrium ion distribution drive the system back toward equilibrium. Volatility is not a failure mode of ionic memristors; it is part of their physical operation. The associated timescales are set by ion transport and binding, which can be tuned chemically through the host matrix. That link between chemistry and relaxation is why the experimental work focuses on the organic polymer-electrolyte subfamily.

\subsection{Key Memristive Parameters}\label{subsec:memristor_params}

Because memristive devices are mechanistically diverse, the field evaluates them through a set of practical parameters. The \textit{high-resistance state} (HRS) and \textit{low-resistance state} (LRS) define the accessible conductance window, and their ratio is the \textit{ON/OFF ratio}. The \textit{set} and \textit{reset voltages} specify the electrical stimuli needed to program the device between states, while the associated \textit{write energy} sets the cost of a programming event. The \textit{switching speed} describes how rapidly the device responds. \textit{Retention} measures how long a state is preserved without re-programming, and \textit{endurance} measures how many cycles the device tolerates before degradation. For analogue operation, the additional parameters that matter are the \textit{linearity} and \textit{symmetry} of the conductance update under positive and negative pulses, together with \textit{device-to-device} and \textit{cycle-to-cycle variability}.

These parameters are most naturally interpreted for devices intended to store \textit{trained weights} in analogue in-memory-computing arrays~\cite{IelminiWong2018, Prezioso2015}. In that setting the target is a fixed conductance matrix implementing a trained neural network. Retention must be long enough to preserve weights between programming events. Endurance must cover re-training and write--verify cycles. Linearity and symmetry determine the numerical accuracy of in-place gradient-based training, and variability reduces inference accuracy. Much of the memristor literature is written from this standpoint. Organic and polymer-composite devices have often been judged poorly under this view, because their retention can be short, their endurance limited, and their device-to-device variability relatively high.

But not every parameter is improved by making it as large, as small, or as stable as possible. Retention is the clearest example. For a trained-weight memory, longer retention is better. For a fading-memory device, the useful retention is the one matched to the signal timescale. The same shift in interpretation applies to switching speed, write energy, update linearity, and symmetry. Their optimum values differ between trained-weight storage and temporal computing, and a single device cannot in general optimise both regimes at once. \Cref{tab:param_optima} sets the two targets side by side.

Variability also needs a more precise language. Intra-device instability, where the same device fails to reproduce its own response under the same protocol, remains a defect. Inter-device heterogeneity is different. It is harmful in a trained-weight array, but useful in a reservoir, because a set of devices with different time constants and nonlinear transfer functions provides a richer projection of the input than a set of identical devices~\cite{Tanaka2019}.

\subsection{Dynamic Computing Paradigms for Volatile Memristors}\label{subsec:volatile_paradigms}

Over the past decade, a complementary set of computational paradigms has developed around volatile, heterogeneous, history-dependent devices. In these schemes, a memristor whose state decays on a controlled timescale acts as a physical \textit{leaky integrator}, one of the elementary building blocks of temporal computation. The three examples below are revisited in the application chapter, where the polymer-electrolyte devices are evaluated using the metrics implied by each paradigm.

The most prominent example is \textit{physical reservoir computing}~\cite{Tanaka2019}, introduced conceptually in \cref{subsec:paradigms}. In a volatile-memristor reservoir, the input is encoded as a stream of voltage pulses applied to an array of devices. Their internal states evolve during the stimuli and relax between them. At each moment, the collective state of the array provides a nonlinear projection of the recent input history into a high-dimensional feature space. A linear readout trained on that space can then solve temporal classification, regression, or prediction tasks. Demonstrations have been reported in diffusive metal-filament devices~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019}, dynamic oxide memristors~\cite{Zhong2021}, and organic electrochemical systems~\cite{Cucchi2021}.

A second paradigm uses volatile memristors as \textit{coincidence detectors} and \textit{temporal-correlation extractors}. If pulses arrive closer together than the relaxation time of the device, the internal state accumulates. If they arrive after the state has already decayed, little accumulation occurs. The device response then contains information about the temporal proximity of events. This can be used to distinguish coincident or near-coincident inputs from dispersed ones. Architectures based on this idea have been proposed and partially demonstrated with STDP-like rules, where the sign and magnitude of the response depend on the relative timing of pre- and postsynaptic events~\cite{Bichler2012}. In this sense, the device directly implements the timing sensitivity introduced in \cref{subsec:plasticity}.

A third paradigm uses banks of volatile memristors with different relaxation time constants as \textit{multi-timescale transient filter banks}. A single input is processed in parallel by devices with distinct intrinsic timescales. Fast devices respond strongly to sharp transients and return quickly to baseline. Slow devices integrate over longer windows. A downstream classifier can then extract onsets, bursts, rhythms, or slow envelopes that no single time constant would capture alone. Ionic memristors are especially attractive here because their relaxation timescale can, in principle, be shifted by changing the host composition or the mobile ion.

For these modes, the metrics differ from the trained-weight metrics listed in \cref{subsec:memristor_params}. In reservoir computing, the central quantity is the \textit{memory capacity} of the device ensemble, which measures how well the present reservoir state reconstructs past inputs at different delays. This is complemented by the \textit{class separability} obtained on a representative benchmark task and by the \textit{robustness} of the computation to intrinsic device variability~\cite{Dambre2012}.

For coincidence detection, the useful quantities are the width and symmetry of the timing window, the discrimination margin between coincident and dispersed inputs, and the energy cost per detected event. For multi-timescale filtering, the central quantities are the accessible span of time constants, the isolation of each channel, and the system-level power under realistic input statistics. HRS, LRS, ON/OFF ratio, and retention in isolation do not capture these requirements. The metrics are \textit{task-matched}: they reward devices whose physical timescales line up with the temporal structure of the signals they process.

The memristor, in this view, is more than a candidate memory cell. It is a circuit concept, a family of physical mechanisms, and a bridge between the biological synapse and dynamical computing. Ionic memristors are especially relevant here because their state is a chemical distribution of mobile species, and because relaxation is part of their operation rather than an afterthought. The next section benchmarks the inorganic memristor families that dominate present-day neuromorphic hardware, with attention to what they do well and to the requirements that motivate a complementary organic route. The two computational modes of memristive devices, static programmable weights and volatile fading-memory elements, are contrasted in \cref{fig:static_vs_fading}.

\section{Inorganic Memristors for Neuromorphic Computing}\label{sec:inorganic_memristors}
%% ------------------------------------------------------------

The broad taxonomy of \cref{subsec:memristor_classes} can now be narrowed to the inorganic families that dominate present-day neuromorphic-hardware demonstrations: metal-oxide resistive memories, phase-change memories, and spin-transfer-torque magnetic tunnel junctions. Each family is technologically important, and together they have made memristive hardware a credible post-von-Neumann substrate. The combination pursued in this thesis is more specific: analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on solution-processable soft substrates. That combination lies outside the region where the main inorganic families are strongest, and this mismatch motivates the organic route developed later.

\subsection{Metal-Oxide Memristors}\label{subsec:metal_oxide_memristors}

The broadest and most technologically developed inorganic family is based on resistive switching in transition-metal-oxide thin films. After the \ce{TiO2} device of Strukov and co-workers~\cite{Strukov2008}, the field quickly expanded to other oxide systems, especially hafnium dioxide (\ce{HfO2}), tantalum oxide (\ce{TaO_x}), nickel oxide (\ce{NiO}), tungsten oxide (\ce{WO_x}), and titanium dioxide itself. Many of these materials were already familiar to silicon processing as gate-dielectric or interconnect-related layers. This made back-end-of-line integration feasible from the beginning. CMOS compatibility is the main practical reason for the prominence of metal-oxide memristors in neuromorphic demonstrations: large arrays can be fabricated on top of existing addressing electronics, and demonstrations such as the metal-oxide crossbar of Prezioso et al.~\cite{Prezioso2015} followed naturally.

At the device level, metal-oxide memristors are usually classified by the mobile species that carries the switching. In \textit{valence-change memories} (VCM), the mobile species is an oxygen vacancy. Its field-driven migration changes the local oxidation state of the host and modifies the conductance of a confined filamentary region~\cite{Yang2008}. In \textit{electrochemical metallisation} or \textit{conductive-bridge} memories (CBRAM), the mobile species is instead a metal cation, typically \ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+}, supplied by an active electrode. Under bias, these cations migrate through the oxide and plate out into a metallic filament.

Both mechanisms often require an initial \textit{forming} step, where a localised soft breakdown establishes the filament geometry that later set and reset pulses modulate. Once formed, the device can behave as a bistable memory or, depending on compliance current and pulse protocol, as a graded multi-state element. A third class of metal-oxide devices operates through non-filamentary \textit{interface-type} modulation, where distributed oxygen-vacancy redistribution adjusts a metal--oxide Schottky interface. This route can support finer analogue gradation, usually at the cost of lower ON/OFF ratio.

The strengths of metal-oxide memristors are those of a mature binary or few-level non-volatile memory. Retention can reach years at room temperature. Switching can occur on nanosecond timescales. Endurance can exceed \(10^{9}\) cycles in carefully engineered stacks, and densities compatible with sub-\SI{10}{\nano\metre} crossbars are in principle possible~\cite{YangStrukovStewart2013, IelminiWong2018}. These properties have made metal-oxide resistive memories the reference platform for commercial and pre-commercial in-memory-computing demonstrations. The same broad family can also be made volatile. Diffusive metal-filament devices, in which Ag or Cu clusters spontaneously dissolve after programming, have been used as fading-memory reservoir elements~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021}. Metal-oxide memristors are not intrinsically non-volatile; their behaviour depends on materials, stack geometry, and protocol.

The weaknesses for high-precision analogue synaptic operation are also well known. In filamentary devices, conductance modulation is intrinsically stochastic. The nucleation of the filament, its lateral position, and its conducting cross-section are set by thermally and electrically driven nanoscale fluctuations. Even in carefully engineered stacks, set voltage, reset voltage, and achieved conductance retain appreciable device-to-device and cycle-to-cycle spread~\cite{Sun2019}. Non-ideal linearity and asymmetry under positive and negative pulses then limit the accuracy of in-place gradient-based training on metal-oxide crossbars~\cite{IelminiWong2018}.

Multi-level operation is possible, but usually only for a limited number of reliably distinguishable states. Achieving a high-resolution analogue weight from a single filamentary cell remains difficult, so many architectures use multi-cell unit codes or hybrid digital--analogue representations. This does not diminish the maturity of the metal-oxide platform. It shows where the platform is strongest: retention, endurance, and integration density. Analogue linearity, symmetric bidirectional update, and fine-grained timescale engineering are harder targets.

\subsection{Phase-Change Memory}\label{subsec:pcm}

Phase-change memory is based on a different physical principle: reversible switching between amorphous and crystalline phases of a chalcogenide alloy. The canonical composition is \ce{Ge2Sb2Te5}, usually abbreviated GST, although related compositions show the same basic behaviour. The crystalline phase is a conductive narrow-gap semiconductor, while the amorphous phase is much more resistive. Switching is thermal. A short, intense \textit{reset} pulse melts a small volume and quenches it into the amorphous state. A longer, lower-amplitude \textit{set} pulse anneals the material above the crystallisation threshold and lets it re-crystallise~\cite{Sebastian2014, Koelmans2015}. Unlike in filamentary metal-oxide memories, the resistance is not set by a single localised path, but by the proportion of crystalline and amorphous material in the programmed volume.

PCM is unusually mature among memristive technologies. It has been developed as a storage-class memory since the 1970s, has passed through commercial product generations, and has become one of the strongest inorganic platforms for analogue in-memory computing. Le Gallo and co-workers demonstrated mixed-precision in-memory arithmetic on PCM arrays with enough accuracy, together with a digital co-processor, to solve practical linear systems~\cite{LGallo2018}. Ambrogio et al.~\cite{Ambrogio2018} later demonstrated neural-network training using PCM elements as the long-term weight store, paired with a volatile capacitor for fine-grained updates. These results make PCM the most serious benchmark for trained-weight analogue applications.

The constraints of PCM become clearer when the device is viewed as a synapse rather than only as a weight cell. The reset operation requires local melting, so write currents and thermal loads are substantial and neighbouring cells must be thermally isolated. The quenched amorphous phase is also not perfectly stable. Its resistance drifts over time, usually with a power-law dependence, and this drift has to be compensated by periodic reading, calibration, or algorithmic correction.

Precise analogue programming of intermediate states requires carefully designed pulse protocols and tight thermal control. The underlying physics is thermal and structural, which limits the degree of chemical control over the relevant timescales. Crystallisation and amorphisation kinetics can be adjusted through alloy composition and device geometry, but not with the kind of molecular handle on ion transport available in soft-matter systems. PCM is a compelling analogue in-memory-computing platform, but its natural strength lies in high-retention, high-endurance trained weights, not in the chemically tunable fading-memory regime pursued here.

\subsection{Spin-Transfer-Torque Magnetic Tunnel Junctions}\label{subsec:stt_mtj}

The third inorganic family departs from ion or atom rearrangement altogether. A magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) contains two ferromagnetic layers separated by a thin insulating barrier, typically \ce{MgO}. Through tunnelling magnetoresistance, the junction resistance depends on the relative magnetisation of the two layers: low for parallel alignment and high for antiparallel alignment. In a spin-transfer-torque (STT) device, a spin-polarised current switches the free layer between these configurations. Read and write operations use the same electrical port, and the internal state variable is the magnetic configuration of the free layer rather than a chemical or morphological state of the host.

STT--MTJs are attractive for neuromorphic and unconventional computing because they combine nanosecond switching, endurance above \(10^{12}\) cycles, femtojoule-scale write energies, and compatibility with back-end-of-line CMOS integration. They also provide a controlled source of stochasticity when the free layer is operated near the superparamagnetic limit. This stochastic dynamics has already been used computationally: Torrejon et al.~\cite{Torrejon2017} used spin-torque nano-oscillators as reservoir nodes for spoken-digit classification, while Mizrahi et al.~\cite{Mizrahi2018} used superparamagnetic MTJs as noisy basis functions for neural-style computation. The MTJ family is not limited to digital memory; its dynamics can also be useful.

The limitations appear when the goal is fine-grained analogue synaptic operation. A simple STT--MTJ is essentially a two-state device, and the resistance ratio between antiparallel and parallel configurations is typically only a few. Increasing the ratio or creating many programmable intermediate states requires more elaborate stacks, such as synthetic antiferromagnetic references, perpendicular-anisotropy layers, or multilayer configurations. These additions increase process complexity and reduce the simplicity of the device.

There is also a direct trade-off between thermal stability, which sets retention, and switching current, which sets write energy. The materials stack is demanding: ferromagnetic multilayers, synthetic antiferromagnets, and crystalline \ce{MgO} tunnel barriers do not lend themselves to solution processing or substrate-agnostic fabrication. STT--MTJs are best viewed as fast, non-volatile, few-state stochastic elements, strong in probabilistic computing and embedded non-volatile memory, but less natural as a platform for analogue, multi-timescale synaptic emulation.

\subsection{General Limitations of Inorganic Memristors}\label{subsec:inorganic_limits}

The three inorganic families reviewed above differ strongly in mechanism and maturity. Still, they share several limitations with respect to the specific neuromorphic-materials problem addressed in this thesis.

The first limitation is \textit{chemical tunability}. Each family is anchored in a relatively restricted materials space: transition-metal oxides, tellurium-based chalcogenide alloys, or engineered ferromagnetic stacks. The available design knobs are mainly geometric, stoichiometric within narrow windows, or process-related. It is rarely possible to tune the key energetic or kinetic parameters by substituting one mobile ion for another with different binding chemistry, or by changing the host matrix in a way that directly alters segmental motion. This is not a criticism of individual demonstrations. It is a feature of the materials space itself: molecular-level control over binding and transport chemistry is not one of the natural handles of these inorganic platforms.

The second limitation is \textit{dynamical timescale engineering}. Each inorganic family can display volatile behaviour in specific implementations. Diffusive metal-filament devices provide fading-memory dynamics~\cite{Du2017, Midya2019, Zhong2021, Ohno2011}. PCM shows resistance drift in the amorphous phase~\cite{Sebastian2014}. Superparamagnetic MTJs introduce stochastic fluctuations on engineered timescales~\cite{Mizrahi2018}. What is harder is to sweep a relaxation time constant systematically over several orders of magnitude within one materials platform using a simple chemical variable. Vacancy drift, crystallisation, and magnetic relaxation are each governed by the intrinsic energy landscape of the host. For heterogeneous reservoirs and multi-timescale filter banks, where deliberately distinct time constants are needed, this limitation is central.

The third limitation concerns \textit{fabrication and integration}. The inorganic families usually rely on vacuum deposition, high-temperature annealing, and top-down lithography. These workflows are excellent for dense crossbars integrated on CMOS, but they restrict substrate choice and processing temperature. Large-area, flexible, biocompatible, or printable platforms are not their natural environment. Solution-processable active layers offer a different route: they can in principle be deposited on a wider range of substrates at mild temperatures, and their active chemistry can be changed by altering the precursor solution. This fabrication difference matters for the soft and distributed systems considered later in the thesis.

The fourth limitation is stochasticity. In reservoir computing, stochasticity can be useful if it appears as controlled inter-device diversity. It is harmful if it appears as intra-device instability. In filamentary and phase-change systems these two effects can be difficult to separate. A device family in which heterogeneity can be introduced deliberately through composition, while each device remains stable under repeated operation, would provide a cleaner route to controlled variability.

These limitations define the gap addressed here. Analogue, ion-mediated, chemically tunable, timescale-rich behaviour on soft, solution-processable substrates is not the natural strength of the inorganic families. The next section asks whether organic and polymeric ion-mediated materials can fill that space. \Cref{fig:inorganic_comparison_matrix} summarises the qualitative comparison, and \cref{tab:inorganic_summary} gives the corresponding quantitative envelope.

\section{Organic Materials for Memristive Applications}\label{sec:organic_memristors}

The inorganic memristor families validate the broader memristive and neuromorphic programme. Each of them solves part of the problem well. The gap left open by the previous section is narrower: a soft, solution-processable device in which ion-mediated dynamics can be deliberately shaped. That requirement points toward organic and polymeric materials.

Organic materials give access to chemistry that is difficult to reach in inorganic stacks. Device cost, cleanliness, and reproducibility still depend on the specific materials, solvents, contacts, encapsulation, and operating conditions. The stronger point is chemical access: in an organic active layer, electronic transport, ionic transport, redox activity, and relaxation kinetics can often be adjusted through synthesis and formulation. Changing the device physics can mean changing a molecule, a polymer host, a salt, or a mixing ratio, rather than redesigning a rigid multilayer stack.

\Cref{subsec:organic_advantages} first identifies the useful features of organic materials for this problem. \Cref{subsec:organic_mechanisms} then surveys the main physical mechanisms proposed for organic memristive behaviour and shows that ``organic memristor'' is not a single mechanism class. Particular weight is given to ion-mediated and mixed ionic--electronic operation, because this is the route used in the rest of the thesis.

\Cref{subsec:organic_history} then gives a concise historical survey: early electrochemical and polymer-film demonstrations, three-terminal electrochemical-transistor synapses, two-terminal redox biomimetics, stretchable devices, molecular devices, and organic reservoir demonstrations. The aim is to identify what the field had already solved, and what was still missing when the work reported here began. The section closes on the question that motivates the composite strategy: which organic architecture is best suited to controllable, reversible, ion-mediated neuromorphic dynamics?

\subsection{Advantages of Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_advantages}

The main advantage of organic materials in memristive and neuromorphic devices is the number of independent variables available in the active layer. A semiconducting polymer can be changed through backbone design, side-chain functionalisation, and molecular weight. An ion-transport host can be tuned through chelating groups, topology, and glass-transition temperature. A redox-active unit can be selected from a broad molecular library. A mobile ion can be chosen independently of the host according to charge, radius, and Lewis acidity. In the inorganic platforms discussed in \cref{sec:inorganic_memristors}, the same level of chemical control is much harder to reach.

Solution processability is the second key feature. Many organic active layers relevant to memristive operation---conjugated polymers, hyperbranched polyethers, polyelectrolytes, redox-active polymers, and their blends---can be deposited from solution by spin coating, blade coating, inkjet printing, spray coating, or roll-to-roll methods. For the composite devices of this thesis, the practical consequence is simple: polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor ratios can be varied by changing a solution recipe rather than by redesigning a multilayer stack.

The mild thermal budget is closely connected to this advantage. Most organic memristive layers can be processed below one hundred and fifty degrees Celsius, and often below one hundred. Top contacts can be deposited through shadow masks without high-temperature post-anneals. This makes the fabrication compatible with flexible plastics, paper, textiles, elastomers, and even biological substrates. The mechanical compliance of the final device follows from the processing route and the soft active layer, not from any single molecule alone. Flexible and stretchable organic synaptic devices~\cite{Kim2018, Park2020} show that this compliance is reachable with standard organic-electronic fabrication and that it matters for wearable, implantable, and conformal neuromorphic systems under the \emph{nervetronic}~\cite{Park2020} heading.

Together, independent chemistry, solution processability, and mild processing define a materials space that is genuinely different from the inorganic one. Low cost, reproducibility, biocompatibility, and long lifetime still have to be demonstrated for each system. What this materials class does provide is control over internal-state physics without rebuilding the entire device stack.

Two consequences matter here. First, organic systems can support finely tunable analogue responses. Their switching mechanisms---charge trapping on molecular sites, redox activity in a macromolecular host, or mobile-ion displacement in a polymer matrix---are often distributed over many sites. This makes intermediate states more accessible than in mechanisms based on a single localised filament.

Second, relaxation timescales can be engineered chemically. Ion transport in a polymer electrolyte depends on how strongly the mobile ion binds to the polymer, how mobile the host segments are, and how many mobile species are present. In an organic composite, each of these quantities is a design variable. Timescales from milliseconds to many seconds, relevant to biological short-term plasticity and slow temporal signals, can be explored within one materials family through compositional changes.

Each demonstration in the literature occupies a different point in this materials space, with its own strengths and compromises. The history of the field (\cref{subsec:organic_history}) shows that combining reversibility, endurance, multi-state analogue control, and interpretable ionic physics in a single two-terminal organic device remained difficult well into the last decade. Ion-mediated polymer composites are pursued here because they bring the most relevant chemical controls into one simple architecture.

\subsection{Mechanisms in Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_mechanisms}

Unlike \emph{phase-change memory} or \emph{valence-change memory}, the label \emph{organic memristor} does not identify one mechanism. Organic devices can show memristive behaviour through several physical processes, each with its own state variable, reversibility, timescale, and degree of analogue control. It is more useful to compare the main families by asking what each one offers, what it costs, and where the ion-mediated family sits among them.

The first family is charge trapping and detrapping in an insulating or weakly conducting matrix. The active layer contains deep or shallow traps associated with molecular defects, embedded nanoparticles, or interfaces between incompatible polymer phases. Under bias, carriers occupy or empty those traps. The device state is then defined by trap occupation, and the resistance changes because the local space-charge environment modifies electronic transport through the matrix.

This route is attractive because trapping is common in disordered organic semiconductors and can occur at modest voltages. Its weakness is limited analogue control. The state space is often effectively binary, the conductance can depend non-monotonically on trap occupation, and the microscopic identity of the traps is often difficult to establish. Retention is set by trap depth relative to thermal energy, and that depth is not easy to tune systematically by composition alone.

The second family is redox switching. The active layer, or one component of it, contains a species whose oxidation state can be changed under bias. This species can be a discrete molecule in a polymer host, a redox-active polymer, or a redox unit attached to a conjugated backbone. The device state is defined by the distribution of oxidised and reduced species; that distribution changes the local electronic structure and the conductance. Redox switching gives a clean mechanistic picture when it works well: the state variable is a chemical oxidation state, the relevant potentials are experimentally accessible, and the process can be reversible over many cycles. Important examples include redox-active conjugated polymer systems~\cite{Liu2016, Goswami2017, Goswami2020} and early electrochemical devices based on coordination complexes~\cite{Zakhidov2010}.

The costs are also clear. Not every redox process is cleanly reversible. Side reactions, dimerisation, and slow degradation can limit endurance. In addition, purely redox-based devices often behave as bistable or few-state systems unless the response is distributed over many sites. Distribution can recover analogue behaviour, but the interpretation then shifts from the chemistry of one well-defined switch to the statistics of an ensemble.

The third family is conformational or structural switching. Here the active layer contains a molecular species whose geometry, isomerisation state, or aggregation state can be modulated by an electric field, local heating, or coupled electrochemical processes. Examples include photochromic and electrochromic switches, and bistable molecular architectures whose conductance depends on molecular conformation~\cite{Goswami2017, Goswami2020}. These systems offer high molecular design precision because the state is tied to a defined structural change. The trade-off is that the synthesis can be demanding, the switching timescale is fixed by a particular molecular rearrangement, and scaling from molecular behaviour to device-level averages introduces additional complexity.

The fourth family, and the one most relevant to this thesis, is ionic redistribution in mixed ionic--electronic systems. The active layer contains an electronic conductor, typically a conjugated polymer, together with mobile ions carried by an ionic host. Under bias, the ions migrate and accumulate near interfaces or within regions of the active layer. Their redistribution modifies electronic transport through interfacial doping, electrostatic band bending, electrochemical doping, or a combination of these effects. The resulting conductance change can be continuous, multi-state, and directly related to the amount and spatial distribution of displaced ionic charge.

This picture is closely related to light-emitting electrochemical cells~\cite{Pei1995, deMello1998, deMello2002, Matyba2009, vanReenen2010, Edman2008, Mardegan2021}, where ionic redistribution under bias creates an in-situ doped junction, and to organic electrochemical transistors (OECTs), where gate-driven ion exchange modulates a conducting-polymer channel. The two-terminal memristive mode uses the same kind of ionic redistribution differently. The driving bias itself plays the role of the gate, and the residual ionic distribution after the bias is removed carries the memory of the stimulus.

This fourth family is central because its state variable is chemically transparent: the position and distribution of mobile ions in a polymer host. When the bias is removed, relaxation becomes a fading-memory process governed by ion transport, polymer binding environment, salt concentration, and film morphology. The price is that ionic motion is slower than purely electronic switching, and side reactions with electrodes must be controlled through chemistry and contact choice.

A fifth family, included for completeness, is organic filamentary conduction. Metallic species, typically \ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+} from an active electrode, migrate through the organic matrix and form conductive bridges similar to those in inorganic CBRAM. These devices inherit the strengths of filamentary switching, such as large ON/OFF ratios and low switching voltages, but also its weaknesses, especially stochastic filament formation and intra-device instability (\cref{subsec:metal_oxide_memristors}). They show that a mechanism can cross the organic--inorganic boundary; the distinctively organic opportunity lies in mechanisms where chemical specificity controls the internal-state physics.

\subsection{Historical Development of Organic Memristors}\label{subsec:organic_history}

The organic memristor literature of the last three decades is not a single lineage. It is a set of parallel streams, each based on different mechanisms and architectures. The useful question is not which device came first, but which problems each stream solved and which combinations of features were still missing when the work reported in this thesis began.

An early strand repurposed the slow, history-dependent ionic redistribution of light-emitting electrochemical cells as a memristive function. Zakhidov and co-workers reported a \emph{light-emitting memristor}~\cite{Zakhidov2010} in which an ionic \(\ce{[Ru(bpy)3]^{2+}}\) complex supplied both the emissive species and the mobile ionic component. Its importance was mainly mechanistic: it showed that ionic redistribution in a polymer host could produce memristive behaviour, even if the device was not yet a practical memory element. In parallel, resistive switching was reported in simple polymer films such as poly(vinyl alcohol)~\cite{Lei2014}. These devices showed that memristive phenomena were accessible in inexpensive polymer matrices, but they were typically binary, mechanistically opaque, and not analogue.

Another strand used the organic electrochemical transistor as the platform for synaptic emulation. Gkoupidenis and colleagues showed that PEDOT:PSS OECTs could reproduce short-term plasticity, paired-pulse facilitation, and long-term potentiation and depression under appropriate pulse protocols~\cite{Gkoupidenis2015}, and later extended this approach to devices with global electrolyte connectivity~\cite{Gkoupidenis2017}. Xu and colleagues reported core--sheath nanowire synaptic transistors~\cite{Xu2016}. Van de Burgt, Fuller, and co-workers developed the non-volatile organic electrochemical neuromorphic device (ENODe)~\cite{VanDeBurgt2017, Fuller2019}, where a dedicated reservoir supplies a linearly updateable synaptic weight. Together, these works showed that organic electrochemistry can support synaptic learning rules at low voltage and with good analogue control. They also established the importance of ion exchange between an electrolyte and a conducting polymer channel~\cite{VanDeBurgt2018}. OECT devices with halide-perovskite gates~\cite{Seo2019} extended the accessible timescales further.

The three-terminal route is powerful, but architecturally distinct from the device pursued here. An OECT synapse contains a gated channel, a separate electrolyte, and, in the non-volatile variant, a dedicated programming reservoir. Read and write can be decoupled, but each synaptic element requires at least a third terminal and a contacted electrolyte volume. A simpler two-terminal device, with the ionic physics contained inside the active layer, remains attractive as a complement, especially for the reservoir and coincidence-detection paradigms of \cref{subsec:volatile_paradigms}.

The two-terminal line developed in parallel. Liu and co-workers reported a biomimicking redox organic memristor~\cite{Liu2016} in which an organic redox system embedded in a polymer matrix produced a short-term-to-long-term memory transition under repeated stimulation. This mattered because it showed coexisting memory regimes in a passive two-terminal device. It also illustrated the main difficulty of the redox route. Redox processes are not always fully reversible, and the long-term regime can involve partially irreversible chemical transformations rather than controlled state evolution. At that point, analogue, reversible, multi-state behaviour in a two-terminal organic device without permanent redox changes remained an open problem.

Two further lines matter here. Stretchable and mechanically compliant organic synaptic devices, including the bioinspired artificial afferent nerve of Kim and co-workers~\cite{Kim2018} and the \emph{nervetronic} framing reviewed by Park and co-workers~\cite{Park2020}, showed that the mechanical advantages of organic processing can be realised at device level. Molecular memristors, especially the metal-coordinated azo-aromatic layers reported by Goswami and co-workers~\cite{Goswami2017, Goswami2020}, showed what precise molecular design can achieve in terms of contrast, endurance, and multi-state operation. Their cost is demanding synthesis, which places them in a different practical regime from the composite devices considered here.

Hybrid systems sit alongside these families. Perovskite--organic hybrids~\cite{Xiao2016} combine the ionic conduction of halide perovskites with organic charge-transport layers. Conjugated polymer systems with non-volatile switching have also been explored~\cite{Shih2017}. More recently, organic electrochemical reservoir computing~\cite{Cucchi2021} has shown that mixed ionic--electronic organic devices can operate as physical-reservoir elements while remaining volatile. That result matters here because it treats volatility as a useful feature rather than as a failure of retention.

By the early 2020s, organic neuromorphic electronics had become a recognised subfield with several active design lines~\cite{VanDeBurgt2018}. Ion-mediated mechanisms had been shown in both three-terminal and two-terminal architectures. Analogue and symmetric programming had been achieved in electrochemically gated devices. Short-term-to-long-term transitions had been demonstrated in two-terminal redox systems. Mechanical compliance had been shown in stretchable devices, and molecular-level control had been shown in engineered molecular memristors.

What had not been consolidated was the full set of features in one simple two-terminal platform. The three-terminal electrochemical route achieved reversibility and analogue control, but with architectural complexity. The two-terminal redox route achieved simplicity, but not necessarily full reversibility. The molecular route achieved precision, but with demanding synthesis. The stretchable route demonstrated mechanical compatibility, but did not by itself define a specific memory mechanism. The gap addressed in this thesis is a two-terminal, reversible, multi-state, ion-mediated device with interpretable chemistry and standard organic-electronic components. The route pursued is a composite of a conjugated semiconducting polymer, an ion-conducting polymer host, and a dissociated alkali-metal salt. The parallel mechanism-family streams up to the early 2020s are collected in \cref{fig:organic_timeline}.

\section{Polymer Electrolyte Composites: The Enabling Strategy}\label{sec:polymer_electrolyte_composites}

\Cref{sec:organic_memristors} narrowed the field to devices where ionic redistribution in a polymer host couples to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor. It also motivated the two-terminal route as a simpler complement to electrochemical-transistor architectures. The remaining question is what should be placed inside the active layer. The strategy adopted here is a \emph{composite}: a semiconducting conjugated polymer, an oxygen-rich ion-conducting polymer host, and a dissociated alkali-metal salt, all processed from a common solution in a standard two-terminal stack.

The strategy rests on three points. \Cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers} recalls the physical chemistry of ion-conducting polymers: oxygen-rich hosts, alkali-cation coordination, transport mechanisms, and composition. \Cref{subsec:lec_precedent} then uses polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells as a precedent for stable field-driven coupling between ionic migration and electronic transport in a soft organic active layer. Finally, \cref{subsec:composite_rationale} gives the design rationale: separate electronic and ionic functions, treat the salt as an independent variable, use cation identity and composition as timescale controls, and build chemically distinct devices that act as a temporal-kernel bank rather than as competing memories.

\subsection{Ion-Conducting Polymers: Principles}\label{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}

An ion-conducting polymer is a macromolecular host in which dissolved or dispersed ions can move under an applied electric field without requiring a liquid solvent phase. The field developed in the late 1970s and early 1980s, when alkali-metal salts were shown to dissolve in poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) and form solid complexes with measurable ionic conductivity~\cite{Armand1989, Berthier1983, Gray1991}. Solid polymer electrolytes were developed mainly for lithium batteries, but the principles that matter here are broader: cation coordination, segmental motion, host crystallinity, and composition. These are the same principles that make polymer electrolytes useful as tunable memristive media.

The central chemical feature of a polyether host is the high density of ether oxygens along the backbone or side chains. In PEO, the oxygens appear regularly along the \(-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{O}\!-\) repeat unit. In the amorphous phase, the chain conformation allows each cation to coordinate several ether oxygens from one or more polymer chains~\cite{Berthier1983, Lightfoot1993}. In Hybrane-type hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts, oxygen density is supplied differently, through ether and ester groups on side chains attached to a branched polymer core.

In both hosts, the oxygen atoms play the same chemical role. They act as hard Lewis bases that solvate alkali cations, stabilise salt dissociation, and define the local coordination environment of the cation. The anion is usually held more weakly, mainly by electrostatic compensation, and tends to be more mobile than the cation in the same host. This is especially true for soft, charge-delocalised anions such as triflate (\ce{CF3SO3-}) or bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (TFSI, \ce{N(SO2CF3)2-}).

Ion transport in these hosts is usually described through three related microscopic pictures. In the \emph{vehicular} picture, a cation and its primary solvation shell move together through the polymer matrix, so long-range transport is coupled directly to polymer motion. In the \emph{hopping} or site-to-site picture, the cation transfers between coordination sites through local rearrangement of oxygens, without requiring macroscopic motion of the host. This is conceptually close to Grotthuss-type transport in proton conductors, although the chemistry of the transfer is different.

In the \emph{segmental-motion-assisted} picture, short polymer segments reorganise around the cation, creating and destroying coordination sites that enable effective diffusion. These three pictures are not mutually exclusive. In a real polyether host, transport is a mixture of all three, and the relative contribution of each mechanism depends on host chemistry, cation identity, salt concentration, and temperature~\cite{Berthier1983, Gray1991}. For the devices studied here, the key point is that ionic transport is controlled by the coupling between polymer dynamics and ionic chemistry.

This coupling directly affects device dynamics. To first approximation, the macroscopic ionic mobility of a polymer electrolyte follows the segmental mobility of the host. Segmental mobility is controlled by the glass transition temperature \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\) and by whether the host remains amorphous under operating conditions. Below \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\), segmental motion is effectively frozen and ionic conduction collapses. Above \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\), segmental motion is activated and ionic transport becomes measurable, often with Vogel--Tamman--Fulcher rather than Arrhenius temperature dependence~\cite{Gray1991}.

Crystalline regions contribute little to conduction and can trap ions in ordered environments. For ion-transport applications, low-crystallinity or fully amorphous hosts are preferred. A low \(T_{\mathrm{g}}\) and a low crystalline fraction can be pursued through copolymerisation, branching, or plasticisation. Hyperbranched architectures such as the Hybrane family are useful partly because branching suppresses crystallinity. For room-temperature memristive operation, the host should remain amorphous and segmentally mobile under ambient conditions, ideally through the intrinsic host design rather than through added plasticisers.

The strength of cation binding depends on the cation identity. Hard--soft acid--base (HSAB) reasoning~\cite{Pearson1963} gives a useful language for this point. Ether and ester oxygens in polyether and polyester-amide hosts are hard Lewis bases: their donor pairs are localised, their polarisability is low, and their coordination to alkali cations is mainly electrostatic. Among the alkali cations, \ce{Li+} is the hardest Lewis acid because it is small and has high charge density. It should interact most strongly with hard oxygen donors. \ce{Na+} and \ce{K+} are progressively softer, with larger radii, lower charge densities, and weaker electrostatic coupling to the same oxygen environment.

The practical consequence is that, in a common polyether host and at fixed composition, the residence time of the cation at a coordination site is expected to follow the coordination-strength order \ce{Li+} > \ce{Na+} > \ce{K+}. In device terms, that ordering should map onto the fading-memory timescale: harder cations should relax more slowly, while softer cations should relax more quickly. This HSAB framing is used here qualitatively. It predicts an ordering, not an exact value. The experimental chapters test whether the measured dynamics follow this ranking.

Composition provides a second set of design knobs. A polymer electrolyte is defined not only by the host and ion identity, but also by the salt-to-host ratio. In a composite device, the ratio of polymer electrolyte to semiconductor also matters. These two ratios control different quantities: the concentration of mobile ionic species available under bias, and the relative volume fractions of ionic and electronic phases in the active layer.

The ratios are not redundant. The salt-to-host ratio sets the number and spatial density of occupied coordination sites. The host-to-semiconductor ratio sets the volume available for ionic transport and the connectivity of the electronic phase. In the later chapters, the polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions are treated as independent composition axes. Composition is not just a recipe variable; it is a physical-chemistry knob on the ionic dynamics.

These considerations motivate the host choices in the thesis. Hybrane DEO750 8500 is a hyperbranched polyester-amide with a high density of ether and ester oxygens. It provides an amorphous, low-crystallinity ion-transport host, and the same material family has already been validated in light-emitting electrochemical cells~\cite{Mardegan2021}. It is used in \cref{ch:poc} as the host of the proof-of-concept Super Yellow (SY)/Hybrane/lithium triflate (LiOTf, \ce{LiCF3SO3}) composite, where it supports the full synaptic functionality demonstrated in a single device.

PEO is then used as the central host for the broader comparative corpus. It plays an analogous chemical role: its ether oxygens coordinate alkali-metal cations, its segmental motion enables transport, and its composition and cation identity can be varied systematically. The thesis does not argue that Hybrane or PEO is universally superior. They are used for complementary purposes. Hybrane establishes the full proof-of-concept behaviour in one exemplar; PEO provides the broader platform for composition- and cation-dependent dynamics. The physical-chemistry principles developed here apply to both.

\subsection{Light-Emitting Electrochemical Cells as Precedent}\label{subsec:lec_precedent}

The reasoning so far depends on a physical claim: ionic redistribution in an organic polymer host can be coupled to electronic transport in a conjugated semiconductor in a stable and reversible way. Polymer light-emitting electrochemical cells (LECs) provide the strongest precedent. In LECs, the same coupling is used to generate electroluminescence rather than memory, and the underlying physics has been studied in detail. They are used here as a \emph{material-level precedent}, not because light emission is a target of the thesis.

In its canonical form~\cite{Pei1995}, an LEC is a two-terminal sandwich device. A conjugated light-emitting polymer is blended from a common solution with an ion-conducting polymer and a dissociated salt. Under bias, ions redistribute through the host. Cations drift toward the cathode and anions toward the anode, producing doped regions near the contacts. These doped regions inject electrons and holes into the undoped bulk, and a p-i-n-like profile develops dynamically during operation. Electroluminescence is generated in the intrinsic region between the two doped fronts.

At the microscopic level, LEC operation has been described through two limiting pictures. In the electrochemical-doping picture, the dominant effect is doping of the conjugated polymer. In the electrodynamic picture, ion accumulation at the interfaces mainly lowers charge-injection barriers. Modelling~\cite{deMello1998, deMello2002} and experiments~\cite{Matyba2009, Edman2008, Wagberg2008} clarified the conditions under which each picture applies, and a unifying framework~\cite{vanReenen2010} showed that the two descriptions correspond to different injection regimes rather than to contradictory mechanisms. Both rely on the same ionic redistribution in the polymer host; they differ in which consequence of that redistribution controls the measured electrical response. Studies of electrochemical side reactions~\cite{Fang2008} have also identified degradation pathways and strategies to suppress them.

Two aspects of this precedent matter directly. First, LEC operation shows that ionic redistribution in a polymer host can coexist with electronic transport in a conjugated polymer over useful operating windows and lifetimes that can reach hundreds or thousands of hours. The ions do not necessarily undergo destructive side reactions within a few cycles, and the conjugated polymer does not necessarily degrade irreversibly under normal operation. The contacts can also be engineered, through metal choice and operating protocol, to support repeated charge injection and withdrawal.

These findings establish the feasibility of stable mixed ionic--electronic composite operation. For a memristive device built from the same materials logic, the implication is straightforward: if ionic redistribution in a polymer host can be stable under LEC operation, then the same redistribution can plausibly serve as the internal state variable of a two-terminal memory device.

Second, LECs clarify the role of the solid electrolyte. In both LECs and memristive composites, the electrolyte is not the main electronic conductor. Its function is to provide mobile ions whose redistribution changes the electronic response of the conjugated polymer. The difference is how this redistribution is used. In an LEC, the bias is maintained so that the doped p-i-n profile sustains emission. In a memristive device, the bias writes an ionic distribution, and the residual distribution after the bias is removed carries the memory. The electrolyte chemistry and its coupling to the conjugated host are governed by the same principles in both cases.

The strongest precedent is the specific material family used for the proof-of-concept device in \cref{ch:poc}. Mardegan and co-workers~\cite{Mardegan2021} showed that Hybrane-family hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts, blended with a conjugated light-emitting polymer, support LEC operation with long lifetimes and controlled electroluminescence. This validates the same semiconducting-polymer / ion-conducting-host / salt architecture later used as a memristive device. The constituent polymers, solvent system, processing protocol, and operating conditions are closely related. The feasibility of reversible field-driven ionic redistribution in this host class is carried from established LEC chemistry into the new memristive function.

The LEC precedent establishes feasibility, not the full result: stable ionic--electronic coupling is achievable in an organic composite. \Cref{ch:poc} then asks whether the same materials logic can support analogue multi-state programming, short- and long-term memory regimes, and clean relaxation kinetics in the SY/Hybrane/LiOTf system. The comparative chapter extends the strategy to PEO-based hosts across compositions and cations, where each formulation has to earn its own device behaviour experimentally.

\subsection{Rationale for the Composite Approach}\label{subsec:composite_rationale}

The composite strategy can now be stated directly. Instead of asking one material to provide electronic conduction, ionic transport, and controlled coupling at the same time, the active layer separates those functions into three components. The semiconductor supplies electronic transport, the polymer electrolyte supplies ion motion, and the salt supplies the mobile species. Each component can be chosen from its own chemical series and varied while the others are held approximately fixed. This turns one tightly coupled materials problem into three more tractable chemistry problems.

This decoupling matters especially for the salt. Although it occupies only a small fraction of the active layer, it strongly affects the dynamics. The HSAB argument of \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers} gives the qualitative direction: harder cations bind ether oxygens more strongly, are harder to displace, and should relax more slowly. The cation becomes a chemical handle on the slow component of the device response.

The anion contributes differently. As discussed above, soft and charge-delocalised anions such as triflate are not strongly coordinated by the host oxygens. They are held mainly through electrostatic compensation and are usually more mobile than the cation. The cation--anion mobility asymmetry is built into the salt chemistry, not introduced afterwards by the device architecture.

This asymmetry can be used directly. If the anion relaxes quickly and the cation relaxes more slowly, the same active layer can carry two memory timescales: a fast component associated mainly with anion redistribution near the interfaces, and a slower component associated mainly with cation redistribution in the ion-conducting host. The relative weight of both components depends on bias amplitude, pulse duration, and read protocol, so separating them experimentally is not trivial. Still, the chemical origin is clear. The device then provides a materials-level analogue of the coexistence of short-term and longer-term plasticity introduced in \cref{subsec:plasticity}. The microscopic mechanisms are different, but the functional requirement is the same: one physical element carries both fast-decaying and slower state variables.

Composition supplies the second set of handles. The salt fraction controls the number of mobile ions and the strength of the ionic response per unit volume. The polyether fraction controls the amount of segmentally mobile matrix in which those ions can rearrange. In principle, these two ratios should help separate the amplitude of the conductance change from the kinetics of its development and relaxation. The comparative chapter tests this separation experimentally.

One-step blend processing makes this systematic study practical. The semiconductor, host polymer, and salt can be dissolved in a common solvent. A new composition can be prepared by changing the mixing ratio of stock solutions and depositing the blend onto a patterned electrode. Changing composition does not require redesigning the stack. The fabrication effort per composition is modest, and nominally identical devices depend on a small number of mixing and deposition parameters rather than on a long multilayer sequence. This makes it practical to build a family of compositions and cations under a common experimental protocol.

The main chemical variables of the composite---cation, anion, polymer fraction, and salt fraction---act less like pass--fail recipe choices than like controls on the internal-state physics. The cation sets a default relaxation tendency through its coordination strength, while composition changes the amplitude of the ionic response and the rate at which it develops and relaxes. Read through a standard electrical protocol, the device presents a dynamical response that reflects those choices.

At this point the materials argument becomes computational. Because fabrication is a single-step blend deposition, chemically distinct composites can be prepared as a small family of devices that share the same electronic transport material and host polymer while differing in composition or cation identity. Read through a common circuit, such a family acts as a bank of temporal kernels with different decay constants. The heterogeneity that would be problematic for trained-weight storage becomes the source of temporal-computing functionality. The application chapter develops this point quantitatively.

The experimental chapters test the rationale in two stages: first on a single Hybrane/LiOTf exemplar (\cref{ch:poc}), and then on a PEO-based corpus across composition and cation identity. The chain of evidence is specific: polymer-electrolyte composites couple ionic dynamics to electronic transport; those dynamics can be shifted through host chemistry, salt identity, and composition; and the resulting spread can be used as a temporal-computing substrate. The two-terminal architecture and the qualitative timescale ladder expected from cation and composition chemistry are collected in \cref{fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder}.

\section{Scope and Objectives of This Thesis}\label{sec:scope_objectives}

The preceding sections moved from the limits of conventional computing to biological synapses, memristive devices, inorganic and organic implementations, and finally polymer-electrolyte composites. This closing section turns that background into the scientific questions, objectives, and chapter structure of the thesis.

\subsection{Open Scientific Questions}\label{subsec:open_questions}

The preceding sections lead to three groups of questions, which are stated here in the form addressed by the following chapters.

The first question is whether a fully reversible two-terminal organic memristive device can be realised from a solution-processed polymer-electrolyte composite using standard organic-electronic fabrication. The historical survey of \cref{subsec:organic_history} identified this exact combination---two-terminal architecture, reversibility, multi-state analogue control, and chemically interpretable ionic physics---as a gap in the field. The second question follows from the first: can such a device support a broad set of synaptic functions in one platform, including analogue switching, distinguishable short- and long-term memory regimes, EPSC-like response, STDP, and an impedance signature consistent with the mechanism? The third question concerns the origin of the memory regimes. Can the coexistence of fast and slow memory components be explained by the cation--anion mobility asymmetry anticipated in \cref{subsec:composite_rationale}? And does cation--host coordination account for the slow component through the HSAB argument developed above?

The next questions broaden the work from one exemplar to a comparative corpus. Within a common PEO host and a common alkali-metal triflate family, how does composition affect device dynamics when polymer-to-semiconductor and salt-to-semiconductor mass fractions are varied around a reference formulation? With composition fixed, how does changing the cation along the \ce{Li+}, \ce{Na+}, \ce{K+} series shift the measured fading-memory timescale? These questions are answered through three measurements available across the corpus: I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation. The richer synaptic suite, including EPSC, STDP, separated memory retention, and impedance, remains a Chapter 2 result rather than a uniform comparative dataset.

The final questions concern use. Can the volatile response of these organic memristors be used as a temporal-computing primitive? Can composition and cation identity provide application-level timescale matching, so that a deliberately heterogeneous device family spans a controlled range of fading-memory constants? Can measured variability contribute to temporal computation instead of only degrading it? These questions are answered in simulation in the application chapter, using behavioural models extracted from the comparative corpus; the proof-of-concept device is used only as a source of priors and consistency checks.

\subsection{Thesis Objectives}\label{subsec:objectives}

The scientific questions of \cref{subsec:open_questions} translate into the ordered objectives below. They structure the remaining chapters and delimit the evidence claimed by the thesis.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Design, fabricate, and characterise a proof-of-concept two-terminal organic memristive device based on a SY/Hybrane/LiOTf composite, processed from a single solution onto a standard ITO/composite/Ag stack, and demonstrate the basic memristive functionality in a single platform.
    \item On that single exemplar device, demonstrate the full set of synaptic functionalities relevant to the thesis: analogue multi-state switching, distinguishable short-term and long-term memory regimes with separately measured retention curves, excitatory-postsynaptic-current behaviour under pulsed stimulation, spike-timing-dependent plasticity with a biologically plausible time constant, and an impedance signature consistent with the proposed mechanism.
    \item Provide a mechanistic account of the two memory regimes of that exemplar in terms of the displacement of mobile triflate anions and lithium cations within the oxygen-rich ion-conducting host, supported by hard-soft acid-base reasoning on the cation--oxygen interaction.
    \item Build a comparative experimental corpus on a PEO-based polymer-electrolyte platform loaded with alkali-metal triflate salts, fabricated under a common protocol, and characterise it through three common dynamical measurements --- I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation --- that are abundant across the corpus.
    \item Within that corpus, quantify (a) the composition dependence of the dynamics through a PEO/lithium triflate (PEO/LiOTf) concentration series in which the polymer and salt mass fractions are varied around a reference composition, and (b) the cation dependence through a PEO/LiOTf, PEO/sodium triflate (PEO/NaOTf, \ce{NaCF3SO3}), PEO/potassium triflate (PEO/KOTf, \ce{KCF3SO3}) set at a fixed reference composition, on the same three common measurements.
    \item Treat TMPE-based composites and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (\ce{LiN(SO2CF3)2}, \ce{NaN(SO2CF3)2}, \ce{KN(SO2CF3)2}) systems as exploratory side evidence with a clearly delimited dataset, used to motivate or cross-check specific trends, and not as parallel main platforms.
    \item Construct compact, experimentally grounded behavioural models per composition and per cation from the three common dynamical measurements of the comparative corpus, with the proof-of-concept device used only as a source of priors and consistency checks where its richer synaptic data are directly relevant; the models capture a fading-memory time constant, a nonlinear pulse-update rule, a device-to-device variability envelope, and a read transfer function.
    \item Demonstrate, in simulation and from those models alone, three application-level temporal-computing schemes that use the composition- and cation-driven timescale spread of the comparative corpus: a heterogeneous physical reservoir for temporal classification, a spike coincidence detector built on the measured variable-delay depotentiation kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built on devices with deliberately distinct fitted time constants.
\end{enumerate}

The objectives mirror the evidence base. Objectives~1--3 are exemplar-level and rely on the full synaptic measurement suite of the proof-of-concept device. Objectives~4--6 are comparative and rely on the three common dynamical measurements, without assuming that the richer synaptic metrics are available across the corpus. Objectives~7--8 are computational and use measurements already acquired in the experimental chapters, so no further fabrication is required for them.

\subsection{Thesis Outline}\label{subsec:outline}

The thesis is organised in five chapters that follow this objective list.

\paragraph{Chapter 1 --- Introduction.} The present chapter provides the conceptual and materials background, ending with the polymer-electrolyte composite as the strategy used throughout the thesis.

\paragraph{Chapter 2 --- Proof of Concept: Polymer-Based Composite Organic Memristive Devices.} This chapter reports the SY/Hybrane/LiOTf proof-of-concept device. It is the only device in the thesis for which the complete synaptic suite is treated as primary evidence: I--V hysteresis, potentiation/depression, EPSC, separated short- and long-term retention, STDP, and impedance. It is also where the two-memory-regime mechanism is developed in terms of triflate-anion and lithium-cation displacement under the HSAB framing of \cref{subsec:ion_conducting_polymers}. The chapter is built on Prado-Socorro et al.~\cite{PradoSocorro2022} and contains the most complete synaptic dataset of the thesis.

\paragraph{Chapter 3 --- PEO--Triflate Polymer-Electrolyte Memristive Devices: Composition and Ion-Identity Dependence.} This comparative chapter reformulates the proof-of-concept architecture on a PEO host loaded with alkali-metal triflates. It studies composition through a PEO/LiOTf concentration series and cation identity through a fixed-composition PEO/LiOTf, PEO/NaOTf, PEO/KOTf set. Both studies use the same three common measurements: I--V hysteresis, variable-number-of-pulses potentiation, and variable-delay depotentiation. TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems are reported separately as exploratory side evidence. Variability is quantified from replicates and treated as a property of the device family.

\paragraph{Chapter 4 --- Data-Driven Temporal Computing with Volatile Polymer-Electrolyte Memristors.} This chapter turns the experimental measurements into temporal-computing functionality without further fabrication. Compact behavioural models are extracted per composition and per cation from the three common measurements. Three schemes are then demonstrated in simulation: a heterogeneous physical reservoir, a spike coincidence detector grounded in the measured variable-delay kernels, and a multi-timescale transient filter bank built from devices with distinct fitted time constants. Circuit-level constraints such as 1T1M addressing, current compliance, read/write protocols, and variability envelopes are treated explicitly.

\paragraph{Chapter 5 --- Conclusions and Outlook.} The closing chapter brings together the device-chemistry axis (\Cref{ch:poc} and the comparative chapter) and the application axis (the temporal-computing chapter). It benchmarks the work against temporal, reservoir, and event-driven neuromorphic hardware rather than against digital memory technologies. It also states the limitations of the evidence base, especially the restriction of the richer synaptic metrics to the proof-of-concept device and the exploratory status of the TMPE and alkali-bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide systems.

The thesis links polymer-electrolyte composition and cation chemistry to measured ion-mediated dynamics, and then uses those dynamics as temporal-computing primitives. The next chapter takes the first step in that chain: the proof-of-concept SY/Hybrane/LiOTf device, its memristive and synaptic functionality, and the mechanistic interpretation that anchors the comparative and computational work that follows.
