## Selection criteria applied

A figure was retained only if it met all three of the following:

1. The surrounding prose has become dense or explicitly comparative.
2. The reader has already accumulated enough context to parse the figure at first sight.
3. The figure measurably reduces the verbal explanation load elsewhere in the chapter, or encodes a load-bearing framing that the thesis-wide argument relies on.

Figures that would merely duplicate the paragraph immediately above them, or that would arrive before the underlying concept had been introduced, were rejected. In all cases the figure is placed **after** the paragraph that creates the need for it, not before.

Fourteen insertion points met the criteria. They are listed in reading order.

## Figure 1 — The von Neumann bottleneck and the memory/energy walls

- **Proposed label.** `fig:vonneumann_bottleneck`
- **Section placement.** §1.1, `subsec:vonneumann`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that introduces the von Neumann bottleneck and the memory wall (the fourth paragraph of `subsec:vonneumann`). The figure arrives once the reader has been told that the CPU–memory bus is the limiting resource, and then reinforces that the bandwidth problem is compounded by an energy cost per data movement.
- **Figure type.** Composite schematic: a block diagram on the left (CPU, memory, shared bus, arrows indicating the mandatory round trip for every operation) and a small energy-cost bar on the right (arithmetic vs. on-chip SRAM vs. off-chip DRAM access, qualitative only, no invented numbers).
- **One-sentence message.** In a classical processor, every operation pays both a bandwidth and an energy toll to move data across the CPU–memory boundary, and the ratio of those tolls has grown with scaling rather than shrunk.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Schematic of the von Neumann architecture and the associated bandwidth and energy costs of data movement. (a) In the stored-program paradigm, the central processing unit and the memory unit are physically separated and communicate through a shared bus, so that every instruction fetch and every operand access must traverse that bus. (b) The energy cost of moving a word across this boundary exceeds, by orders of magnitude, the energy cost of the arithmetic operation performed on it, and this asymmetry has increased as technology has scaled. Together, the bandwidth ceiling of the bus (the von Neumann bottleneck) and the associated energy ceiling (the memory and energy walls) set the context in which non-von-Neumann and neuromorphic alternatives are evaluated.

## Figure 2 — Architectural responses to the von Neumann bottleneck: in-memory and event-driven paradigms

- **Proposed label.** `fig:paradigms_responses`
- **Section placement.** §1.1, `subsec:paradigms`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the opening paragraph of `subsec:paradigms` that names the two responses (in-memory / near-memory / processing-in-memory, and event-driven / temporal). The figure operates as a roadmap: the reader sees both architectures side-by-side before the prose describes each one in turn, so the deep-dive paragraphs that follow can be parsed against an already-loaded visual.
- **Figure type.** Three-panel architectural schematic that builds on Figure 1 and is deliberately disjoint from Figure 8. Panel (a) recapitulates the classical von Neumann data path (CPU ↔ shared bus ↔ memory) with the bus highlighted as the bottleneck inherited from Figure 1, drawn small enough to act as a visual anchor rather than a repeat. Panel (b) shows the *in-memory* response: a resistive crossbar with input voltages on rows, programmed conductances encoding a matrix, column currents emerging as the matrix–vector product, and an explicit annotation that no datum crosses a CPU–memory boundary during the operation. Panel (c) shows the *event-driven* response: a sparse, time-resolved input event train enters a dynamical substrate whose intrinsic relaxation is itself the computation, with only a trained linear readout downstream; a small inset trace contrasts a clocked dense waveform with the asynchronous event stream. Throughout, the emphasis is on **data flow relative to the bottleneck**, not on device figures of merit.
- **Distinction from Figure 8 (`fig:static_vs_fading`).** Figure 2 lives at the *architecture* level — it answers "where does data move during a computation, relative to the bottleneck of Figure 1." Figure 8 lives at the *device* level — it answers "what figures of merit must a single memristive cell satisfy to play either role." The two figures are deliberately complementary: Figure 2 motivates *why* two architectures are pursued; Figure 8 specifies *what the device must do* in each. Figure 2 has three panels (vN baseline + the two responses) and no figures-of-merit table; Figure 8 has two panels (one per device mode) plus the figures-of-merit inset. Figure 2 shows information flow with arrows; Figure 8 shows static device-mode contrasts. The crossbar appears in both but plays different rhetorical roles — in Figure 2 as a system that bypasses the bus, in Figure 8 as a programmable-weight cell whose retention/endurance/linearity are the metrics of interest.
- **One-sentence message.** The in-memory and event-driven paradigms attack the von Neumann bottleneck from two different directions — the first by collapsing the storage/compute boundary in space, the second by replacing clocked dense data movement with sparse temporal events flowing through a dynamical substrate whose own physics performs the computation.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Two architectural responses to the von Neumann bottleneck. (a) In the classical stored-program architecture (recapitulated from \cref{fig:vonneumann_bottleneck}), every operation traverses a CPU–memory bus that fixes the bandwidth and energy ceilings of the system. (b) In the in-memory paradigm, computation is performed inside the memory array: a resistive crossbar with programmed conductances executes an analogue matrix–vector multiplication by Ohm's and Kirchhoff's laws, so the dot product no longer crosses the CPU–memory boundary. (c) In the event-driven paradigm, dense clocked data are replaced by a sparse stream of asynchronous events that drive a dynamical substrate whose intrinsic relaxation is itself the computation; only a linear readout layer downstream is trained. The figure is intended as an architectural roadmap for \cref{subsec:paradigms} and is complementary to \cref{fig:static_vs_fading}, which addresses the device-level requirements that each paradigm places on a single memristive cell.

## Figure 3 — Neuron anatomy and the action-potential waveform

- **Proposed label.** `fig:neuron_action_potential`
- **Section placement.** §1.2, `subsec:neural_signal`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the second paragraph of `subsec:neural_signal` (the paragraph that introduces the action potential, the \ce{Na+}/\ce{K+} channel sequence, the all-or-nothing character, and the explicit callback to event-driven computing in `subsec:paradigms`). The figure earns its place there because both of its panels' contents have just been described in prose: neuron anatomy in the preceding paragraph and the action-potential waveform in the paragraph it follows. It also lands before the synapse-level discussion picks up in the next paragraph and before \cref{fig:synapse_plasticity}, so the chapter's biology coverage progresses cleanly from neuron level to synapse level.
- **Figure type.** Two-panel didactic schematic. Panel (a): a labelled neuron cartoon — dendrites collecting inputs, soma, axon hillock as the firing threshold region, axon (myelinated segments optional), and axon terminals contacting a downstream cell; small arrows indicate dendritic input integration, threshold crossing at the hillock, and unidirectional propagation along the axon to the terminals. Panel (b): the action-potential waveform on a membrane-potential vs. time axis, showing resting potential, threshold, the \ce{Na+}-driven depolarisation, the peak, the \ce{K+}-driven repolarisation, the after-hyperpolarisation, and the return to rest, with the threshold and the stereotyped all-or-nothing character marked. The two panels are connected visually by indicating that the waveform of (b) is what travels along the axon of (a).
- **Distinction from Figure 4 (`fig:synapse_plasticity`).** Figure 3 is at the *cellular* level — a whole neuron and the electrical event that travels along its axon. Figure 4 is at the *sub-cellular synapse* level — the presynaptic terminal, the cleft, the postsynaptic membrane, and the plasticity-timescale ladder. Figure 3 provides the orientation that lets the reader parse Figure 4's zoom-in: the axon terminal in Figure 4 (a) is the structure at the right-hand end of Figure 3 (a), and the calcium-triggered release event in Figure 4 (a) is initiated by the action potential of Figure 3 (b) arriving at that terminal. The two figures are sequential zooms, not parallel views.
- **One-sentence message.** A neuron is a polarised cell that integrates dendritic inputs at the soma, fires a stereotyped all-or-nothing action potential at the axon hillock when the membrane potential crosses a threshold, and propagates that waveform to its synaptic terminals — a single physical sequence that anchors every later use of "spike", "spike train", "threshold", and "event-driven" in the chapter.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Anatomy of a neuron and the action-potential waveform. (a) A neuron collects synaptic inputs through its dendrites, integrates them at the soma, and fires an outgoing electrical event when the membrane potential at the axon hillock crosses a threshold; the resulting waveform propagates unidirectionally along the axon to the synaptic terminals, where it triggers chemical transmission onto downstream cells (the zoom shown in \cref{fig:synapse_plasticity}). (b) The action potential is a stereotyped millisecond-scale depolarisation produced by the opening of voltage-gated \ce{Na+} channels, followed by a \ce{K+}-mediated repolarisation and a brief after-hyperpolarisation before the return to the resting potential; its all-or-nothing character means that information about the input is carried by the timing and rate of these events rather than by their amplitude, which is the cellular basis of the event-driven view of neural computation introduced in \cref{subsec:paradigms}.

## Figure 4 — The chemical synapse and the plasticity timescales

- **Proposed label.** `fig:synapse_plasticity`
- **Section placement.** §1.2. The figure opens at the end of `subsec:neural_signal` and is referenced again in `subsec:plasticity`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that completes the description of neurotransmitter release and postsynaptic current generation in `subsec:neural_signal`. Placing the figure there lets the subsequent plasticity subsection refer back to an image the reader has already parsed.
- **Figure type.** Two-panel schematic / taxonomy. Left panel: anatomical cartoon of a chemical synapse (presynaptic terminal with vesicles, synaptic cleft, postsynaptic membrane with receptors, Ca²⁺-triggered release). Right panel: a timescale ladder summarising short-term plasticity (ms), STDP window (tens of ms), and LTP/LTD (minutes to hours), labelled by mechanism rather than by numerical value.
- **One-sentence message.** A biological synapse is a single physical element that supports a hierarchy of plasticity regimes spanning six orders of magnitude in time, and this hierarchy is the engineering target against which candidate synaptic hardware is judged.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Anatomy and plasticity regimes of the chemical synapse. (a) A presynaptic terminal releases neurotransmitter vesicles into the synaptic cleft in response to a calcium-triggered action potential, and postsynaptic receptors convert the released signal into an excitatory postsynaptic current. (b) The same synapse supports a graded hierarchy of plasticity regimes — short-term facilitation and depression on millisecond timescales, spike-timing-dependent plasticity on tens of milliseconds, and long-term potentiation and depression on timescales from minutes to hours — each associated with distinct microscopic mechanisms. This hierarchy, spanning several orders of magnitude in time in a single physical element, is the engineering target against which candidate synaptic hardware is judged in the remainder of the chapter.

## Figure 5 — Energy and density of synaptic-equivalent operations across biology, digital CMOS, and memristive devices

- **Proposed label.** `fig:synaptic_energy_density`
- **Section placement.** §1.2, `subsec:why_hw`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the opening paragraph of `subsec:why_hw` that quotes \(\sim 10^{14}\)–\(10^{15}\) synapses operating at \(\sim 20\,\mathrm{W}\) in biology and contrasts that envelope with the per-operation cost of a von-Neumann CMOS implementation. The figure earns its place there because the prose has just committed to the comparison numerically; the chart fixes the comparison visually before §1.3 introduces the device alternative. It also breaks up the long stretch of unillustrated text between `fig:synapse_plasticity` (end of §1.2.2) and `fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint` (in §1.3.1), which currently spans the entirety of §1.2.3 and the §1.3 / §1.3.1 introductions.
- **Figure type.** Single-panel log–log scatter / bubble plot. Axes: energy per synaptic-equivalent event (x) versus areal synaptic density (y). Marker classes:
  - **Biology** anchor: cortical synaptic region placed from the per-event energy estimate of Attwell & Laughlin and the cortical synaptic-density estimate of Herculano-Houzel, drawn as a labelled bounded region rather than a point to acknowledge the spread.
  - **Digital CMOS neuromorphic** envelope: representative ranges drawn from the published large-scale platforms cited via Indiveri & Liu (e.g., TrueNorth-class, Loihi-class, SpiNNaker-class order-of-magnitude regions), with the per-event energy dominated by SRAM-cell access plus arithmetic and the density limited by the cell + multiplier footprint.
  - **Inorganic memristive** families: metal-oxide filamentary, PCM, and STT-MTJ, drawn from the same review sources as the inorganic comparison matrix (Sun 2019; Ielmini & Wong 2018; Kuzum 2013) so that the two figures stay quantitatively consistent.
  - **Organic / soft-ionic target band**: forward-referenced to §1.5–§1.6, drawn as a target arrow / shaded band rather than as a published cluster — this is the design space the thesis proposes to occupy, not a survey of existing demonstrations.
- A diagonal **iso-power reference line** corresponding to the \(\sim 20\,\mathrm{W}\) brain-scale envelope (energy per event × density × characteristic event rate) is overlaid to anchor the plot to the power budget quoted in the prose.
- All numerical envelopes are conservative orders of magnitude drawn from the indicated reviews; individual devices may sit outside the bands shown. No invented numbers.
- **Distinction from Figure 1 (`fig:vonneumann_bottleneck`) and from the inorganic comparison matrix (now Figure 9).** Figure 1 expresses the *system-level* energy asymmetry between arithmetic and data movement, qualitatively, with no density axis; this figure expresses the *element-level* energy of one synaptic-equivalent event and pairs it with a density axis. The inorganic comparison matrix compares memristor families on qualitative axes (retention, tunability, substrate compatibility, reservoir-readiness); this figure compares the same families plus biology and digital CMOS on two quantitative physical axes. The three figures answer different questions, and the chapter's energy-and-density argument benefits from carrying all three.
- **One-sentence message.** Biological synapses, digital CMOS neuromorphic implementations, and present-day inorganic memristive families occupy distinct, largely non-overlapping regions of the energy-per-event / density plane, and the soft-ionic two-terminal route developed in the rest of the chapter is the only one positioned to approach the biological region on both axes simultaneously.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Approximate energy and density envelopes of synaptic-equivalent operations across implementation regimes. Each cluster represents a class of physical realisation: biological cortical synapses, digital CMOS neuromorphic implementations in which a synapse occupies an SRAM cell plus an arithmetic unit, the inorganic memristor families surveyed in §\ref{sec:inorganic_memristors}, and the soft-ionic composite target region forward-referenced to §\ref{sec:polymer_electrolyte_composites}. The diagonal reference line marks the brain-scale iso-power envelope (\(\sim 20\,\mathrm{W}\) for \(\sim 10^{14}\)–\(10^{15}\) synapses) introduced at the start of §\ref{subsec:why_hw}. Numerical bounds are conservative orders of magnitude drawn from the reviews cited in the surrounding text (Attwell & Laughlin 2001; Herculano-Houzel 2009; Kuzum 2013; Indiveri & Liu 2015; Ielmini & Wong 2018; Sun 2019); individual devices may sit outside the envelopes shown. The figure complements \cref{fig:vonneumann_bottleneck}, which makes the same energy-asymmetry argument at the system level, and \cref{fig:inorganic_comparison_matrix}, which compares the inorganic families on qualitative rather than quantitative axes.

## Figure 6 — The memristor as the fourth circuit element and its pinched-hysteresis fingerprint

- **Proposed label.** `fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint`
- **Section placement.** §1.3. The figure is positioned at the end of `subsec:tio2_memristor`, once the TiO₂ demonstration has been discussed.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that identifies oxygen-vacancy drift in TiO₂ as the microscopic origin of the measured pinched hysteresis. At that point the reader has been given the Chua quadrant argument and a concrete physical realisation, so the figure ties the symbolic and experimental views together.
- **Figure type.** Two-panel didactic schematic. Left panel: the constitutive-relations diagram of the four fundamental circuit elements — resistor (v–i), capacitor (v–q), inductor (i–φ), memristor (q–φ) — with the fourth element highlighted. Right panel: an idealised pinched-hysteresis current–voltage loop at two driving frequencies, showing the characteristic narrowing of the loop with increasing frequency.
- **One-sentence message.** The memristor is defined by a charge–flux constitutive relation that the other three passive elements do not express, and its macroscopic fingerprint is a frequency-dependent pinched hysteresis in the current–voltage plane.
- **Caption draft.**
  > The memristor as a constitutive element and its experimental fingerprint. (a) The four fundamental two-terminal passive circuit elements, arranged by the pair of constitutive variables they relate: the resistor (v–i), the capacitor (v–q), the inductor (i–φ), and the memristor (q–φ), which completes the quadrant. (b) A memristive device driven by a periodic voltage exhibits a pinched current–voltage hysteresis loop that narrows with increasing driving frequency and that passes through the origin at each zero-crossing; this loop is the unique experimental signature of memristive operation and is observed in all of the device families surveyed in the remainder of the chapter.

## Figure 7 — Mechanism gallery for memristive device families

- **Proposed label.** `fig:mechanism_gallery`
- **Section placement.** §1.3, end of `subsec:memristor_classes`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the closing paragraph of `subsec:memristor_classes` (the second ionic-family paragraph that ends by linking volatility to the chemistry of the host matrix). The five preceding paragraphs each describe one mechanistic family in dense prose; the figure consolidates that enumeration into a side-by-side visual taxonomy before the chapter pivots to the figures-of-merit framework in the following subsection.
- **Figure type.** Five-panel device gallery. Each panel pairs (i) a schematic vertical cross-section of a representative two-terminal stack with (ii) an idealised current–voltage trace under a triangular voltage sweep, drawn on a common axis convention so the panels can be read across.
  - Panel (a), filamentary: side-by-side CBRAM (active Ag or Cu top electrode, solid electrolyte, inert bottom electrode, metal filament drawn at the set state) and VCM (inert top electrode, transition-metal oxide, inert bottom electrode, oxygen-vacancy filament) sub-stacks. Representative I–V: abrupt set into a compliance-limited LRS branch followed by an abrupt reset.
  - Panel (b), interface / non-filamentary: a metal–semiconductor or mixed-ionic–electronic stack with the mobile-ion distribution drawn as a spatially extended profile rather than a localised filament, and a small Schottky-barrier band-bending inset. Representative I–V: smooth, gradual, area-scaling hysteresis without abrupt set or reset.
  - Panel (c), phase-change: a mushroom-type chalcogenide cell (\ce{Ge2Sb2Te5} active region, bottom heater, top electrode) with the amorphous and crystalline regions indicated. Representative I–V: a sharp threshold-switching event preceding the crystalline branch, with reset to the amorphous state by a melt–quench pulse.
  - Panel (d), spintronic: a magnetic-tunnel-junction stack (free ferromagnetic layer, tunnel barrier, pinned ferromagnetic layer with a fixed-orientation arrow) with parallel and antiparallel magnetisation states indicated. Representative I–V: two nearly-linear branches separated by a small ON/OFF ratio of order two.
  - Panel (e), ionic: a polymer-electrolyte composite stack (conjugated polymer network, oxygen-rich host, coordinated alkali cations, dissociated salt) between an inert bottom electrode and an active or inert top electrode, with arrows indicating field-driven ionic redistribution under bias and a relaxation arrow indicating spontaneous return toward equilibrium when the bias is removed. Representative I–V: continuous, sweep-rate-dependent hysteresis without abrupt transitions. The panel is marked as the family developed in the remainder of the chapter.
- **Distinction from Figure 6 (`fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint`), Figure 9 (`fig:inorganic_comparison_matrix`), and Figure 14 (`fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder`).** Figure 6 is the *theoretical* fingerprint: a single canonical pinched-hysteresis loop, common to all memristive devices regardless of mechanism. The mechanism gallery is its *physical* counterpart: for each family it pairs the device structure with the qualitative I–V shape that the structure produces, so the reader can begin to read off mechanism from a measured trace. Figure 9 compares the inorganic families (plus the soft-ionic target) along qualitative *design* axes (retention, tunability, substrate compatibility, reservoir-readiness); the mechanism gallery compares the same set along *structural* and *electrical-signature* axes instead. Figure 14 develops the thesis-specific composite stack together with a cation/composition timescale ladder; the ionic panel of the mechanism gallery is generic and is drawn at the same level of detail as the other four panels, so the families remain visually comparable. The four figures occupy disjoint roles: theoretical fingerprint (Figure 6), structural and I–V taxonomy across families (this figure), design-axis comparison (Figure 9), and thesis-specific device with chemical knobs (Figure 14).
- **One-sentence message.** Memristive devices are united phenomenologically by pinched-hysteresis I–V loops but differ markedly in physical structure and in the qualitative shape of those loops, and the five families surveyed in `subsec:memristor_classes` can be told apart at first sight from a labelled cross-section paired with a representative I–V trace.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Mechanism gallery for the five memristive device families introduced in \cref{subsec:memristor_classes}. Each panel pairs a schematic cross-section of a representative two-terminal stack with an idealised current–voltage trace under a triangular voltage sweep. (a) Filamentary devices, in their conductive-bridge (active-electrode metal filament) and valence-change (oxygen-vacancy filament) variants, switch abruptly between a high-resistance and a compliance-limited low-resistance branch. (b) Interface or non-filamentary devices switch through a spatially distributed change in the active layer, producing smooth, gradual, area-scaling hysteresis without an abrupt set or reset. (c) Phase-change devices toggle between amorphous and crystalline states of a chalcogenide alloy through Joule-heating melt–quench and anneal pulses, with a sharp threshold-switching event preceding the crystalline branch. (d) Spintronic magnetic tunnel junctions switch the relative orientation of two ferromagnetic layers, producing two nearly-linear branches separated by a small (order of two) ON/OFF ratio. (e) Ionic devices, in their polymer-electrolyte composite form most relevant to this thesis, redistribute mobile ions across the active layer under bias and relax back toward equilibrium when the bias is removed, producing continuous, sweep-rate-dependent hysteresis without abrupt transitions. The figure is intended to make the five families visually distinguishable before the chapter proceeds to the figures-of-merit framework of \cref{subsec:memristor_params}; it complements the canonical pinched-hysteresis fingerprint of \cref{fig:memristor_theory_fingerprint}, which is shared by all five families regardless of mechanism.

## Figure 8 — Static programmable weights versus volatile fading-memory elements

- **Proposed label.** `fig:static_vs_fading`
- **Section placement.** §1.3. End of `subsec:volatile_paradigms`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that states explicitly that retention time is a design variable, not a figure of merit to be maximised, and that device-to-device heterogeneity can be a resource in a reservoir. The figure earns its place by making the two complementary computational modes visually comparable in a single frame.
- **Figure type.** Two-panel conceptual schematic. Left panel: a trained crossbar array performing a matrix–vector multiplication, with each cell holding a programmed conductance (static weight). Right panel: a reservoir of volatile nodes driven by a time-varying input, each with a different decay time constant, followed by a trained linear readout layer. A small inset table lists the figures of merit appropriate to each mode (retention, endurance, ON/OFF for the crossbar; memory capacity, class separability, timescale spread for the reservoir).
- **One-sentence message.** Memristive hardware supports at least two complementary computational modes — static programmable weights for trained networks, and volatile fading-memory elements for temporal processing — and each mode has its own set of relevant figures of merit.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Two complementary computational roles for memristive devices. (a) A trained crossbar holds programmed conductances as non-volatile weights and performs matrix–vector multiplication by Ohm's and Kirchhoff's laws; here retention, endurance, linearity and symmetry are the relevant figures of merit. (b) A reservoir of volatile fading-memory nodes, driven by a time-varying input, projects temporal signals into a high-dimensional dynamical state; only a linear readout layer is trained, and the relevant figures of merit become memory capacity, class separability, and the spread of available time constants. The thesis treats these two modes as complementary targets for memristive hardware rather than as a hierarchy, and develops the second of them as the primary application of the polymer-electrolyte composite family.

## Figure 10 — Molecular-mechanism gallery for organic memristors

- **Proposed label.** `fig:organic_mechanism_gallery`
- **Section placement.** §1.5, end of `subsec:organic_mechanisms`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the closing paragraph of `subsec:organic_mechanisms` that names the fifth (organic filamentary) family, and immediately before `tab:organic_mechanisms`. The bridging sentence is updated to reference both this figure and the table together, so the reader sees a visual gallery and a qualitative comparison matrix side by side, each playing a complementary role.
- **Figure type.** Single-row gallery of five small molecular-level cartoons drawn at the level of the active-layer chemistry only. No device stacks, no electrodes, no I–V traces. Each panel shows the state variable of its family in two states (or as a redistribution under bias).
  - Panel (a), charge trapping: a disordered polymer region with a sparse population of localised trap sites (deep wells / shallow wells / interphase boundaries), shown filled vs. empty under bias.
  - Panel (b), redox switching: a redox-active moiety (small molecule or pendant group) shown in its oxidised and reduced states, with the local electronic structure schematically annotated.
  - Panel (c), conformational switching: a molecular unit toggling between two well-defined geometric or isomeric states (e.g., cis/trans, open/closed), with the change in conjugation or transport pathway indicated.
  - Panel (d), mixed ionic–electronic redistribution: a population of mobile cations within an electronically conducting polymer matrix, displaced and accumulated near one interface under bias and relaxing back toward equilibrium when the bias is removed. Drawn at the chemistry level (no electrode stack), with an arrow showing field-driven motion and a counter-arrow indicating relaxation. Marked as the thesis-relevant family.
  - Panel (e), organic filamentary conduction: metallic cations (\ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+}) migrating through the organic matrix and forming a conductive bridge, drawn without the active electrode.
- **Distinction from Figure 7 (`fig:mechanism_gallery`) and Figure 14 (`fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder`).** Figure 7 is the *stack-level* taxonomy across all five general memristor families (filamentary, interface, PCM, MTJ, ionic); it pairs each family with a full device cross-section and a representative I–V trace. This figure operates one level deeper *for the organic subset only*: it strips away electrodes, stacks, and I–V traces, and isolates the active-layer chemistry, so the reader can read off the *state variable itself* (trap, redox moiety, conformer, ion cloud, filament). It therefore does not duplicate Figure 7's ionic panel, which is presented at the stack level alongside the other four general families. Figure 14 develops the thesis-specific composite stack and is not a comparative figure; this gallery places that single stack in its mechanistic context within the organic landscape.
- **One-sentence message.** The five organic memristive families differ at the level of the active-layer chemistry — the state variable is a different physical or chemical degree of freedom in each — and the mixed ionic–electronic family is the only one whose state variable is a continuously gradable, chemically tunable ion distribution.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Molecular-level cartoons of the five mechanism families behind organic memristive behaviour, drawn at the level of the active-layer chemistry only and without device-stack or current–voltage detail. (a) Charge trapping: localised trap sites associated with defects, embedded nanoparticles, or interphase boundaries in a disordered polymer matrix, filled and emptied under bias. (b) Redox switching: a redox-active moiety alternating between its oxidised and reduced states, with the local electronic structure changing accordingly. (c) Conformational switching: a molecular unit toggling between two well-defined geometric or isomeric states with different conjugation or transport pathways. (d) Mixed ionic–electronic redistribution: a population of mobile cations within an electronically conducting polymer matrix, displaced and accumulated near an interface under bias and relaxing back toward equilibrium when the bias is removed. (e) Organic filamentary conduction: metallic cations (typically \ce{Ag+} or \ce{Cu+}) from an active electrode migrating through the organic matrix to form a conductive bridge. The figure is intended as a chemical complement to the stack-level device taxonomy of \cref{fig:mechanism_gallery} and to the qualitative comparison of \cref{tab:organic_mechanisms}.

## Figure 12 — Ion-conducting polymer chemistry: PEO coordination, transport modes, and the cation-ordering hypothesis

- **Proposed label.** `fig:polymer_electrolyte_chemistry`
- **Section placement.** §1.6, `subsec:ion_conducting_polymers`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that develops the hard–soft acid–base argument for relative cation–oxygen binding strength along the \ce{Li+} > \ce{Na+} > \ce{K+} series and presents it as a hypothesis for fading-memory ordering. At that point the reader has just been told that (i) the host oxygens are hard Lewis bases coordinating alkali cations, (ii) ion transport in the host combines vehicular, hopping, and segmental-motion-assisted contributions, and (iii) cation residence time at a coordination site could plausibly follow an HSAB ordering — three threads whose mutual relationship is easier to absorb visually than in prose. Chapter 3 later tests this hypothesis and finds no robust host/anion-independent ordering. Placing the figure immediately before `subsec:lec_precedent` lets the LEC discussion proceed without having to reload the chemistry argument from the previous subsection.
- **Figure type.** Three-panel molecular-level schematic, drawn entirely at the active-layer chemistry level (no electrodes, no stack, no I–V trace).
  - Panel (a), coordination: a short PEO segment with its $-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{CH}_2\!-\!\mathrm{O}\!-$ repeats, wrapped around a coordinated alkali cation such that four to six ether oxygens donate lone pairs into the cation's coordination sphere; a triflate (or TFSI) anion is drawn nearby and labelled as charge-compensating but only weakly coordinated. A small inset shows the equivalent oxygen-rich coordination environment in a Hybrane-type hyperbranched polyester-amide side group, making explicit that the two hosts of the thesis share the same coordination chemistry.
  - Panel (b), transport modes: three small adjacent cartoons of the same cation moving through the host by (i) vehicular translation, with the cation and its solvation shell of ether oxygens displacing together along the chain; (ii) site-to-site hopping, with the cation transferring between coordination pockets through local oxygen-cage rearrangement; (iii) segmental-motion-assisted diffusion, with a short polymer segment reorganising around the cation to create and destroy coordination sites. Each cartoon is annotated with the host property it depends on (chain mobility, oxygen density, distance to the glass transition).
  - Panel (c), HSAB ladder: the three alkali cations \ce{Li+}, \ce{Na+}, \ce{K+} drawn to relative ionic-radius scale, aligned along an axis labelled "cation–oxygen coordination strength" decreasing from left to right; a parallel axis labelled "hypothesised fading-memory time constant" runs in the same direction, with the qualitative expectation $\tau_{\ce{Li+}} > \tau_{\ce{Na+}} > \tau_{\ce{K+}}$ marked as a hypothesis rather than a result. The panel deliberately anticipates the ordering tested in Chapter 3, while the final thesis text must state that this ordering is not robust across host/anion chemistry in the available archive.
- **Distinction from existing figures.** \cref{fig:mechanism_gallery} (Figure 7) and \cref{fig:organic_mechanism_gallery} (Figure 10) include an ionic / mixed-ionic–electronic panel, but at the level of *what the state variable is* across families; this figure operates one level deeper for the polymer-electrolyte case only, isolating the coordination chemistry, the transport modes, and the HSAB ranking that motivates a testable cation hypothesis. \cref{fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder} (Figure 14) shows the device stack and a qualitative composition-led timescale ladder at the device level. The three figures form a coherent sequence: chemistry → mechanism family → device.
- **One-sentence message.** Ion transport in the polyether-hosted polymer-electrolyte composite is framed by three coupled ingredients — multidentate cation coordination by ether oxygens, vehicular / hopping / segmental-motion-assisted transport of the coordinated cation, and a plausible HSAB-derived cation-residence hierarchy — and these ingredients motivate, but do not prove, the cation-ordering hypothesis tested and rejected as a robust rule in Chapter 3.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Molecular-level chemistry of ion transport in the polyether-hosted polymer-electrolyte composite. (a) A short poly(ethylene oxide) segment coordinates an alkali cation through four to six ether oxygens, with the triflate anion held nearby as a weakly coordinated charge-compensating counter-ion; the same coordination chemistry applies to the hyperbranched polyester-amide hosts used in \cref{ch:poc}. (b) Long-range ionic transport in this host proceeds by three coupled contributions: vehicular translation of the cation together with its solvation shell, site-to-site hopping between coordination pockets through local oxygen-cage rearrangement, and segmental-motion-assisted diffusion in which short polymer segments reorganise to create and destroy coordination sites. (c) Within a common polyether host, hard–soft acid–base reasoning motivates the hypothesis that stronger cation–oxygen coordination could lengthen residence time and fading memory along the \ce{Li+} > \ce{Na+} > \ce{K+} series. Chapter 3 treats this as a quantitative test, not as a guaranteed design rule.

## Figure 13 — Light-emitting electrochemical cell operating models and their memristive reuse

- **Proposed label.** `fig:lec_models`
- **Section placement.** §1.6, `subsec:lec_precedent`.
- **Position relative to prose.** After the paragraph that names the electrochemical-doping model and the electrodynamic model of LEC operation, cites their respective experimental supports, and notes that the two descriptions were later reconciled as different injection regimes of a common ion-redistribution mechanism. The figure earns its place because the two models share the same underlying ion redistribution and disagree on which *consequence* of that redistribution dominates the electrical response — a contrast that prose alone communicates only with difficulty and that a side-by-side band-diagram pair makes immediate. It also lands the bias-on / bias-off distinction that underwrites the memristive reuse argument of the next subsection.
- **Figure type.** Four-panel comparative schematic, all panels sharing a common position axis from cathode (left) to anode (right) so the four views can be read across.
  - Panel (a), shared substrate: a vertical LEC sandwich with a conjugated light-emitting polymer blended with an ion-conducting polymer host and a dissociated salt between two electrodes. Arrows indicate cation drift toward the cathode and anion drift toward the anode under sustained bias; doped regions form at the contacts.
  - Panel (b), electrochemical-doping model: an energy-band diagram on the same position axis, showing a spatially wide n-doped region at the cathode, a wide p-doped region at the anode, and a narrow intrinsic recombination zone in the bulk; emission is localised at the i-region. The annotation makes clear that the dominant effect of the ionic redistribution is *bulk doping of the conjugated polymer*, after the Pei / Smith picture.
  - Panel (c), electrodynamic model: an energy-band diagram on the same position axis, showing two thin ion-accumulation layers at the metal/polymer interfaces, sharp Schottky-barrier lowering at each contact, and an essentially flat-band bulk in between. The annotation makes clear that the dominant effect of the same redistribution is *injection-barrier lowering at the contacts*, after the deMello picture.
  - Panel (d), unification and memristive reuse: the same active layer, now with the driving bias removed; the residual ionic distribution left behind by the previous bias is highlighted as the *stored internal state*. A short side note records that (b) and (c) are limiting cases of the same redistribution under different injection regimes (van Reenen \emph{et al.}~\cite{vanReenen2010}), and that the memristive composite of \cref{fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder} reuses the bias-off configuration as memory rather than sustaining the bias-on configuration to produce light.
- **Distinction from existing figures.** \cref{fig:organic_mechanism_gallery} (Figure 10) places mixed ionic–electronic redistribution within the broader organic-mechanism landscape at the chemistry level; this figure zooms further in on a single experimentally consolidated platform (the LEC) and contrasts two specific microscopic interpretations of the same redistribution. \cref{fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder} (Figure 14) shows the thesis device and its chemical knobs; this figure shows the precedent device whose operating physics is being repurposed, and lands the bias-on / bias-off distinction that underwrites that reuse. Panels (b) and (c) are deliberately drawn on the same band-diagram axis so the bulk-vs-interface contrast is immediate.
- **One-sentence message.** The electrochemical-doping and electrodynamic models of LEC operation are not rival mechanisms but rival readings of the *same* ion redistribution, and the memristive composite of this thesis reuses that redistribution by reading the residual ionic distribution left behind once the bias is removed, rather than the dynamic p-i-n profile sustained while the bias is on.
- **Caption draft.**
  > Light-emitting electrochemical cell operating models and their memristive reuse, drawn on a common cathode–anode position axis. (a) Shared substrate: under sustained bias, cations drift toward the cathode and anions toward the anode, forming doped regions at the two contacts. (b) Electrochemical-doping model: the dominant effect of the ionic redistribution is *bulk doping* of the conjugated polymer, producing a spatially wide p-i-n profile with emission localised at a narrow intrinsic region (after Pei \emph{et al.}~\cite{Pei1995} and Smith \emph{et al.}~\cite{Smith1997}). (c) Electrodynamic model: the dominant effect of the same redistribution is *injection-barrier lowering* at the metal/polymer interfaces, producing thin ion-accumulation layers at the contacts and an essentially flat-band bulk (after deMello~\cite{deMello1998, deMello2002}). The two pictures were later reconciled by van Reenen \emph{et al.}~\cite{vanReenen2010} as limiting cases of the same redistribution under different injection regimes. (d) Memristive reuse: with the bias removed, the residual ionic distribution left behind by the previous bias is the *stored internal state* of the device; the polymer-electrolyte composite of \cref{fig:composite_architecture_timescale_ladder} reads this bias-off configuration as memory, rather than sustaining the bias-on p-i-n profile to produce light.
